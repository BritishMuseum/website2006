Reading Room

At the heart of the Great Court is the magnificent Reading Room, now restored to its 1857 glory and home to a modern information centre open to all Museum visitors. The Walter and Leonore Annenberg Centre combines the very best in modern technology with more traditional sources of information retrieval.

When completed in 1857, it was immediately hailed as one of the great sights of London. Despite being amongst the most famous rooms in the world, it has only been generally accessible to those with a Reader's ticket. With its opening to the public on December 7, 2000, it began a new chapter in its distinguished history and for the first time, is available to all Museum visitors. Its magnificent interior has been carefully restored, including the repair of the papier mâché interior of the dome and reinstatement of the 1857 azure-blue, cream and gold decorative scheme devised by Sydney Smirke.

It houses the Paul Hamlyn Library, a new public reference resource library of some 25,000 books, catalogues and other printed material focusing on the world cultures represented in The British Museum.

COMPASS, the Museum's multimedia object database, is free to use and available to all Museum visitors on touchscreens in the Reading Room. The system provides information on around 5,000 objects from the Museum's collections. For each object, you will find a short article written by a specialist and high quality images you can enlarge and study in detail. COMPASS is designed for easy browsing, with links between objects, background information, and suggestions for further reading.
