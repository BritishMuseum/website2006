Pioneers of aerial archaeology
Thursday 16 November
Stevenson Lecture Theatre
With the advent of aviation in the late 19th century, archaeologists realised that ancient sites,
sometimes buried under earth or sand, could be seen more clearly from above – in some cases
sites were discernible only from the air. Charlotte Trümpler, Ruhrlandmuseum Essen, looks at
some of the ground-breaking archaeologists with particular attention to the award-winning
pioneer of aerial photography Georg Gerster.


Flying visits: The Past from Above
Thursday 23 November
Stevenson Lecture Theatre
Georg Gerster's aerial photographs provide a unique view of human endeavour around the world.
When considered alongside important objects from the British Museum's collection, these images
present us with a remarkable global tour of world cultures. Lesley Fitton and Sam Moorhead,
British Museum, introduce Georg's photographs featured in The Past from Above and
discuss the issues they raised about the permanence or fragility of some
of mankind's greatest achievements.
(Sign-interpreted)


From city skyline to monastic cell: Christian landscapes of Europe,
Africa and the Near East
Friday 8 December
The practice of the Christian faith takes place in city-centre cathedrals and in remote desert
monasteries. Rowena Loverance, British Museum, discusses The Past from Above exhibition
which includes spectacular examples of Christian churches and monasteries, whether
converted temples, such as the Parthenon at Athens, fortified churches such as
Viscri in Romania or dramatically-situated monasteries such as Debra Damo
in Ethiopia and Skellig Michael off the west coast of Ireland.

A Condor's-eye view:
South American archaeology from the Air
Friday 26 January
BP Lecture Theatre
Aerial photography in South America reveals unexpected new perspectives on famous
archaeological sites as well as less well known ones. Colin McEwan, British Museum,
explores the architectural remnants of sacred places, the evidence for human intervention
in shaping and managing the landscape and the contrast between what can be observed
from the air and the ground.

Documentaries
All start at 18.30, Stevenson Lecture Theatre
Admission free, booking required

Drowned Out
Friday 12 January
A true story of one family's inspired stand against the destruction of their land, homes and
culture by the Narmada Dam. Shot over three years, Drowned Out follows the Jalsindhi
villagers through hunger strikes, rallies, police brutality and a six-year Supreme Court case.
It stays with them as the dam fills and the river starts to rise.
Director: Franny Armstrong
UK/India, 2002, 75 mins

The Enigma of the Nazcas
Friday 26 January
In 1998, two passionate archaeologists, Giuseppe Orefici and Egla Barone, decided to
undertake an impressive excavation campaign on the huge site of Cahuachi in an attempt to
solve the mysteries of this little-known civilisation.
Director: Thierry Ragobert
France, 1999, 52 mins

Hadrian's Wall - Edge of the Empire
Friday 2 February
A fascinating journey along the 73 miles of Hadrian's Wall in the company of Eric Robson
as he unravels the mysteries of the wall and the route. The documentary utilizes on-site
filming and computer graphics to tell the story of this Roman ruin.
Director: Bob Carruthers
UK, 1999, 50 mins

Jiroft, Lost Kingdom of Aratta
Friday 9 February
In 2003 Professor Yousef Madjidzadeh unearthed traces of a 5000-year-old civilisation in
south Iran, including the walls of a city covering 100 hectares. He is convinced that it is the
mythical kingdom of Aratta, as described in the most ancient Sumerian legends.
Director: Olivier Julien
France, 2005, 52 mins

The Story of the Weeping Camel
Friday 16 February
With guest speaker John Hare, Wild Camel Protection Foundation This enchanting film
follows a family of herders in Mongolia's Gobi region who face a crisis when a mother
camel unexpectedly rejects her newborn calf.
Directors: Byambasuren Davaa & Luigi Falorni
Germany/Mongolia, 2004, 87 mins
