Changing Face: Masks from The British Museum
A Partnership UK touring exhibition from The British Museum

The Manchester Museum 16 January - 18 April 2004

Leicester City Museums (New Walk) 5 June - 29 August 2004

Hancock Museum, Newcastle, Tyne and Wear 11 September 2004 - 16 January 2005

Bolton Museum and Art GAllery 29 January 2005 - 12 March 2005

A national tour based around this exhibition is proposed by The British Museum as part of its 250th anniversary celebrations in 2003.

This exhibition is third in the series of collaborations between The British Museum and the Henry Moore Institute. It not intended to be a comprehensive survey of masks but a small selection of choice examples which will illustrate the ways in which masks and masquerading have been used in diverse cultures and diverse periods.

By selecting examples across the breadth of The British Museum's collections it will give the visitor the rare opportunity to compare a mask from Ancient Egypt directly with one from Japan or from 17th century England. The chosen examples fall into simple, opposing categories such as life and death, male and female, human and divine, ritual and theatrical, conceal and reveal which either confirm or confound our pre-conceptions about masks. The word "mask" has become a generic term. In most - but not all - cases, it means something worn by humans to cover the face during a festival or ceremony as a part of a headdress or an overall ensemble, though it has also come to mean just about any depiction of the human face. Although many masks are imaginative depictions or idealized portraits, the wax death-mask of Oliver Cromwell displayed in the exhibition, is a very exact portrait of a historical figure.

In the West, masks are often associated with disguise or protection, such as the Ribchester cavalry sports helmet which has a visor in the form of a face. Masks are also widely used as a dramatic device such as in the Japanese No theatre tradition where they were worn by a male actor to represent a specific character, whose representation remained the same for hundreds of years. The mask can also be a vehicle for transformation so that the wearer can become a demon or a deity such as the gilded Egyptian mummy mask which symbolized the deceased becoming divine and the Devil dancer mask from Sri Lanka which was used to exorcise evil spirits from the sick and inflicted. Other masks represent a form of human transformation and include those worn by the Sande society during female initiation rites in which a girl became a woman. Sande masks illustrate a very specific form of idealised female beauty and are the only example of women making and wearing masks in Africa.

Masks do not necessary follow the size or form of the human head, such as the wooden king mask from Bamum in the Cameroons which is so large to emphasise and impress upon the viewer the power and might of the ruler wearing it. Some masks were never intended to be worn and could exist as deities in their own right. The mask from Teotihuacan, for example, is carved stone and thus very heavy with no eyeholes so it was probably used as the face of a composite sculpture of a god. Another example of a mask that was not worn, by humans anyway, originates from New Guinea and figured in ceremonies associated with the cult of the Yam. However, masks are not always of the human face. In the North West Coast of America wolves, bears and owls were depicted and when worn evoked the bravery or wisdom of the wearer's ancestors.
