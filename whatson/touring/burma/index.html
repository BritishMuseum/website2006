Visions from the Golden Land: Burma and the art of lacquer
A touring exhibition from The British Museum
Venues in the US to be confirmed

This exhibition explores one of the foremost means of artistic expression in Burma - the use of lacquer. It is based on the recent gift to the British Museum of the Ralph and Ruth Isaacs Collection of Burmese Lacquer, but also includes important examples of Burmese lacquer art from the host venues (in Exeter, the magnificent Truscott Buddha image and other Buddhist sculpture and Burmese materials; in Sunderland, vessels, musical instruments and sculpture from museums in Tyne and Wear). This is an opportunity for joint activity between national and regional museums under the Partnership UK scheme and demonstrates the importance of the holdings of regional museums.

Burma's rich and dramatic culture is almost unknown in Europe. Although it occupies the largest mainland area of Southeast Asia, Burma's political isolation has ensured that its contacts with the rest of the world have been much reduced since it became independent from Britain in 1948.

Lacquer - the sap of a tree which is tapped like rubber - can be used in a wide variety of ways and traditionally in Burma almost all parts of life were affected by it. This exhibition highlights a number of the most important areas of lacquer use in Burma, as well as explaining methods of production and of decoration. Especially prominent is its use in the service of Buddhism, not only in the form of sculptures of the Buddha and manuscripts containing the Buddhist dharma and indeed chests to hold them, but also the many different types of vessel which were used for the offering of gifts to monastery and temple. Lacquer, as a heat-proofing agent which also protects against insects, was also widely used in the domestic sphere, and this area is represented in the exhibition as well. Much new research on the subject has been revealed through work preparing the exhibition and its catalogue, such as the use of lacquer vessels in diplomacy, the identification of lacquer masters and their workshops, the translation of the many inscriptions on vessels, and the documentation of contemporary workmanship (a finely decorated five-fold screen was specially commissioned for the exhibition). A short video, shot at Pagan in central Burma, demonstrates the way lacquer vessels are made and decorated.

Lacquer vessels were produced in an extraordinary array of organic and tactile shapes, in cinnabar red, blazing gold and in lustrous black. In the last two centuries perhaps the most common types were containers for carrying offerings to the monastery (hsun ok) and the cylindrical boxes (kun it) used to hold the paraphernalia for the preparation of the betel quid. This is a small leaf parcel containing tobacco, areca nut, spices, sugar and powdered lime, which is used as a digestive in Burma and other parts of Southeast Asia.

Postcards are available and a fully illustrated catalogue by Ralph Isaacs and T. Richard Blurton accompanies the exhibition; it is published by British Museum Press, priced £24.95.
