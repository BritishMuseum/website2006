Introduction

Before the advent of written history, human activity is recorded in other ways. All the evidence that we have for our prehistoric past comes from material remains, both objects and sites, that our ancestors have left behind. Although material remains offer fascinating insights into the lives of our forebears, we are unable to read monuments and objects like texts. It is the task of archaeologists to uncover and interpret this evidence, sometimes with the help of ethnographic research on more recent cultures. As new evidence and materials come to light, so our interpretation maybe modified. In this exhibition, the visitor will be able to see a range of the most important objects from our prehistoric past up to the end of the Bronze Age and will be able to engage with current thinking in the field.

The first technology

One characteristic that distinguishes us from our nearest relatives, the great apes, is our ability to make rather than just use tools. The oldest known tools made by early humans (hominids) are flaked pebble tools found on sites in the Rift Valley in East Africa, like Olduvai Gorge (1). The earliest examples, from Hadar in Ethiopia, date to about 2.5 million years ago. As humans moved out of Africa to Europe and across Asia, they took with them "handaxe" technology (2). It is an incredible fact that this versatile tool, persisted for well over a million years during the Palaeolithic or Old Stone Age. People depended on stone tools until the introduction of metals.
