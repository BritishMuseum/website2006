The Raymond and Beverly Sackler Galleries of Ancient Anatolia

This gallery illustrates the various cultures which succeeded each other in Anatolia from Neolithic times to the conquests of the Persians (536 BC) and Alexander the Great (334 BC).

Prehistoric (5500-3000 BC) objects include pottery, obsidian (volcanic glass) blades, figurines and seals.

Early Bronze Age (3000-2000 BC): Black-burnished pottery from Yortan cemetery in the west, decorated spindle whorls, stylised stone figurines, a silver cup from near Troy and a silver figure of a bull from central Anatolia.

Middle and Late Bronze Age (2000-1200 BC): The business archives of Assyrian merchants from northern Mesopotamia written in cuneiform on clay tablets in sealed clay envelopes from Kültepe in central Anatolia, seals, pottery and stone moulds for making lead figurines and jewellery. Hittite Empire cuneiform tablets, seals, pottery, a beautiful ivory figure, a small gold figurine of a Hittite god and jewellery including miniature gold and lapis lazuli figures.

Iron Age (1200-536 BC): Bronzes excavated in the temple at Toprakkale in the eastern kingdom of Urartu, including furniture fittings and attachments for huge cauldrons. Metalwork from the Caucasus, to the northeast of Urartu, and objects from Phrygia, Lydia, Lycia and Caria to the west. In Room 53 are objects and sculpture from the Neo-Hittite city of Carchemish, where Hittite culture survived.
