From Syria, there is a large collection of nearly forty funerary busts from Palmyra, mostly acquired in the 19th century.

A group of stone reliefs from the excavations of Max von Oppenheim at Tell Halaf was purchased in 1920. More excavated material was forthcoming from the excavations of Max Mallowan (1904-78) at Chagar Bazar and Tell Brak in 1935-38, and C.L. Woolley at Alalakh in the years just before and after the Second World War. From Alalakh comes the statue of King Idrimi.

Until the 1950s the Palestinian collection was relatively modest, but important additions were made in 1954 and 1958 with a neolithic plastered skull and the contents of a Middle Bronze Age tomb from the excavations of Kathleen Kenyon (1906-78) at Jericho. The collection of Palestinian material was greatly strengthened with the acquisition in 1980 of around 17,000 objects found at Lachish by the Wellcome-Marston expedition of 1932-38.

In recent years, excavations at Tell es-Sa'idiyeh in Jordan directed by the British Museum curator Jonathan Tubb from 1985 onwards, have produced much interesting material.

From the Arabian peninsula, there is an outstanding collection of ancient South Arabian stone sculpture and other material from Yemen (formerly Aden). An important collection of inscribed bronze votive plaques was presented as early as 1862, and 83 items was donated as recently as 1985.

Lastly, from the Phoenician colonies in the Mediterranean, come nearly 200 Punic and neo-Punic stelae from Carthage in Tunisia, most of them from the excavations of the Reverend Nathan Davis in the 1850s, and the rich grave-goods from a cemetery at Tharros in Sardinia.
