research          
 

The curators of the Department are all involved with research in one form or another, exploring the past cultures of the Asian continent.

Archaeological fieldwork has for many years been carried out by Robert Knox and Justin Morris in Bannu Division of the North West Frontier Province of Pakistan, as part of a collaborative project with University College London, the Pakistan Heritage Society (Peshawar), University of Sydney and Bryn Mawr College, Pennsylvania. Work in Bannu has ranged from the Palaeolithic to the Early Historic Periods (see Fieldwork).
Robert Knox is now engaged in writing up the final report of the excavations at neolithic Sheri Khan Tarakari.

Dr Michael Willis is directing a research project in and around the city of Vidisha in central India. The aim of this project is to examine ancient Vidisha through historical and human geography, religion, archaeology, epigraphy, architecture, vernacular building and other facets of material culture. He is, as well, completing a catalogue of The British Museum's holdings of North Indian temple sculpture.

Richard Blurton is currently engaged in a major collaborative research project, Tribal Transitions, in the far northeast of India, in Arunachal Pradesh, working with colleagues from the School of Oriental and African Studies, Arunachal University and NGO's. This five-year documentary study is funded through the government's Economic and Social Council. His remit is the study of mterial culture and its continuing change, and invollves art historical, architectural and historical analysis, as well as the collecting of contemporary materials for the Museum's collections, the latter funded by the Townley Group of The British Museum Friends. Also, he is the joint editor and contributor to a volume of essays on recent research in Burma, Burma. Art and Archaelogy (published by BM Press, December 2002). Future plans include an exhibition on Tibetan banner paintings, thang-kas, from a private collector, in September 2003.

Justin Morris is currently co-ordinating the online publication of an interactive gazetteer of prehistoric and protohistoric sites in northwest South Asia. As well, he is finishing his PhD thesis on Lithic Technology during the later prehistoric period in the North West Frontier Province of Pakistan. He is currently writing up his recent excavations at Lewan in Bannu Division of the NWFP.

Carol Michaelson researches Chinese jades and early Chinese material.  She is currently co-ordinating the digitization of the Department's Dunhuang and related material and has prepared a new gallery of Chinese jade, which opened in November 2002.  Currently she is writing a book on Chinese jade which will be published in 2003 and working with the British Museum's scientific research department on a project analysing ancient lapidary skills related to jade working.

Jane Portal She is collecting objects in the Korean field and in recent months has travelled to Pyongyang to collect contemporary material.

Jessica Harrison-Hall's current research focuses on the porcelain industry in 15th century Fujian Province, China. This research will lead to a new history of production and consumption of Ming ceramics in south eastern China. Following her publication catalogue of Late Yuan and Ming Ceramics, she has begun a five year project to research Chinese Qing Dynasty (1644-1911) pottery and porcelain for a comprehensive catalogue of the Museum's largely unpublished holdings. Also she has research interests in contemporary Vietnam art and is building a new collection of paintings with the help of colleagues in Vietnam.

Dr. Sheila Canby's research is currently working on 16th-century Safavid art in preparation for the exhibition 'Hunt for Paradise:  Court Arts of Safavid Iran, 1501-76' which will be shown at the Asia Society in New York and the Poldi Pezzoli Museum in Milan in 2003.  Additionally, she is preparing several papers, one on the connections between 16th-century illuminated Qur'ans in India and Iran, another on the usefulness of surveys of Islamic Art in the museum setting and one on the Peter Mundy album of
c.1620, a traveller's account and its Ottoman illustrations.

Dr. Venetia Porter is continuing her work on Islamic writing and epigraphy, and in particular is preparing a catalogue of the Museum's collection of Islamic seals, which is in its final stage (to be published in 2004). In addition she is carrying out research on the department's collections of Islamic tiles.
