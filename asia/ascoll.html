collection's history

The founding collection of Sir Hans Sloane (1660-1753) contained a small number of objects from the Asian continent including a collection of Japanese material that had been acquired from the family of the German physician Engelbert Kaempfer (1651-1716) who had brought it back from Japan. Only a very few objects were acquired from Asia between 1753 and the end of the Napoleonic wars.

The early 19th century saw the collection and donation of a number of South Asian objects such as the gilded bronze figure of Tara from Sri Lanka in 1830, the Bridge Collection of east and central Indian sculpture in 1872, and the Amaravati Collection in 1880.

Sir Augustus Wollaston Franks, Keeper of the Department of Antiquities from 1866 to 1896, ensured that the collection of Asian material continued, resulting in the establishment in 1921 of the sub-Department of Oriental Antiquities.

In 1933 the Asian collections of the Prints and Drawings sub-Department were united with the Department of Oriental Antiquities forming one of the most significant such collections of art and antiquities in the west.

In 2003 the Department of Oriental Antiquities and the Department of Japanase Antiquities merged to become the Department of Asia.

The department aims to develop the chronological and geographical coverage of the collections, adding to the already strong collection of Indian sculpture, Islamic pottery, Chinese ceramics and paintings and Japanese paintings and swords. In the past twenty years activity has grown from the Department's traditional areas to include archaeological work in Pakistan and India, and a focus on collecting in the modern field in all parts of Asia.

Key areas of the collections include:

  • The most comprehensive collection of sculpture from the Indian subcontinent in the world, including the celebrated Buddhist limestone reliefs from Amaravati.
  • An outstanding collection of Chinese antiquities, paintings, and porcelain, lacquer, bronze, jade, and other applied arts including the collection of Buddhist paintings from Dunhuang in Central Asia and the "Admonitions scroll" by Gu Kaizhi.

  • The most comprehensive collection of Japanese pre-20th century decorative arts in the western world.

  • A broad range of Islamic pottery, paintings, tiles, metalwork, glass, seals, and inscriptions.
