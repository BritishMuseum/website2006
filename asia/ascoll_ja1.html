The Importance of the Collections
For the pre-20th century, the decorative arts are among the finest in the Western world, while for paintings and prints the collection is pre-eminent within Europe, both for scope and quality.The collection of Japanese paintings (about 4,500) includes many fine pieces from the Kamakura period and later, while the collection of Ukiyo-e numbers about 7,000 works by all important artists.

From the 1950s a concerted effort to broaden the collection has brought a number of significant early antiquities including Kamakura period Buddhist sculpture and Momoyama period painted screens into the collection. Archaeological material includes a significant group of 366 objects from the Kofun period collected by William Gowland (1842-1927), and the collection of swords, largely a result of the R.W. Lloyd bequest, contains a number of significant blades of exceptionally high quality. Another important recent gift was the private collection of rare ritual masks donated by Mr Nobutaka Oka in 1999.

The pre-20th century ceramics collection includes many fine pieces of Tea Ceremony wares, and fine export porcelain of the 17th century and later. A recent addition in the form of a gift of 500 pieces of Edo period domestic style porcelain from Mr Shibata Akihiko makes the collection the most comprehensive outside Japan. The collection of 20th century prints has been brought from almost nothing to comprehensive holdings since the early 1980s and printed illustrated books amassed over the past two decades are now the most important holdings in Europe; the collection of contemporary objects, especially ceramics, continues to grow. In particular, after the major exhibition of specially commissioned works by 62 members of the Saga Ceramic Art Association in 2000, the potters generously donated one item each to the British Museum.

How the Collections were formed
From the early 17th century porcelain and lacquerware, predominantly furniture, was exported to Europe and Asia through the Dutch East India Company. The finer quality ware was acquired for country houses and palaces for both decorative and utilitarian functions. Many houses open to the public under the auspices of The National Trust and other organizations will have some Japanese material, often porcelain vases and dishes decorating mantelpieces over fireplaces. Some, like the Burghley House collection, are more extensive and provide well-documented information in the form of original inventories.

The British Museum's original gift of the Sir Hans Sloane collection contained Japanese material which had been acquired from the family of the German physician Engelbert Kaempfer (1651-1716) who had brought it back from Japan. Exhibitions in Vienna, Paris, and in London in 1862, were followed by a series of international expositions in Europe and America throughout most of the Meiji era(1868 -1912). The arts and crafts of Japan were sold at auctions, and both specialist and non-specialist shops. Examples include Liberty's of Regent Street, Mr. Kato of New Oxford Street, and later Mr. Yamanaka of Bond Street who had a large staff of Japanese art salesmen. Great collections were formed by private individuals to be later given, sold or bequeathed to Museums. The collections contain much of that material which became obsolete during the Meiji era (1868-1912) with the Westernization of Japan: arms and armour, personal accessories, lacquer, ceramics, Buddhist material and "unusual" items of the Edo period.

The material
After the Meiji Restoration in 1868, many of the provincial lords, or daimyo , sold their possessions: collections of paintings, arms, swords and armour, personal accessories, lacquer, tea ceremony wares, ceramics, Noh theatre masks and "unusual" items of the Edo period all became available. Our collections contain many such items which came to be considered obsolete at this time with the Westernization of Japan. Inro, netsuke , and other paraphernalia traditionally carried suspended from the belt fell out of use following the adoption of Western dress (men's suits had pockets, and women took to carrying handbags). Bronze mirrors were replaced by glass. Nib and ink pens gradually replaced the brush and rubbed ink-stone. After the separation of Shinto and Buddhism in the Meiji era, much religious material including Buddhist altar equipment, sculpture, and paintings (became available) lost their significance and became available.
