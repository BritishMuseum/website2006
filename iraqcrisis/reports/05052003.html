Report of the Interpol Meeting 'International Conference on Cultural Stolen Property in Iraq'
Lyon 5-6th May 2003 by Irving Finkel , Assistant Keeper, Depatment of the Ancient Near East.

5th May

In attendance were about 70 people: experts, dealers, UNESCO, and Interpol and customs agents. The day began with brief introductory remarks by two Interpol officials, followed by a presentation by Mounir Bouchenaki, Asst. Dir. Gen for Culture, UNESCO. These presentations emphasized the need for worldwide cooperation in tracking down stolen cultural property and the importance of synchronized and interlinked databases about these objects. Examples of such cooperation were drawn, including the return by Iraq of the looted Kuwaiti objects in 1991 and the ongoing attempts to control the looting of sites and museums in Afghanistan. It was also pointed out then and by other later speakers that the large-scale looting of Iraqi antiquities has been going on for about 12 years now, shortly after the first Gulf War.

There was a series of presentations by experts: Yasser Yabbaa, John Russell and Robert Biggs (U. Chicago), Irving Finkel (BM), M. van Ess (German Arch.Inst.), Bonnie Magness-Gardiner (State Dept.) Richard Halverson (Customs), plus experts from Lyon museums.

Yabbaa highlighted the richness of Islamic art and architecture in Iraq, stressing the development and sophistication of Iraqi cultural institutions (museums, libraries, and universities), and to assess the potential damage to these collections, especially the Iraq Museum and Library and the Mosul Museum.

John Russell presented objects that were or may have been lost in the looting. His list would be used as the foundation of the Red List.

I reported to the audience about the earlier meeting at the British Museum with Donny George and stressed the need to combine efforts quickly, the need to prevent exporting over the borders in Iraq and to clarify the illegality of dealing in materials in Iraq.

Van Ess emphasized the role of American and European digs in establishing an inventory for what might be lost from the storage of the IM.

She and others have noted the Iraqi's reluctance to divulge at this point a complete list of missing objects.
