History of the Department
The Department of Prints and Drawings, founded in 1808, is one of the oldest departments in the British Museum. The original collection was split off from the Library Departments (which are now housed in the British Library) and was formed of parts of the collection of Sir Hans Sloane, which had come at the foundation of the Museum in 1753; the bequest of William Fawkener in 1769 and the collection of a Trustee, the Rev. C.M. Cracherode, bequeathed in 1799. Cracherode was a reclusive and wealthy clergyman, who spent his entire life in building up his collections of books, drawings, prints, shells and minerals, which were one of the finest of their kind ever formed in this country. Although the huge collection of printed ephemera formed by Sarah Banks, the eccentric sister of Sir Joseph Banks, was given in 1818, it was only in the 1820s that the curatorial staff of the Department began to build on this base. The heroic period of acquisition, especially of prints, came between the 1820s and 1860s, when the generous purchase grants from the Government were used to buy on a huge scale.

Major acquisitions were made of William Smith's collection of early German and Italian engraving, and Edward Hawkins' collection of British satirical prints. The first catalogues began to be written in the 1870s, but it was in the Keepership of Sir Sidney Colvin that new staff were appointed, most notably Campbell Dodgson, Lawrence Binyon, A.M. Hind and A.E. Popham, whose scholarly publications first gave the Department the international reputation that it retains to the present day. Colvin realised that the collection of drawings lagged far behind the prints, and was responsible for the purchase of the Malcolm collection in 1895. This contained over 1,000 old master drawings of the highest quality. In the same period William Mitchell gave his outstanding collection of German Renaissance woodcuts, George Salting bequeathed a small but remarkable group of old master prints and drawings, and Baron Cheylesmore his huge collection of portrait mezzotints.

Since the Second World War, major additions have come to the Department through the gift by Count Antoine Seilern in 1946 of the main bulk of the Phillipps-Fenwick collection of old master drawings; in 1949 from the bequest of over 5,000 works, mostly prints from the previous 75 years, by the former Keeper of the Department, Campbell Dodgson, and in 1968 of sixteen outstanding 19th-century French drawings bequeathed by the collector César Mange de Hauke. Since the mid-1970s considerable efforts have been made to keep the collection abreast of modern developments, and groups of work continue to be built up to enable visitors to see as many good examples of modern drawing and printmaking as possible.

Factsheets have been produced on Blake, Cotman, Dürer, Hogarth, Michelangelo, Henry Moore, Rembrandt, Rubens, Turner and on certain special subjects: the collections of seventeenth century baroque drawings by artists working in Rome; American prints and drawings; British Satirical prints and Modern Cartoon drawings; British Watercolours; and the Modern Collection in general.
