23 May - 17 August
The Arts of Japan: a selection from the permanent collections
Admission free, Room 91 (Level 4, Montague Place Entrance)

The main exhibition is in Room 91 (Level 4) There are Japanese Buddhist sculptures on the North Stairs and prehistoric ceramics in the Montague Place Entrance

A wide range of materials includes archaeological and Buddhist works, ceramics, lacquerwares, swords and armour, Kyoto paintings of the 18th-19th centuries, and colour woodblock prints. Particularly featured are displays of painted fans for the summer, and domestic porcelains from the Shibata gift. Some outstanding new acquisitions are on view for the first time.

Situated on the periphery of East Asia and receiving waves of influence from that continent, Japan is the repository of cultural forms sometimes extinct in their lands of origin. Never invaded in historical times, many threads of ancient culture survive unbroken. Even when seismic changes have occurred, the old has rarely been displaced completely by the new. Imported Buddhism found accommodation with native kami worship (Shinto) in the 6th century. Military government by the samurai maintained the symbolic structures of the imperial lineage and court -- even while usurping their power -- from the 12th century.
A modernising Meiji regime promoted radical Westernisation from the 1870s in the name of the same ancient imperial dynasty.

The collections of traditional Japanese arts at the British Museum are the most comprehensive in Europe. Through them can be explored the ritual complexities of esoteric Buddhism; the aesthetic, romantic lifestyle of the court described by great literary works such the Tale of Genji (early 11th century); the rigour sought by a medieval samurai elite in the arts of Zen Buddhism; and the wide use and enjoyment of elegant paintings, prints, decorated ceramics and lacquers by samurai and merchants alike in Edo (Tokyo) and other large cities during the Edo Period (1615-1868).

Recent exhibitions:
Kazari: Decoration and Display in Japan,
15th - 19th Centuries

Organised by the Japan Society, New York and The British Museum, in association with the Suntory Museum of Art, Tokyo.


Made possible by Fidelity Investments through the Fidelity Foundation. With gratitude to Mr Kazuo Okada for generous support through the Japan Foundation.

What is Kazari?
Kazari is the Japanese art and experience of arranging and displaying decorative objects. It refers not only to the object, but also to its use in specific settings and contexts, and requires the active participation of imagination or memory. Stimulating the senses through the acts of viewing, using, or adorning a work of art, kazari manifests the dynamism inherent in Japanese aesthetics, and suggests the process that transforms the everyday into something extraordinary.

The exhibition
The exhibition is divided into six sections which show the context in which decorative objects such as paintings, ceramics, textiles, lacquerware and metalwork were displayed and used in everyday life. Most of these rare and outstanding items are on loan from collections in Japan and the United States and are on show in London for the first time. Because of problems of fading, some items can only be shown for a limited time. Therefore some of the paintings have been changed, and a different set of textiles will be on display until the exhibition closes, including silk kosode (kimono) with embroidered, dyed and painted designs, quilted cotton firefighters' jackets, warriors' overjackets (jimbaori) in imported European wool, and theatre costumes.

The exhibition also contains some superb ceramics including original pieces by the 18th century master Ogata Kenzan, the 19th century Nin'ami Dohachi, and some outstanding Shigaraki and Iga tea wares. Also on display are lacquered No theatre drums, boxes and cosmetic sets, distinctive hair accessories and ornamental metal vases.

The exhibits will be organised in six chronological and thematic sections, presenting superb examples of decorative and fine art objects that correspond to particular periods of high cultural achievements from the Muromachi (1392-1573), Momoyama (1573-1615), and Edo (1615-1868) periods. From the shogun's court in the fifteenth century, through the prosperous merchants of the early seventeenth century, to the pleasure districts of burgeoning Edo, the exhibition will show how the arts of decoration and display were integral to Japanese culture.

The catalogue
In full colour, with contributions by nine leading international scholars in the field of kazari studies, the book makes a significant contribution to the appreciation and understanding of Japanese arts.

The exhibition is co-curated by Nicole Coolidge Rousmaniere Sainsbury Institute for the Study of Japanese Art and Culture (SISJAC), London and Norwich, and Tsuji Nobuo,Tama Art University, Tokyo.

Permanent Exhibitions
The Japanese Galleries (the Konica Gallery, the Main Gallery, the Urasenke Gallery) opened officially on 6 April 1990. They are used for a programme of changing exhibitions. They include two semi-permanent elements. One is the Tea House in the Urasenke Gallery, which itself forms an exhibit furnished with modern tea utensils; and an adjacent case which always has a display of historic Tea Ceremony wares. The other is a group of cases on the entrance landing, sponsored by Brian and Esther Pilkington, to hold rotating selections of netsuke, inro and sword furniture from the Ingram and Hull Grundy Collections.
