18th century British and American peace medals
Department of Coins and Medals, the British Museum

For more than two centuries, bronze and silver medals were presented to Native leaders by European administrators, explorers and by US Indian agents. Ornamentally they took the place of what had originally been shell gorgets, in use by Indians for five hundred years before European contact, by peoples of the Mississippian tradition. Peace medals were used to ensure peaceful passage for explorers, and to cement treaty negotiations. In return buffalo robes, pipes and calumet stems, and clubs were freely given to the European and European-American negotiators. The European medals of, for instance, Louis XIV, the Georges and Queen Victoria, all bore likenesses of monarchs. After the American Revolution Indian leaders continue to prefer medals with portraits, so the United States between the presidencies of Thomas Jefferson and Benjamin Harrison copied the European style by using presidential images.
