Iroquoian trophy, c. 1886, Canada.
Mullanphy collection.

This composite wood trophy is designed in the form of a small frame for a photograph or picture. It was intended to sit on a desk, mantelpiece or occasional table in a Victorian home to symbolise Native North America to the purchaser.

It stands like a painter's easel on hinged back support. The frame consists of a number of carved renditions of a series of Indian artefacts, mostly out of proportion to one another. There is a canoe, axes, bows, snow shoe, spear, and a pair of lacrosse sticks. These are Iroquoian, rather than say Ojibwa in style, and so this frame might have been either made by the Huron/Wendat at Wendake/Villages des Huron, or in a Québec Mohawk community such as Kanawake / Caughnawaga, or Kanasatake / Oka.

Shops in Montréal or Québec City would have sold this type of souvenir, as perhaps also would have curio stores at Niagara Falls. The trophy is a very fine example of the type of item sent to world's fairs or exhibitions, this one was probably acquired at the 1886 Colonial and Indian Exhibition at South Kensignton in London.
