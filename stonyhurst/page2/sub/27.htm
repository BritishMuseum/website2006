Model Mi'kmaq birchbark canoe decorated with porcupine quillwork
before 1820, Nova Scotia. Stonyhurst Collection.

Birchbark is a most useful raw material: light, flexible, strong, and easily obtained. The best bark is collected just before winter, in the Autumn during a period of Indian summer. On a hot day the bark comes off the log easily, or alternatively it may be peeled off after soaking with boiling water


Mi'kmaq woman making baskets. Mary R. McKie, fl. 1840-1862, watercolour. Winkworth Collection of Canadiana, National Archives of Canada, Ottawa.

Articles of bark were traditionally sewn with split spruce root. Bark was used for many houseshold containers, for berry collecting for instance, and for storing maple syrup. Birchbark objects have been prized by Europeans and Euro-Americans for many centuries.In the nineteenth century numerous communities made Victorian style souvenirs to sell to visitors.
