Northern Plains Parfleche
Before 1825, Mullanphy Collection.


Rawhide section of a parfleche, or storage envelope, painted with abstract geometric designs. While this French Canadian term refers to parrying (parer) an arrow (flêche), and so to raw hide shields, parfleches are containers formed of sheets of decorated rawhide. They are folded with rectangular, rather than triangular, flaps.

Parfleches were used in great numbers by women of the Plains, Rocky mountain and Plateau regions to store everything from pemmican, ground buffalo meat with fat and berries, to household utensils. Their purpose, was, of course to facilitate transportation, whether it was by dog travois in the pre-horse days or, later, by horse.

The preferred skin was buffalo before their virtual disappearance by the 1880s. Buffalo rawhide was replaced by cattle, and also by elk and horse hide. The fresh hide would de-fleshed, and then stretched and dried by pegging towards, but just off the ground. Washing, scraping and thinning would then take place. The best way of removing hair was by pounding with a flat axe back or stone; this also made of the exterior surface of the skin bright and white; an alternative was scraping off the hair with an elk antler scraper.

The painting was executed on the flesh side of the skin, and sized, with a liquid made from skins scrapings, to protect the finish article and contents from damp. The abstract geometric painting style is associated with that used on Great Lakes and Woodlands robes, and in general terms is without symbolic meaning. When parfleches were worn out, the rawhide would be cut up and recycled to make moccasin soles; the painted sides would be left on the interior.
