Native lands, maps and treaties

The Missouri-Mississippi valleys

"From an Indian point of view St. Louis would have been a very interesting city, maybe not big enough to be threatening like New York. It would have been close enough to the open land so that they might feel still comfortable, and full of strange and interesting people, lots of trade and diplomatic opportunities."
JoAllyn Archambault

The riverine heartland of America includes the Mississippi, Missouri and Ohio River basins. There Indian civilisations flourished, most notably those of the peoples of the Mississippian traditions. The mounds of the city of Cahokia, with a population of 20,000 equal to that of London's in the twelth century, lie opposite the city of St Louis in Illinois. The decline in population preceded European colonisation, and accelerated from the sixteenth century onwards with the introduction of new diseases such as small pox. By the time of colonisation in the eighteenth century the Mississippi lay at the heart of an elaborate trade net work, for fur, timber and other raw materials which stretched throughout much of what is now the United States. The main entrepôt city was French St. Louis, founded in 1764.

Maps

Hunting societies depend on high levels of technical ecological knowledge: this includes knowledge of geography, stars, seasons, and vegetable and animal resources.

At the time of European contact maps were made utilising knowledge of this kind: they were drawn on skin and birchbark, carved of wood, engraved on snow and ice, or rendered in dry sand. Most were highly ephemeral. European and North American explorers often relied on this knowledge, copying, engraving and publishing this information while allowing original maps to disappear. Native maps are characterised by an angular schematic representation of physical features. One of the earliest surviving Native maps shows the states of Indiana and Illinois.

Bryan Mullanphy brought this unique map on skin to Lancashire. It is suggested by geographer G.Malcolm Lewis, that this map relates to the sale of lands negotiated just before the American Revolution in 1774-5 by the Wea people in the Wabash river drainage in Indiana.

The Wea were one of six confederated Native tribes or peoples known as the Miami in the late eighteenth century. The nature of the confrontation of Indians and white people is well illustrated by the biography of the Miami leader, and war chief, Little Turtle, Michikinikwa (c. 1752-1812). He fought with the British during the American Revolution. His people, along with others of the Midwest refused to accept United States' rule imposed after 1783. Instead in the early 1790s, Little Turtle inflicted major defeats on the United States in defending Indian sovereignty. But Little Turtle's recognition of the extent of United States power led, after Indian defeat at Battle of Fallen Timbers in 1794, to his signing a treaty at Fort Greenville the following year. This ceded much of the Ohio region to the United States. Thereafter, as a peace chief, and having been abandoned by his former British allies, he was obliged to sign further treaties. Dissatisfaction with these led eventually to the resumption of war, in 1812, the year of Little Turtle's death from gout.

Negotiations and treaties

"Land is at the heart of negotiations between Indians and whites. The British crown and later the American government sequestered to itself the right to control land acquisition with Indian tribes."

JoAllyn Archambault

From the time of first contact with Native North America, European nations sought to acquire Indian lands legitimately from Native nations. Treaty transfers were negotiated by sovereign states such as Britain and France, and land purchases were entered into by speculative land companies. The young United States insisted in the eighteenth century that all land transfers be negotiated with the federal government. Some 800 treaties were made by the US Federal Government before the process ended in 1871. Less than half were ratified. In the European sense treaties are an aspect of realpolitick; for Native Americans they validate and substantiate their rights as first inhabitants. Much land was sold to speculator companies, as indicated in the 1774-5 map in the Mullanphy collection; other lands were alienated from Native nations after the Homestead Act of 1862. Grants and sales of land provided the capital for the building of the 1869 trans-continental railroads and universities, such as the University of Texas, Austin.
