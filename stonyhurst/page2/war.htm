Warfare and the hunt

In the 1820s the primary source of food for Plains and Prairie Indians was the buffalo.

"The 1820's were a time of intense intertribal warfare, and most of it resulted from tribes that were displaced from the east and northeast, moving into the plains seeking new lands and resources. Unfortunately they were moving into other people's territories and hunting their game which precipitated conflict."

 JoAllyn Archambault

Buffalo were hunted originally by creating pounds, into which the beasts could be driven and killed, with spears and bows. They were also hunted by stealth and by driving animals over cliffs. A military-like precision was required for success in the hunt.

During the eighteenth century increasing numbers of horses were acquired for the purposes of the hunt, from Spanish sources. Horses were used for travel with an 'A' framed sled or travois, and for warfare. Warfare was conducted over resources and to obtain horses.

As nomadic peoples following the movement of buffalos, Plains people were great travellers. Travel, warfare and trading took place mostly in the summer, and were conducted by men. Added to these activities were all important negotiations with incoming Americans. Indian people were eager to develop trading relations, and wished to organise peaceful settlement on the frontier. Most artefacts collected at this period were items of men's equipment, freely given, obtained on these occasions, either in the so-called wilderness or in cities such as St. Louis.

Plains Indian Warfare

"All tribes controlled their own lands and fought to preserve their exclusive use of them and their natural resources. To hunt or travel in another tribe's territory was an invitation to war."

JoAllyn Archambault

As mentioned the arrival of the horse brought an elaboration of culture, and particularly here of material culture and art.

During the pre-horse era war and fighting required large shields, of the thickest neck rawhide of bison. Horseback warfare required smaller shields, but also armour for the protection of horses.

In the Mullanphy collection is a fine shield, painted white with a hide cover. This is decorated with a turtle, perhaps a protective personal or clan symbol. Also in the collection were originally two pieces of painted rawhide, each with two circles, which fitted across the horse flanks underneath the saddle and girth, that is the belly strap holding the saddle.

Horses were themselves also decorated, with ownership marks and protective symbols. Art, pictographic and symbolic, tended towards the realistic, depicted war exploits in a stylised manner. On shirts and robes war records proclaimed the status of warriors, while on clubs the numbers of war parties or dead might be incised into the staff.

It is important to emphasise that the killing or scalping of the enemy, was not the only way of obtaining honour. Instead there was a hierarchy of honours, tribally and inter-tribally recognised, in which the touching of enemies, the capture of horses, and of weapons and accoutrements formed an intricate whole.

Feathers, and the development of feather headdress, and war bonnets, of the black and white feathers of the immature golden eagle, also played a part in the process of indicating high honour.

Two main styles of headdress co-existed in the nineteenth century - the Blackfoot 'straight up' headdress, with vertical feathers in a ring, and the Sioux-type bonnet with a spray of feathers attached on or around a cap, sometimes with a long train streaming down.

Eagle feathers were obtained by luring birds with a bait. A pit would be dug, large enough to hide a man. This would be covered over with brush and a rabbit placed on top. When an eagle set down, the legs would be grabbed and some of the tail feathers removed.
