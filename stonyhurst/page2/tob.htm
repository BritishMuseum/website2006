Tobacco, the sacred herb

"All adult men owned a pipe of their own as did some women. Pipes ranged from elaborately decorated ones to small, plain ones. Smoking had important social, religious, political and recreational dimensions for all Plains peoples."

 JoAllyn Archambault

The four tobacco pipes form numerically the largest group of objects in the Mullanphy collection. These consist of four elbow-shaped stone pipe bowls, and four flat stems of ash. Technically they are called calumets, from the French chalumet, meaning reed or pipe.

The genus tobacco consists of a number of species originating across the Americas; the kind smoked today is Nicotiana tobaccum, from Brazil with a higher nicotine content than local North American species such as Nicotiana rustica, and N. quadrivalvis. The cultivation of tobacco began more than two thousand years ago. It is known to have been smoked in tubes by people of the ancient Adena culture of the Midwest.

"Of course tobacco was not the only herb smoked in pipes. While Nicotiana was one of the species used for smoking, other substances included various shredded barks, pulverised roots, and dried leaves, all of which might be combined with nicotiana if available. Trade tobacco was highly valued once introduced."

JoAllyn Archambault


The use of tobacco, and other sacred materials such as eagle down, sweet grass, and cedar bark, acts today to bless and sanctify a very wide range of activities. The stereotyped concept of the peace pipe indicates only one partial, though important use. Tobacco is more generally smoked communally to share blessings, to open trade relations, or alliance in war, or to start a ceremony relating for instance to healing or prayers for rain, and other horticultural and hunting projects. Dancing, with calumet stems and staff, was and is an important aspect of tobacco related ceremonies.

"In addition to the formal events at which tobacco was smoked, there was recreational smoking during which both men and women smoked. Sometimes one sees, small, simple pipe bowls and these may have been recreational pipes complete with shorter, plain stems. Men smoked when they had their warrior society meetings which were largely social events for the 'guys'..."

JoAllyn Archambault

Well into the second half of the nineteenth century calumet stems were decorated with porcupine quillwork embroidery in a variety of techniques, and with featherwork. Featherwork includes the addition of mallard and woodpecker skin, iridescent and red, and also hanging sprays of feathers - particularly the black and white feathers of the immature golden eagle.

"Contemporary pipe makers use the same materials to decorate stems although very elaborate stems are usually sold to non-Indians. I have never seen such a elaborate stem used by an Indian in religious practice."

JoAllyn Archambault

One of the pipes in the collection has a calumet stem, of ash, with a series of lead or pewter inlaid symbols, burnt or stained dark brown. These include three or four main elements: stars, horned animal heads, wing like profiles, and the US Eagle. Images of horned heads, probably bison, are common elements in medicine and religion to do with buffalo, and appear in pictographs. Stars, sun and moon are also important in religious iconography, particularly among the Pawnee. The flying bird-like profiles remain enigmatic.

The image of the bald eagle, in the United States crest, is however fairly straightforward. Shortly after the Declaration of Independence in 1776, Benjamin Franklin, John Adams and Thomas Jefferson, were appointed by the Continental Congress to develop a two sided seal to use in national business to authenticate documents. This story is described by Bruce E. Beans in Eagle's Plume (1996).

After much discussion, the design created by William Barton, a Philadelphia lawyer, was adapted by the Irish secretary of Congress Charles Barton and accepted in 1782. The design of an eagle originally symbolised Zeus, Zeus having assumed the form of an eagle when pursuing Ganymede with thunderbolts. In the great seal of the United States the shield is striped, with 13 stars above for the original colonies. The thunderbolts have been replaced by arrows, here representing war, peace indicated by the olive branch on the left. Benjamin Franklin, and later the naturalist John James Audubon did not approve: Franklin wrote scathingly in 1782 that the Bald Eagle 'is a Bird of Bad Moral Character. He does not get his Living Honestly' but steals other bird's catches like men 'who live by Sharping and Robbing'.

Unfortunately we do know when nor why this calumet stem was created. However it must have been made for some quite specific purpose. It is likely to have been used on the occasion of treaty making; this might for instance have been on an occasion when a US government commission visited a specific village to make a treaty; or possibly when a Native delegation made a journey to make a treaty in, say, St. Louis. The pipe would have been created specifically for the occasion. The inlay of these symbols would have been simply achieved: by pouring the molten metal into the carved elements, and perhaps, as with later inlaid stone pipe bowls, wrapped with raw hide to create a mould and avoid spillage.

"During these diplomatic events it was common for Indian leaders to give elaborate pipes to the high status visitors which is why so many of these have ended up in collections."

JoAllyn Archambault

Detail of metal inlay, with the U.S. eagle crest in the middle, on ash peace pipe-stem.

Smoking pipes were used to create social bonds during treaties. The wooden stem of a pipe with a stone bowl is decorated with pictographs. These symbols include stars, buffalo heads (relating to fertility), and the US Eagle. The image of the bald eagle is taken from the United States crest, as seen on coins, indicating that this pipe was made to be used during a specific treaty negotiation.
