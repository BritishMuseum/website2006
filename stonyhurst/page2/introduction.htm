Introduction

This web site introduces Native North American through a newly acquired collection. A group of superb and often unique Sioux objects introduce ideas about Native American peoples and their struggles on the American frontier in the early nineteenth century (1775-1825). Later Native souvenirs, from Algonquian peoples, takes the story to Reservations and the end of the nineteenth century, while a section on Powwow celebrates the story of Native revival to-day. The collection also includes items acquired in the nineteenth century by Jesuit missionaries and their associates.

The period was one of exceptional change - of the arrival of French and British colonists, of the American Revolution and of the War of 1812. For Native peoples the arrival of the United States, and the creation of territories and then states, spelled the end of effective autonomy for a group of diverse Nations.

On tour
Highlights of the collection will be touring the north of Ireland, the birth place of the main collector, John Mullanphy.
The exhibition Irish American Trade, Native American Art, will be at the Ulster American Folk Park, Omagh July 1-October 22, Ulster Museum, Belfast October 29-March 30 2005, and at Fermanagh County Museum, Enniskillen April 14-May 26 2005. Please check respective websites for dates and details.

At the BM
A rotating selection of the Sioux and Algonquian collection will be on permanent display in the North American Gallery (26) at the British Museum, and further details of star objections are available on COMPASS - the Museum's object database. Organic objects, for reasons of conservation, cannot be placed on permanent display.

Note: Indian people use the terms 'Native people' and 'Native Nation', as well as 'Indian' to describe themselves. In 1492 when Christopher Columbus arrived in the Bahamas, he thought he was in India; hence the misleading epithet 'Indian'. This remains in use to-day, for instance as in the newspaper Indian Country Today, and The National Museum of the American Indian. In Canada 'First Nations' is the preferred term for Indian or Native.
