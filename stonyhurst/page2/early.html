Early trading: Women's roles

"Women's work was essential for survival on the Plains. They were half of a whole that made life possible. Plains societies had complementary gender roles, both of which were necessary if the people were to survive. Women's work was valued and valuable, respected by men, and more consistent than male work which was more sporadic. Hence the commonplace observations by white men which portrayed the male as lazy and the woman as constantly working."

JoAllyn Archambault

During the early nineteenth century it was mostly men who travelled on hunting, trading and treaty negotiating expeditions. It was therefore men's goods, mostly clothing, weapons, and pipe paraphernalia, that were acquired by early collectors such as the Mullanphys.

Women's roles were vital: firstly they were responsible for child raising. Infants were kept swaddled in cradle boards, beautifully decorated with Indian and European folk motifs, quill embroidery and imported goods such as brass bells. Sphagnum moss, with disinfectant qualities was used to wrap babies in, easily available and easily disposed of.

From an economic point of view the laborious women's work of preparing skins, for clothing for own use, and for trade was vital. In the early nineteenth century the trade in beaver pelts, from what is now Montana, dominated the fur trade. These were prepared by women, as were buffalo skins, which later became the most important article of trade.

Men, always keen to travel, were traders, needing a constant supply of skins to sell. These were required to obtain new necessities such as firearms, increasingly necessary for defence, against invasive Americans, and also traditional Native enemies pushed westwards by the frontier.

In some tribes men married more than one wife, to ensure an increased supply of skins. In turn women were given imported finery such as cloth, beads and silver brooches by their husbands.

Hardest work was the preparation of skins: defleshing, scraping and softening skins is very time consuming. These include preparation of buckskin and antelope for clothing, buffalo skin for tipis and saddles. A woman might expect to make several thousand moccasins in her life time.

Rawhide was used for creating parfleches (folding containers) used to store goods, such as pemmican (dried buffalo meat pounded and mixed with fat and berries).

Non-Natives could easily prepare rawhide. In the middle of the nineteenth century demand for this material, for industrial belts to drive machinery, contributed to the virtual extermination of the buffalo by American hunters. This lead to the disappearance of the nomadic way of life, at the time when Native Peoples were being confined to Reservations.

While accomplished warriors depicted their exploits in pictographs on robes, women were involved spiritually with the buffalo and buffalo hunt. Women's robes were painted with abstract representations of buffalo, designed to ensure the fertility and availability of the animal on whom all depended for food, clothing, tipis, and rawhide for containers, receptacles, spoons and also armour.

"Moccasins are something that a girl learns how to make at a relatively young age. Probably by the end of their life women have made thousands of them."

JoAllyn Archambault

Male Indian traders depended on their wives' abilities in the laborious business of preparing skins for economic success. The more successful a trader was, the easier it was to acquire muskets and ammunition, required in the hunt, and in warfare both with non-Natives and with other Native groups competing for ever reduced space on the Plains. Among some Indian poples men took more than one wife to ensure labour for skin preparation.

Through twentieth century Indian women continued to make clothing and highly decorated costume for Native celebrations, such as the Powwow. These summer events bring together peoples from across to the continents to celebrate Indian dance and culture. Highly decorated costume, often incorporating beadwork, is an important element of self expression, both for individuals and for cultural diverse tribes. Competitions stimulate creativity, and the evolving fashions, such as the jingle dress.
