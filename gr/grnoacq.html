The Corinth or Guilford Puteal

In 1813 Frederick North, son of George III's Prime Minister Lord North and a noted, if eccentric, philhellene, had shipped to London some sixty crates of marble sculpture for display in his Westminster home. Among them was a monument already known to western travellers to Greece, then under Ottoman Turkish rule. It had stood in the garden of a Greek official named Notara, a cultured man with a fine library, indeed a member of a distinguished family who could trace their descent back to the Byzantine Paleologi. In the earliest years of the 19th century Notara presided over a guest-house for western travellers to Corinth. In his garden the monument had served as a well-head, Notara inverting the hollowed drum in an attempt to save the remaining decoration. This was at least its second term of duty in the role of well-head or puteal: the previous owner, a Turk, had displayed it the right way up, thereby endangering the remains of the figured decoration. Its exterior surface carved in low relief, the monument had already lost its upper moulding, much of the bead-and-reel decoration of its lower moulding, and, most likely from an act of vandalism of unknown date, the heads of the figures moving in two processions around the drum.

Notwithstanding these severe depredations, the puteal, half a metre in height and just over a metre in diameter, remained visually attractive and intellectually interesting: whilst in Notara's garden it was drawn by Edward Dodwell's artist Simone Pomardi, and at an unknown date by Dodwell himself; also by the Count von Stackelberg, from casts made of it and taken to Athens; it was, too, described by Dodwell in his account of his travels in Greece, and by Colonel William Leake.

Of the processions, one was led by Apollo, followed by his sister Artemis trailing her stag, and a third figure, most likely their mother Leto. Behind Leto came Hermes, instantly recognisable in his winged sandals, and leading a trio of elegant women, probably nymphs. Confronting Apollo stands Athena, her arm extended to hold her helmet; behind her marches Herakles with his club on his shoulder and a quiver beneath his arm, with an unidentified woman behind him- possibly his bride Hebe.

Carved in Pentelic marble, the figures were spaced wide apart, and were designed in an elegant version of the style current in archaic Greece. However, it was quickly recognised that the monument was not an original archaic work of the 6th century BC, but a later reprise of the style. Dodwell perceptively recognised its close links with a relief in the collection of the Villa Albani in Rome, catalogued in the 18th century by the founding father of classical art history, J. J. Winckelmann.

In 1817 Frederick North succeeded to his title and became the 5th Earl of Guilford. He lived another ten years, and on his death the Westminster house and its contents were acquired by the M.P. Thomas Wentworth Beaumont, a member of a Yorkshire family. When the German scholar Adolf Michaelis came to compile his great work on Ancient Marbles in Great Britain, the Corinth puteal was lost to scholarly knowledge, and in a stirring article in the Journal of Hellenic Studies he urged London-based classicists to look for it. A century later, its whereabouts remained unknown, and the puteal, featured in several works on archaising sculpture, was known only from the 19th-century drawings. These had enjoyed, it seemed, a greater respect than their subject..
