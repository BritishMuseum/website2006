Archives

An early source of information on the collection which remains a frequently consulted and useful research tool for the curators are the entries describing individual antiquities made by Samuel Birch, Keeper of Oriental Antiquities from 1860. These manuscript notes, known as the Birch slips, are now bound into numbered volumes.

The earliest records of museum administration, and correspondence concerning Egyptian antiquities, are held in the museum's Central Archives. Although the museum was founded in 1753, a separate Department of Antiquities was not founded until 1807, and it was not until 1860 that there was a three-way division of the Department of Antiquities into a department for Greece and Rome, a department for Coins and Medals, and the Oriental Department (comprising Egypt, Assyria, British and Medieval Antiquities and Ethnography). In 1886, the Oriental Department was renamed Egyptian and Assyrian Antiquities, and it was not until 1955 that the department split into the Department of Egyptian Antiquities (now renamed the Department of Ancient Egypt and Sudan) and the Department of Western Asiatic Antiquities (now the Department of the Ancient Near East). This rather complex history has meant that the Egyptian correspondence, for a time, was bound yearly with the Western Asiatic material, and these papers are now held in the library of the Ancient Near East Department. The letters are arranged alphabetically within what are mainly single volumes for each year from 1826.

The rest of the archival material is amazingly diverse, defying neat classification. It includes the personal papers and diaries of former keepers, curators and excavators, early nineteenth-century photographs and heavy wooden boxes of glass negatives, early drawings and watercolours, and an important set of 'squeezes', made by J.G. Wilkinson. These were impressions, on specially treated paper, of ancient reliefs and inscriptions. There are also early x-rays of various mummies, and memorabilia from the museum's blockbuster Tutankhamun Exhibition held in 1972, when long queues of visitors stretched around the block. The museum keeps records of its displays and exhibitions, and the original artwork for its catalogues. All the museum's records are part of the public records of the nation and thus come under strict regulations for their preservation.
