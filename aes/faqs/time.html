Time, Eternity and the Year

Egyptian words for eternity

djet 'linear time', succession of unique and unrepeated moments with linear 'land' determinative.

neheh 'circular time', cycle of repeated seasons and ages with circular 'sun' determinative.

Note that the stock phrase translated as 'for ever and ever' specifies 'to djet and neheh'.

The year

renpet 'year', abed 'month', su 'day' (in dates) (usual word for day is 'heru')

The solar year (the time it takes the earth to go around the sun) is 365.25 days. In the ancient and medieval periods, the river Nile followed a natural annual cycle of summer to autumn flood (fields covered by the river), winter to spring season of sowing crops (fields emerge from the receding waters), and spring to summer dry season (river at its lowest). The annual flood was heralded in the skies by the day (in July) when for the first time in months the star Sirius rose in advance of the sun at dawn.

The Egyptian words for the three seasons were akhet 'flood', peret 'spring' (literally 'emergence') and shemu 'drought'. The agricultural year revolved around this Nile cycle, with an idle autumn during the flood, a sowing season in late winter and spring, and the labour intensive harvest season in early summer.

Although the Nile cycle was one of the most regular patterns in nature, the height of the flood was never entirely predictable, depending on rains in the Ethiopian highlands at the river sources. A low Nile brought famine, and an excessive flood might wreak devastation, as in 1821 when the river swept away the ruins of the Ptolemaic temple at Qau in Upper Egypt.

During the nineteenth and twentieth centuries governments sought to control the river in order to prevent the death and disease caused by low or high Niles; from the 1830s to the 1960s a series of dams and barrages were built, culminating in the construction of the High Dam at Aswan. This control and the introduction of cash crops in the early nineteenth century have changed the agricultural base of Egyptian life; cotton, bananas, potatoes and other imports have replaced the flax, barley and emmer wheat of ancient Egypt.

The civil or regnal year (i.e. year of reign of the current king) consisted of 12 months of thirty days each, with an addition of 5 days at the end of the year to round it up in single days as near as possible to the solar year.

The missing quarter day meant that the civil year slipped by one day every 4 years, and by a full year every 365 x 4 years. This means that it is more difficult for us to calculate specific dates in ancient texts; it does not mean that the system functioned any less efficiently for its basic purpose of measuring time across generations e.g. for legal and administrative cases.

Some festivals and periods of temple observance did not need such long-term time-measuring; therefore they used instead the shorter, dramatic lunar cycle, where the moon visibly loses and gains its shape every 28 days.

This meant that in effect three calendars were in operation: in addition to the monthly lunar cycle, there was the 'natural' solar year determining the agricultural cycle of flood, sowing-season and harvest, and the 'civil year' by which most important occasions were fixed (e.g. the 'harvest festival' in month 9 of the civil year would, with the moving of the civil year of 365 days against the natural solar year of 365.25 days, move gradually backwards out of the harvest season into the flood season until 365x4 years brought the cycle full circle again and it was celebrated again on the correct day - this shows the religious power of the civil 'royal' year with its precedence over the natural solar year).

Civil years were calculated from year 1 of the reigning king. This made it necessary to keep king lists recording years, months and days of each reign: the only surviving manuscript is the Turin King list, a fragmentary papyrus of the 13th century BC, with kings from the beginning until (in the preserved portion) 17th dynasty and the Hyksos 'foreign kings' of c. 1650-1550 BC.

Summarising king lists were inscribed in stone for certain occasions: at Abydos in royal cult temples of the 19th Dynasty (13th century BC) to commemorate the full string of ancestral kings back to the god-king Osiris, and at Karnak in a sanctuary of Thutmose III of the 18th Dynasty (c. 1450 BC) to record apparently all kings whose statues were at the temple at that date (perhaps specifically those kings whose cults were renewed at the time that their statues had to be moved to make way for the grand new temple constructions of Thutmose III). These monuments provided crucial materials to 19th century Egyptologists in their first attempts to introduce order into the mass of materials surviving from ancient Egypt, because the Abydos and Turin King lists are in the same order and this proves to be the chronological sequence of kings.
