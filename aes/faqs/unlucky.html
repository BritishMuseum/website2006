The 'Unlucky Mummy'

What is it?

The 'Unlucky Mummy' is not a mummy at all, but rather a gessoed and painted wooden 'mummy-board' or inner coffin lid (height 162 cm), numbered EA 22542. It was found at Thebes and can be dated by its shape and the style of its decoration to the late 21st or early 22nd Dynasty (c 950-900 BC).

The beardless face and the position of the hands with fingers extended show that it was made to cover the mummy of a woman. Her identity is not known: the brief hieroglyphic inscriptions contain only short religious phrases, and omit mention of the name of the deceased. The high quality of the lid indicates that the owner was a person of high rank. It was usual for such ladies to participate in the musical accompaniments to the rituals in the temple of Amen-Ra; hence early British Museum publications described the owner of 22542 as a 'priestess of Amen-Ra'. E A Wallis Budge, Keeper of Egyptian and Assyrian Antiquities from 1894 to 1924, also suggested that she might have been of royal blood, but this was pure speculation and is not supported by the iconography of the lid.

History of EA 22542

The mummy-board was presented to the British Museum in July 1889 by Mrs Warwick Hunt of Holland Park, London, on behalf of Mr Arthur F Wheeler. It was displayed in the 'First Egyptian Room' of the Museum as early as 1890 and has remained on public view ever since, with the exception of periods during the First and Second World Wars, when it was removed from its case for safety. It has left the Museum only once, in 1990, when it formed part of a temporary exhibition held at two venues in Australia. It is currently displayed in Room 62. The mummy to which EA 22542 belonged is said to have been left in Egypt; it never formed part of the collections of the British Museum.

The 'Unlucky Mummy' in folklore

The mummy-board has acquired a reputation for bringing misfortune, and a vast web of mythology has developed around it. It has been credited with causing death, injury and large-scale disasters such as the sinking of the Titanic in 1912. None of these stories has any basis in fact, but from time to time the strength of the rumours has led to a flood of enquiries on the subject. A disclaimer written by Wallis Budge was published in 1934, and yet since that time the myth has undergone further embellishment. The main elements of the story are as follows.

A 'curse' on the owners

The mummy-board is said to have been bought by one of four young English travellers during a holiday in Egypt at some time during the 1860s or 1870s. Shortly afterwards one of these men suffered a gunshot wound which deprived him of the use of his right arm. A second died in another shooting incident. The third and fourth both died in poverty within a short time. The mummy-board was passed to the sister of one of the travellers, but as soon as it had entered the house the occupants suffered a series of misfortunes. The celebrated clairvoyant Madame Helena Blavatsky, while visiting the house, is alleged to have detected an evil influence, which was ultimately traced to the mummy-board. She urged the owner to dispose of it and in consequence it was presented to the British Museum. The carrier who transported the lid to the museum died within a week and even a man who had helped to carry the case into the building suffered a serious accident. These incidents were attributed to the malign spirit of the 'priestess' who had been buried with the mummy-board.

Photographic peculiarities

The lid had been photographed before being given to the British Museum. When the negative was developed it was said to have revealed in place of the painted mask 'the face of a living Egyptian woman of malevolent aspect'. The photographer committed suicide within the year. In or about 1904 the photographer Mansell was commissioned to photograph the lid again. Returning after a visit to the museum to make preparations, Mansell's son injured his thumb during the railway journey, and the photographer reached home to find that one of his children had suffered a dangerous accident. While photographing EA 22542 the following day the same photographer injured his face on a glass display case and damaged a piece of photographic apparatus.

A 'soul in torment'

Among those who took a special interest in the 'Unlucky Mummy' were Thomas Douglas Murray (died 1911) and the journalist W T Stead. Murray, an amateur Egyptologist and associate of the excavator Flinders Petrie, is reported to have been instrumental in having the lid presented to the British Museum. Stead was among the first to publish the story of its supposed malign influence. Both men were interested in the paranormal, and 'decided that the expression on the face [of the lid] was that of a living soul in torment'. As recounted by Budge, 'They wished to hold a seance in a room and to perform certain experiments with the object of removing the anguish and misery from the eyes of the coffin-lid.' The publication of their views resulted in donations of money from Algeria and New Zealand to pay for floral offerings to be laid before the lid. 'Sad to say', recalls Budge, 'I believe that the money offerings were absorbed by the hard hearted Treasurer of the Museum, and no flowers were bought.'

Removal to the basements

By 1908-9 the lid had gained a nationwide reputation for causing unexplained mishaps. It was now also believed to emit strange sounds in the night. The terrified security staff of the museum, it was alleged, refused to approach it and demanded that it be removed from the public galleries. It was therefore, according to the story, removed from display and stored in the museum's basements. An exact replica was made and displayed in place of the original, until an American visitor noticing this, threatened the museum with public humiliation for exhibiting a 'fake'.

The Titanic

Once satisfied of the reason for the substitution, the American offered to buy the original lid for a museum in the USA. The offer was accepted by the authorities of the British Museum, and the purchaser shipped the lid on the Titanic on its maiden voyage in 1912. The presence of such an ill-omened object on board caused the ship to collide with an iceberg and sink. The lid, however, survived this disaster: according to one version of the story, the wealthy owner succeeded by bribery in having the piece brought safely to the USA; an alternative account states that the wooden crate containing the lid was salvaged after the disaster. Such was the notoriety of 22542 that the story of its supposed association with the Titanic was in circulation within months of the disaster - yet at no point in the interim had it left the British Museum. A possible explanation for the association of 22542 with the Titanic lies in the fact that W T Stead was among those who died when the ship sank and, according to one of the survivors, told the story of the British Museum's 'unlucky mummy case' to fellow passengers during the voyage.

Further exploits

Following its alleged arrival in the USA the lid is supposed to have continued to bring misfortune. Its American owner sold it to a Canadian, who took it to Montreal. 'Even there (recounts Budge) it continued its evil course, and when finally its owner decided to send it back to England, it sunk when the Empress of Ireland went down in the St Lawrence River'.

The Egyptologist Margaret Murray claimed in her autobiography (1963) that she had concocted this part of the story, apparently as a badly-timed joke. By this time, however, the myth was spreading without assistance. Owing to its reputation, the mummy-board came to be suspected of causing disasters of even greater magnitude; hence reverses suffered by British forces during the First World War were also attributed to the malign influence of the 'priestess'.

The stories outlined here are a mixture of pure invention, unsubstantiated rumour and misunderstanding. Although proof of the mummy-board's alleged supernatural powers is completely lacking the mythology surrounding it has evolved over the past hundred years and continues to acquire new dimensions.
