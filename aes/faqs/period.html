Kings, Periods and Dynasties

Pharaonic civilisation lasted for more than three thousand years; to study it accurately and in detail it is necessary to divide this great span of time into smaller periods. Egyptian history has traditionally been characterised by periods of great achievement ('Kingdoms') separated by periods of disunity ('intermediate periods').

The other characteristic of Egyptian history is the division of it into dynasties. Such a division can be traced back to Manetho, an Egyptian priest who wrote a history of Egypt in Greek in the 3rd century BC. The original of his text has not survived, although most of it has been transmitted down through excepts included in the works of later authors. Manetho presumably considered his dynasties as each indicative of a particular ruling family, but it is quite clear from modern research that this is not always the case. For example, the last king of the 17th dynasty and the first of the 18th were probably brothers, while there are no clear family breaks from the 1st to the end of the 5th dynasties. Thus Manetho's division probably represent some family lines, but in other cases a change of dynasty might indicate the beginning of an important era, or simply a convenience brought about by a change of column in the original from which he was working. One such ancient king list has survived on papyrus; other king lists are known from temples and tombs. One of the latter, that in the temple of Ramesses II at Abydos, is now in the British Museum.

The principal periods are:

  • Early Dynastic Period
  • Old Kingdom ('Early Bronze Age')
  • First Intermediate Period
  • Middle Kingdom ('Middle Bronze Age')
  • Second Intermediate Period
  • New Kingdom ('Late Bronze Age')
  • Third Intermediate Period
  • Late Period ('Early Iron Age')

The Late Period ends with two periods of Persian domination separated by a last sixty years of independence 404-343 BC.

In 332 BC Alexander the Great absorbed Egypt along with the other provinces of the Persian Empire into his realm.

After his death his generals divided the Empire between them, with the general Ptolemy becoming ruler of Egypt, and from 303 BC officially king. The last descendant of Ptolemaic to rule Egypt was Cleopatra VII with Caesarian, her son by Julius Caesar. At her suicide in 30 BC Egypt became a province of the Roman Empire. In AD 641-2 the Arabs conquered it, bringing it into the Islamic world.
