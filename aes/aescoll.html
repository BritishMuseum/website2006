A brief history of the Egyptian Collections

Egyptian objects have formed part of the collections of the British Museum since its beginning. The original start of the Museum was to provide a home for the objects left to the nation by Sir Hans Sloane when he died in 1753, about 150 of which were from Egypt. During the rest of the 18th century, a small number of other objects came into the Egyptian collections.

European interest in Egypt began to grow in earnest after the invasion of Napoleon Bonaparte in 1798, particularly since Napoleon included scholars in his expedition who were to record a great deal about the country. After the British defeated the French in 1801, a number of antiquities which the French had collected were confiscated by the army, and were presented to the Museum in the name of George III in 1803. The most famous of these was of course the Rosetta Stone.

Egypt then came under the control of Mohamed Aly, who was determined to open the country up to foreigners. One of the side-effects of this was that foreign consuls began to form collections of antiquities. Britain's consul was Henry Salt, who amassed a collection of antiquities, and whose agent was Belzoni. Belzoni was responsible for bringing the statue known as the 'Younger Memnon' back to Britain. Salt's two collections formed an important core of the collection, and was supplemented by the purchase of a number of papyri in the following years. By 1866 the collection consisted of some 10,000 objects.

Antiquities from excavations started to come to the Museum in the later 19th century as a result of the work of the Egypt Exploration Fund (now Society). The major source of antiquities came from the efforts of E.A. Wallis Budge (Keeper 1886 -1924), who regularly visited Egypt and built up a wide-ranging collection of papyri and funerary material. The collection stood at about 57,000 objects at Budge's retirement.

Many smaller pieces have come to the collection since the Second World War. The collection presently stands in the region of 80,000 objects, and objects are still donated to the Museum, and purchased where they would fill obvious gaps in the overall collection.

A more detailed account of the development of the collections, written by T.G.H. James, former Keeper of the department, will be found here.
