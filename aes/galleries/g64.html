The Raymond and Beverly Sackler Gallery of Early Egypt
Room 64
Free admission

The exhibition in this gallery illustrates the development of early civilisation in Egypt, from the Stone Age down to 2500 BC. Following an extremely long period during which life changed little for the early hunter-gathering peoples of the Egyptian desert, the introduction of agriculture from the fifth millennium BC by settlers in the valley of the river Nile created the conditions for rapid advances in technology and social organisation. These farming communities which existed before the unification of Egypt into a single State are known as the Predynastic cultures, which passed through a series of stages distinguished by characteristic styles of pottery and small objects. The typical products of these phases are displayed in this gallery. They show increasing sophistication over time, particularly towards the end of the Predynastic Period in around 3100 BC. The majority of objects have been found with burials, which were made in simple desert graves like the one illustrated in Case 15.

The unification of Egypt under a single king occurred in about 3000 BC, the first ruler of the united land and founder of the First Dynasty being King Narmer from southern Egypt. Objects from this period, including some fine decorated palettes with scenes of hunting and conflict, are exhibited in Case 6. The stability brought about by unification led to even more rapid development in the production of fine goods, including stone vases, metal tools and vessels and ivory carvings. The use of writing, first developed during the period of unification, now became increasingly common. Many of the finest objects came from the tombs of the kings and their high officials and examples are shown in Cases 10, 11 and 12. The increasing wealth of the land and strong central control in the early Old Kingdom led to dramatic achievements in architecture, with the building of the first royal pyramid-tomb in 2680 BC (Case 13) and the construction of the Great Pyramid for King Khufu only 100 years later (Case 14).
