The Raymond and Beverly Sackler Gallery of Egypt and Africa
Room 65
Free admission

This gallery traces the history of Egypt's connections with the ancient cultures of north-east Africa, in particular those of Nubia. This region (now located within modern Sudan) was formerly of great economic importance; the pharaohs obtained huge quantities of gold from Nubian mines, and the area also served as a commercial 'corridor' to sub-Saharan Africa. The ivory, incense, rare animals, slaves and other much-prized luxury goods which passed overland and along the Nile made the region a target for Egyptian ambitions. Control of the area passed repeatedly between the local rulers and the Egyptian kings.

The display illustrates the material culture of the indigenous Nubians from the fourth millennium BC to the medieval period. Monumental sculptures from Egyptian settlements and fortresses throw light on the pharaonic domination of the region, particularly during the New Kingdom (c. 1550-1070 BC), and there is a range of attractive ceramics, metalwork and sculpture from the Kushite kingdom which flourished in the last centuries BC and the early centuries AD. These monuments reveal an unusual integration of Egyptian and Nubian cultural traits, and several objects are inscribed in Meroitic, the indigenous language of Kush, which still defies translation.
