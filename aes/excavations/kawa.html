Kawa: Survey and Excavation on the Sudanese Nile
Page 1 of 3

Compared with Egypt archaeology in Sudan is still in its infancy. Although Sudan's archaeological heritage rivals that of its northern neighbour it boasts few of the grandiose and extremely well preserved monuments such as those constructed by the pharaohs of ancient Egypt. Today there is increasing interest in Sudan and its pivotal role as the zone of contact between the civilisations of the Mediterranean Basin and the Levant and those of Central Africa is attracting considerable attention.

Kawa

Dating from this period of climatic stress is the ancient town of Kawa which lies on the right bank of the River Nile 100km upstream of the Third Cataract in the Dongola Reach. The settlement occupies, or has formed, a prominent mound covering approximately 40 hectares. It is one of the largest and most important archaeological sites in Sudan with a long history spanning at least from the 14th century BC into the 4th century AD. However it stands in an even more ancient landscape. Neolithic pottery is scattered in the plain immediately to the east while a large cemetery of the Kerma Kingdom (2500 - 1500 BC) lies a little to the north east.

The ruins have been a focus of British interest during three centuries; the earliest recorded excavations being those conducted by Colonel Colthorne and his troops, members of the Gordon Relief Expedition, in 1885. They uncovered, during the course of a days digging a small temple with painted walls but the advent of the all too frequent sandstorms over the next two days filled in the efforts of their labours, and they abandoned the enterprise.

Major excavations were undertaken at Kawa by the Oxford Excavations in Nubia expedition led by Professor Francis Llewellyn Griffith from 1929-31 and, after his death, by M. F. Laming Macadam and Laurence Kirwan in the winter of 1935-6. Although on a large scale, much of the site remains to be investigated and at no point were the earliest deposits reached. The oldest artefacts found were of the Kerma period but no structural evidence of that date was revealed. Kawa lay within the area conquered by the Egyptian pharaohs of the XVIIIth dynasty, particularly through the efforts of Thutmose I and Thutmose III in the early to mid 15th century BC. Epigraphic evidence indicates that the Egyptian town at Kawa was called Gematon `The Aten is Perceived' and this strongly suggests that a settlement was founded (or re-founded) here later in the New Kingdom, either in the latter part of the reign of Amenhotpe III, or in that of his son Amenhotpe IV better known as Akhenaten, that is in the mid 14th century BC. Griffith's team excavated the well-preserved remains of a stone temple (Temple A) built by, or possibly completed by, Tutankhamun.

Temple A remained in use for many centuries and inscriptions were found within it of a number of later XIXth and XXth Dynasty pharaohs including the most famous of them all, Ramesses II. The latest attested Egyptian pharaoh at Kawa is Ramesses VI (1156 - 1149 BC). On the collapse of Egyptian control of the region, a process completed by the earlier 11th century BC, the fate of the town is unknown. It is next referred to on an inscription of the early 7th century BC which however refers to the earliest ruler of the Kingdom of Kush known to us by name, Alara (early 8th century BC), and implies that there was probably a functioning Temple of Amun there at that time.

 

 


The Temple of Amun at Kawa built by King Taharqo between 684 and 680 BC, during its excavation by the Oxford Excavation Committee in 1935/6 (RAF photograph courtesy of the Institute of Archaeology, London).

The most famous of the Kushite kings, Taharqo, who is mentioned in the Bible, passed through Kawa in 690 BC on his way to Egypt and was appalled at the state of a temple there. He ordered the reconstruction of that building and, in 684 BC, began the construction of a large temple to the principal god of the Kushite pantheon, Amun (Temple T) which was completed in four years by a team of architects and masons brought from Memphis especially for the task (Plate 1). At that time Kush was the only power in the Nile Valley, the rule of its king stretching from Central Sudan to the Mediterranean. Kawa was a nome capital, a regional capital, and held a prominent place in the ritualised demonstration of royal power of the Kushite kings. A number of inscriptions, including that of King Irike-Amanote (c. 431-405 BC) carved on the walls of the Temple of Amun, record that the town was one of several sites visited by the new Kushite monarchs. At each of these the king's divine mandate to rule was confirmed by the local god, here Amun of Gematon, depicted as a ram-headed god with the horizontally twisted horns of the sheep species ovis longipes palaeoaegyptiaca.
