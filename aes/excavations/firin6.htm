Kom Firin

A fortified enclosure


Magnetometry survey in south-east part of Kom Firin. The fortified enclosure and its northern gateway is clearly visible, with the temple within, slightly off-centre. The western and southern stretches of the enclosure wall have largely been destroyed.

In September 2004, the magnetometry survey was extended outwards from the temple area, in a bid to identify the position and size of the temple enclosure. The survey data clearly reveals the presence of a massive enclosure wall (5m thick), delimiting an area of 220x200m. Selective excavation trenches confirmed this feature was a mud-brick wall with exterior gypsum plaster lining.

In the north-west and north-eastern, fortifying bastions of 10x10m are evident at the external corners. The northern stretch of the wall is pierced by a monumental gateway, consisting of two towers measuring approximately 18x8m. A test trench revealed the inner face of one of the towers, associated with a mass of limestone chippings, some with worked surfaces, which may hint at an original decorated limestone element in this gateway.

Outer edge of eastern stretch of enclosure wall, with gypsum plaster facing.

At present, ceramic evidence does not provide a clear date for the fortified enclosure wall. However, the brick size, construction method and alignment are identical to that in the temple itself, suggesting a Ramesside date.

It seems likely that this large fortified enclosure, with a modest temple set within it, alongside other smaller-scale structures, represents one of the line of Ramesside installations designed to guard against the increased threat from the Libyan desert. Texts from the reigns of Merenptah and Ramses III refer to several of these forts. Thus far, only one had been identified archaeologically, at the site of Zawyet Umm el-Rakham on Egypt's north-western coast. Excavations at this site, still ongoing, suggest an enclosure wall of similar size, with narrow gateway, external corner bastions, and a small temple within.

 

Future seasons of fieldwork at Kom Firin should further clarify the nature of this fortified enclosure, the smaller structures within, and the development of the town throughout the subsequent centuries.
