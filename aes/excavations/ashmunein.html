Egypt and the el-Ashmunein Excavation 1980-1990
Page 1 of 5

The Museum's direct involvement in work in Egypt recommenced in 1980, with the beginning of excavations at the site of El-Ashmunein in Middle Egypt.

This site is marked by mounds of crumbling mud-brick ruins and destroyed stone temples, the surviving elements of the great Pharaonic city of Khmnun, redeveloped in Roman times as Hermopolis Magna.

Ten two-month seasons of excavation were directed by Jeffrey Spencer of this Department.

Among the discoveries were: 

  • Remains of temples from the New Kingdom and later
  • Over 300 pieces from a monumental stela of King Osorkon III 
  • A calcite altar inscribed for King Amenhotep III 
  • A major processional street of the city known anciently as the 'Dromos of Hermes' 


The later seasons revealed mud-brick houses of the Third Intermediate Period and part of a cemetery of the early Middle Kingdom.

Much of the site relates to the Graeco-Roman period. During the project, Donald Bailey of the Greek and Roman Department excavated and recorded monuments of Roman Hermopolis.

This excavation has been fully published.
