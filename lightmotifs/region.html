Lantern processions in northern Honshu

The north of the main island of Honshu in Japan is famous for its summer festivals in early August each year and particularly for their characteristic giant lantern floats, usually referred to as nebuta or neputa. These festivals attract several million visitors to the region and are the focus of cultural politics as towns and cities emphasise the distinct artistic styles of their festivals. Although most people in Japan have heard of the festivals of Aomori and Hirosaki, other local towns such as Kuroishi, Goshogawara and Ominato also have procession of giant lanterns at this time of year.

Hirosaki is a castle town known for its neputa, lantern floats in the shape of fans rising from a square base on which they sometimes rotate. The fan shape provides two large surfaces which are used as a canvas by renowned local artists. Each side has a different style of painting: the front generally shows a fierce battle scene taken from historical records or famous Japanese tales while the back is characterised by the portrayal of a beautiful woman often framed by scenes of death. The floats are some 10-15 metres high with articulated flaps at the top that are lowered to pass under street signs and electrical cables. The procession of over sixty floats is slower than in Aomori: there is no dancing and the music also has a slower rythm. According to local interpretation, the Hirosaki Neputa Festival is about soldiers going off to war and generally has a more sombre mood, while the Aomori Nebuta Festival celebrates their boisterous and victorious return from battle.

Gigantism is taken to its extreme in Goshogawara where the floats on parade can be between 25 and 30 metres high. Far fewer are on parade (five in 2000) than in the festival processions of other nearby towns. Recently revived, the festival in Goshogawara has fewer resources and the same floats may be paraded for two or three years in a row. As opposed to the flat forms of the Hirosaki neputa, Goshogawara's tachi-nebuta ('standing nebuta') are made with construction methods similar to those used in Aomori City. The main differences with the Aomori nebuta is that they usually portray one character standing upright on an elaborate narrow tiered platform and that the construction is in parts that are assembled to create the towering form.

 

Ominato is a port on the Shimokita peninsula which stretches north east around the large bay forming the northern tip of the main island of Honshu in Japan. Here, the nebuta floats are three-dimensional scenes similar to those made in Aomori City, but smaller in size. The narrow street that follows the shore line is the main procession route and, for most of the way, the floats are paraded sideways to the great pleasure of the crowds sitting outside the houses. Where the streets widen and at cross-roads, the floats are made to turn to give life to the dynamic scenes portrayed.

Drums, flutes and cymbals are played in all these festivals, with a group of musicians following or preceding each float. In Hirosaki and Aomori, the same tune is played in the procession, but at a different pace. In the procession itself in Aomori, although all groups play the same tune with a similar rythm, they each have their own tune which is played once they follow the float outside the procession route and when competing on stage.

Not all processions include dancers. The nebuta festival in Aomori is characterised by a lively and energetic skip which all costumed participants, men, women and children, are welcome to join. In Ominato, dancers are women and young girls, wearing a distinct costume identifying the neighbourhood (and, therefore, the float) to which they belong and the step is a formal choreography.
