The nebuta artists in Aomori have usually trained from a young age with previous generations of craftsmen. The lead artist working at The British Museum is Takashi Kitamura. He was ten years old when he first started learning about nebuta construction.

His team of assistants includes his twin brother, Akira Kitamura, also a nebuta artist, as well as Maki Fujiwara, Tomoko Kitamura, Reiko Kitamura, Miyuki Oda, Tokuko Iwasaki and Keiko Osanai. The electrical engineer is Katsutoshi Funahashi, assisted by Michiko Funahashi.

Maximilian Marshall is a British lantern artist who regularly contributes to the Thames Festival. He has joined the team to work alongside Takashi Kitamura and deepen his understanding of methods and ideas used in the construction of festival floats from Aomori City.

Biographical notes

Takashi Kitamura was born in 1948 in the region of Aomori. He and his twin brother, Akira, have been involved in the construction of nebuta floats since they were children and later training under Keizo Kitagawa. Like most neputa artists, this is not his principal activity. He is a plasterer and decorator by trade, but creating nebuta floats is crucial to his sense of being. His comments illustrate a dynamic relation to his art: 'exploring new forms and techniques is crucial for the protection of tradition'. Takashi Kitamura regularly wins prizes for his floats in the Nebuta Festival. In 2001, one of his floats won the overall First Prize and the other came third. He was also awarded the newly re-instated prize for Best Nebuta Artist.

 

 
Akira Kitamura is working with his twin brother Takashi Kitamura on the construction of the nebuta for The British Museum. He too was taught by Kitagawa Keizo from an early age and, although the two brothers do collaborate in Japan, he is an independent artist with his own commissions for floats that parade in the Nebuta Festival. For him, the most important element of a nebuta is composition. But more than an artistic expression, Akira Kitamura draws his purpose in life from his work on the nebuta. His float on the theme of attempt by the Mongols to invade Japan in the 13th century, depicting a battle scene on a boat, won him the Fourth Prize in the 2001 Nebuta Festival.

 

Maki Fujiwara, 38 years old, is cousin to Takashi and Akira Kitamura (her father and their mother were brother and sister) and was invited to join the Nebuta Team to come to The British Museum. She is an independent glass artist who trained at the Toyama Institute of Glass (Japan) and the Penland School of Crafts (USA) and has her own studio in Aomori since 1994. Her appreciation of three-dimensional art forms, such as nebuta construction, is heightened by a sense of belonging to her place of origin, Aomori: 'people from the north [of Japan] are seen to be closed because of the harsh winters. The festivals in summer-time are very important: they are an explosion of life and an outlet for energetic expression.'

 

 

Katsutoshi Funahashi was born in 1942 and has been an electrical engineer for over forty years. His involvement in the lighting of nebuta floats began three years ago and since then he has worked with a number of different artists, including Takashi Kitamura. His role requires close collaboration with the artist designing the float to ensure that the luminosity of the float conforms with the desired overall effect. The most important aspect of his work is to ensure the nebuta is evenly lit from the inside. This involves a careful balance between placing the light bulbs at a distance from the paper covering the float while ensuring the complex inner structure of the float does not cast shadows onto the paper surface.

 

Michiko Funahashi was born in 1942. She and her husband, Katsutoshi Funahashi, have set up a thriving business in electrical engineering and have worked together for over thirty years. She started assisting him on the lighting of nebuta floats three years ago. To her, the most important aspect of her work is attention to detail and ensuring that the complex wiring is conform to electrical norms.

 

  

Maximilian Marshall, 27 years old, is the only British member of the Nebuta Team. He studied print-making at the Southampton Institute and is now a London-based artist. In recent years, he has been exploring lantern forms and has contributed regularly to the Thames Festival, both as an independent artist and through leading workshops with local schools and community groups. His interest in the construction of the nebuta at The British Museum lead to a visit to northern Japan in the summer of 2001 to research festival floats. The fan-shaped neputa floats in Hirosaki were the inspiration behind the float he made for this year's Thames Festival.

 

 

Tomoko Kitamura is a key person in the Nebuta Team. She started papering after she got married 24 years ago to Takashi Kitamura. As the wife of the lead artist on this particular float, she is to a large extent responsible for organising the team's work and particularly that of the women who cover the wire structure with paper. She is interested in the way in which the team alters over the years: when she first started, she was the youngest member of the team, but at 45 she is now one of the older leaders and welcomes and trains new members.

 

 

Reiko Kitamura (50 years old) joined in the work on the nebuta a few years after her marriage with Akira Kitamura. The most interesting aspect for her of the papering stage is that each space in the wire structure, and, therefore, each piece of paper is a different size and different shape, making short cuts impossible. The work is measured and paced: ‘one forgets time is passing' while working. After 18 years of experience, she describes it as having become a ‘natural process'.

 

 

Miyuki Oda's interest in nebuta matters started as a haneto (dancer) in the festival procession, but she now also has nine years experience in decorating the floats on Takashi Kitamura's team. She was first introduced to this work through her friendship with Tomoko Kitamura. The most interesting process for her is the application of paraffin wax and colours to the completed structure as this is what will largely determine the overall impact of the float in the procession.

 

Tokuko Iwasaki (38 years old) was first introduced to working on the nebuta seven years ago by friends of hers who knew the Kitamura family. Contributing to the overall impression the float will make on the audience is one of the most interesting aspects of her work. She also describes the nature of the work during the papering stage as addictive: there is alway another piece of wire to cover. 'Once we get started, we can't stop'.

 

 
Keiko Osanai was introduced by friends of hers and this was originally her main reason for joining the Kitamura family team. Since she started six years ago at the age of 41, she has been particularly interested in the process of learning and the progress that is made over the years through increasing experience. The first two years are perhaps the most difficult for learning to apply the paper to the wire structure, but 'little by little one is allowed to more'.
