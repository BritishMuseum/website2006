Why is the numeral IIII rather than IV commonly used on clock dials?
This has been a subject debated for many years and two theories exist. The first is that it was simply used for aesthetic reasons, the argument being that the numeral IIII would balance far better with the heavy figures of VIII and XII on the opposite side and the top of the dial.

The second is that the use of the numeral IIII on clock dials in the medieval period was simply following a common practice of the period when clocks began to be made. There is ample evidence from the Roman Empire from the time of Julius Caesar that the Romans themselves used both IV and IIII numerals, as well as XIX and XVIIII for 19. It therefore seems likely that the use of the numeral four in the form of IIII simply grew from its use on sundials in the period preceding the invention of the mechanical clock.

In clock making, there are exceptions where the Roman IV is used. Firstly on a group of clocks made in London in the late 17th century which had a system known as 'Roman Striking' in which two bells were used. A low bell sounded the fives and a high bell sounded the units. The popularity of these clocks was short lived. The other notable exception is the Westminster Great Clock, popularly known as Big Ben, whose dial was designed in the Gothic style with a classical Roman IV at four o’clock.

When was the earliest clock made?
Nothing is known of the date, place or inventors of the earliest mechanical clocks. However, there are documentary references to clocks in Europe in the last quarter of the 13th century. In 1271 Robert the Englishman wrote a commentary on Johannes de Sacrobosco’s Tractatus de Sphera Mundi, in which he stated that clockmakers had not found a method by which a wheel would make one revolution for every one of the equinoctial circle. In about 1275 Albrecht, the author of Juengeren Titurel described a clock which showed astronomical indications. The oldest surviving clock is thought to be in Salisbury Cathedral and dates from about 1386.

When were the earliest watches made?
The watch has its origins at the beginning of the 16th century. By the end of the 15th century, portable clocks were becoming relatively common following recent advances in the making of small coiled springs strong enough to drive the small light movements. By simply miniaturising the movement and lightening the case, as well as providing it with a cover to protect the dial and a pendant and ring so that it could be carried on the person, the watch came into being. At this time, watches were expensive luxury items usually worn on a chain or ribbon around the neck, functioning as items of status rather than timekeeping machines.

When were the first wrist watches made?
A list of gifts offered to Queen Elizabeth I in 1572 included 'One armlett or shakell of golde, all over fairely garnishedd with rubyes and dyamondes, haveing in the closeing thereof a clocke'; it would be possible to consider this as a forerunner of the wristwatch. The wristwatch that we are familiar with today gradually developed at the end of the nineteenth century. At first they were little more than pocket watches worn on the wrist, but eventually strap-lugs were applied and the dial turned through ninety degrees from the winding button to bring it upright to the wearer.

When were spring driven clocks first made?
One of the earliest references to a spring driven clock is contained in the miniature illumination of a French translation of Henrich Seuse’s Horologium Sapientiae dated around 1450. This shows eight different devices used for indicating and keeping the time. On a table are the most recent technological developments and among these is a depiction of a table clock with the cover removed to clearly show a spring drive coupled with a fusee.

When was the pendulum invented?
Drawings made by Leonardo da Vinci (1452-1519) show that he considered the application of the pendulum to clocks. The drawings appear in the Codice Atlantico and the Ravaisson-Mollen manuscripts of 1493-4. Credit should also be given to the famous astronomer Galileo Galilei (1564-1642) who did much work with pendulums . In 1641, by then blind, Galileo imparted the idea of a pendulum clock to his son Vincenzio who failed to complete the invention before his death in 1649.

It was not until 1656 that a pendulum was successfully applied to a clock by the Dutch astronomer, mathematician and horologist Christian Huygens (1629-95), thereby transforming the accuracy of contemporary clock making from parts of an hour to seconds per day.

When was the balance spring invented?
A balance spring evens out the period of oscillation of a balance wheel in a watch thereby dramatically increasing its its accuracy and efficiency of operation. It was invented in 1675 by an Englishman named Robert Hooke (1635-1703) who lived in London.
