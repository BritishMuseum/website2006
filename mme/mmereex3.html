Rescue excavations at Deir 'Ain 'Abata, Jordan, were begun in 1988 with the support of the Department of Medieval and Modern Europe. The project, which continued until 1996, resulted in the discovery of a monastic complex dating to the Early Byzantine period, c.5th-7th centuries AD. A triple-apsed basilica church was uncovered which was built over a natural cave. Early Christians believed that this was where Lot and his daughters took refuge after the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah (Genesis 19). The church was adorned with four mosaic pavements, three of which were inscribed in Greek and two of which were dated April 605 and May 691 respectively. Three other inscriptions on stone blocks invoked St Lot, confirming the identification of the site. The church was flanked by a large, 8-metre deep reservoir to the south, and a refectory and hostelry to the north. Early and Middle Bronze Age tombs, and Hellenistic/Roman finds which were uncovered may allude to a pre-Christian veneration of the site.

Post excavation work which began in 1993 with support of the Department of Medieval and Modern Europe is now complete and will be published by the British Museum Press in 2001. A selection of the site archive is held at the British Museum - for further information please contact the Departmental Study Room.

In 1995 the site was proclaimed an official holy site by His Majesty the Late King Hussein. Since then the Ministry of Tourism and Antiquities of Jordan has conducted conservation of the ancient structures and tourism development which includes an on-site museum. The British Foreign and Commonwealth Office donated £20,000 towards the preservation of the site.
