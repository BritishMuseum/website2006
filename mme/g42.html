The Medieval Gallery

The Medieval Gallery
Objects from western Europe and the Byzantine world from the period c.800 - c.1500 (with the exception of some icons from later periods) are arranged in roughly chronological order with cases devoted to Carolingian and Ottonian art, Romanesque and Gothic metalwork and ivory, medieval coinage, medieval jewellery, popular religion, secular life and arms and armour.  The displays include examples of panel painting, alabaster carvings, leather work, some tiles (see also room 43) and some  scientific instruments (see also room 44).

Spectacular loans-in

The Kingston Lacy Screen
The screen is an extremely important survival of medieval panel painting.  Dating from the early to mid 14th century, it depicts two kings in conversation with two bishops.  All of them are likely to be saints.  One of the kings holds a large ring, denoting St. Edward the Confessor, while the other appears to hold an arrow, the symbol of St Edmund.  Although a nineteenth century label on the reverse mentions it as coming from the parish church of Heyford in Northamptonshire, its earliest history remains unknown.  The screen has suffered from vandalism during the years of the Reformation and from damage due to subsequent neglect.  It now lacks the vibrancy of its original colour scheme but the high quality of the work is still evident and this, along with the regal subject matter, may suggest a high-status patron.  On loan from The National Trust

The All Souls Jewel
A gold jewel in the form of a flower decorated with white ronde bosse enamelled petals and set with a tourmaline.  Although little is known of  the history of this rare jewel, documentary evidence shows that it belonged to All Souls College from  as early as 1466 - 94 during the time of the fifth Warden, John Stokes.   English or French, 15th century. On loan from the Warden and Fellows of All Souls College, Oxford.

The Lacock Cup
A rare survival of a piece of English, medieval, secular plate dating from about 1430 which was later re-used as a chalice. On loan from the Vicar and Parochial Church Council of Lacock, Wiltshire.
