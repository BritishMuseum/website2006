Department of Medieval and Modern Europe > research > excavations > Russia

Excavations at Novgorod in northern Russia conducted between 1996-99 were co-directed by the Department of Medieval and Modern Europe in collaboration with Bournemouth University and Institute of Archaeology, University College London.

Novgorod is the largest medieval city north of the Alps and served as one of the four commercial offices (Kontore) of the Hanseatic League from the 14th to early 16th centuries. It stood on the cross-roads of European trade with Asia and was the principal source of pitch (for the caulking of ships) and pelts for medieval Europe. Its domestic material culture was rich and cosmopolitan in character with influences through trade from the Christian west, Orthodox East and from Islam.

The British Museum and London University team has been funded by the EU agency INTAS (an international association for the promotion of co-operation with scientists from the new independent states of the former Soviet Union) to assist Russian archaeologists in the development of new approaches and techniques in deeply stratified urban sites.

The results of post-excavation research were published in a British Museum Occasional Paper in 2001.
