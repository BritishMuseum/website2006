Grete Marks, artist potter: ceramics of the interwar years
until 30 August
Room 48
free admission

This small display is dedicated to the ceramics of Grete Marks, made at her factory between 1923 and 1934.

Born into an educated Jewish family, Marks (née Heymann, 1899-1990) studied at the Bauhaus from 1920-21. In 1923 she and her first husband, Gustav Loebenstein, set up the Haël Werkstätten near Berlin. She soon achieved international renown. With her use of bold shapes, dynamic decoration and subtle glazes she brought the individuality of studio pottery into a factory context.

But in 1934 Marks was forced to sell the factory. The Nazi authorities slandered her pottery in a malicious article in the propagandist paper, Der Angriff, on 'Jewish ceramics in the chamber of horrors'. The article unfavourably contrasted Haël products with those made at the same factory under new ownership.

In 1936 Grete left for England. She designed for various factories in Stoke-on-Trent, and in 1938 set up her own 'Greta Pottery' with her second husband, Harold Marks. But she never regained her former prominence, partly because her work was so different from English factory production at the time. Most of the pieces shown here were given by her husband and daughter, Harold and Frances Marks.
