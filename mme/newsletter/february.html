As part of London’s Africa 05 celebrations, the British Museum presents a dedicated series of new exhibitions, events and films exploring the dynamic heritage of Africa past and present – from the 2 million year old hand tools found in the Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania, to unique masterpieces of contemporary African art.

The influence of Africa past and present on the cultures of the world is enormous and this is uniquely visible in the collections of the British Museum. You can begin to explore this online using COMPASS, the Museum’s digital collections resource.

You can also book tickets on the web for special events.

To find out more just hit the links or enter our main site.

  MADE IN AFRICA

The Asahi Shimbun Displays
27 January - 3 April, Admission free

Made in Africa takes us to the very beginning of an extraordinary journey which started 2 million years ago in the Olduvai Gorge in Tanzania. The hand tools found there announce the unfolding of human history as we know it, and from these African beginnings spring the heritage of the world.

 
 
Chopping tools from Olduvai forge Tanzania, about 1.8 million years old

' These are amazing pieces. They have great meaning to me personally because this is how it came to be known that human history started in Africa. What I mainly like are the colours and shapes, and to acknowledge that these were used in place of today’s knives, axes, shovels and forks as cutting tools.'

COLLEEN DOWDIE, KINGSTON, JAMAICA

 3 February - 28 August

Made with decommissioned weapons from Mozambique’s civil war, the Tree of Life sculpture is one of the centrepieces of the Africa 05 season. Part of the Transforming Arms into Tools programme, funded by Christian Aid and the British Museum.

Waiting for Happiness

MARCH - APRIL 2005

The British Museum hosts a rare short season of contemporary African cinema and documentary including Waiting for Happiness, winner of the Grand Prize at FESPASCO (Africa’s most important film festival) and Abouna, a modern-day tragedy about childhood dislocation in Chad.

 HOW CAN ART BUILD A NATION?

Friday 11 - Saturday 12 February
Friday, 09.15 - 17.00 Saturday, 09.30 - 12.30
£30, concessions £15

This special two day conference brings some of the most respected
African artists into one space to discuss the state of African art in the context of the Western contemporary art landscape. The sessions are centred on three main themes: identity, collectives and medium. These issues will also be discussed on the Friday evening of the conference for the BBC World Service Debate How can art build a nation?
