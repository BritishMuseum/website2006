Welcome to Mercury,

It's a busy month at the Museum as Views from Africa, a series of exhibitions, discussions and performances exploring unique African stories of encounters with Europe, gets under way.

Meanwhile, for families, technology buffs and avid historians, the extraordinary virtual reality film, Mummy: the inside story, extends its run until 14 August - with half price tickets on Thursday and Friday Lates. Adult Learner's Week begins Sunday 22 May with the Open University drop-in day. And for art lovers with a taste for the Orient, Music of Ink brings together six contemporary artists and musicians to debate the influence of Chinese culture on their work.

Museum forecourt, 09.00-18.00 daily

The Africa Garden is open to the public until September and is the final creation of the  BBC's Ground Force team. New sculptures by artists from Ghana to Mozambique, some with work featured in the Museum's African Galleries, now stand among spectacular flora from three climate zones enabling visitors to consider from different perspectives the African cultures they come from.

Thursday 19 May, 18.00

Every day 6500 Africans die from AIDS in conditions of unacceptable poverty, but they are not alone. Join an essential discussion, hear stories from researchers working around the world and ask: where next for the fight against this most costly disease?

Fabian Cataldo (Goldsmiths College) speaks on universal access to treatment in the fevelas of Rio de Janeiro. Max Carocci (Royal Anthropological Institute) bears witness to the spiritual response to the disease by the Native Americans of San Francisco. Paul Boyce (London School of Economics) reflects on dealing with social stigma in Calcutta.

Writing about Africa
 
Thursday 26 May, 18.30–20.00
 
Writing is always a political act, not least when the subject is Africa. Are we getting a 'real' report in our Western newspapers and publications? In whose hands does the story lie? The issues are debated by a panel of writers including Onyekachi Wambu, Aminatta Forna, Henry Bonsu and Michela Wrong.

From trade to religion, Ancient Civilizations explores global themes of continuing importance in fun, interactive ways.

Discover monumental buildings like the Great Temple of the Aztecs or the Ziggurat of Ur. Compare an Anglo-Saxon ship burial and an ancient Chinese tomb. Part of the British Museum's award-winning online resources, Ancient Civilizations has been specially designed for upper Key Stage 2 and Key Stage 3 children and their teachers.

Chinese calligrapher, Zeng Laide

Saturday 18 June, 14.00

What has been the impact of Chinese classical painting, literature and calligraphy on contemporary art and music?

Six artists from London to Beijing come face to face to consider how the soul, philosophy and ethics of Chinese art and music are being transformed and transmitted today.

Featuring:
Yang Lian (poet), Romesh Gunesekera (writer), Qu Lei Lei and Denis Brown (visual artists), Zeng Laide (calligrapher), Rohan De Saram (cellist)
