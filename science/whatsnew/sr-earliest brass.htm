Europe's Earliest Brass

It is not often that we can report being happy to confirm that a metal really is brass and not gold, without having got the word order mixed up!

This happened recently following the re-examination of the small foils covering the maker's stamps (Figs. 1 & 2, below, left & right) on a fine iron sword found on the foreshore of the Thames at Syon Reach, Isleworth and which came to the British Museum in 1929 (Fig. 3, bottom). For many years the sword was regarded as being Anglo Saxon, and as such it was examined and the foils analysed and identified as being of brass. This passed without comment, as brass, the alloy of copper and zinc, had become common in Europe during the Roman period. But more recent research has shown that the sword definitely belongs to the Iron Age, to a phase known as La Tene II, which is firmly dated to the 3rd or early 2nd centuries BC. This is, of course, long before the Romans arrived in north west Europe, and suddenly the brass of the foils took on a new significance.


Before discussing this, we must explain just a little about brass, and why it is important. Zinc ores are quite widespread, and the metal is now very common, but in antiquity zinc metal was all but unknown, and the alloy brass only appeared long after bronze (the alloy of copper and tin), had been in use. This is because zinc is very reactive and volatile when hot, in fact it is a gas when smelted, such that it has to be condensed in special distillation apparatus, well beyond the skills of the ancients (the process was discovered in India about 1,000 years ago, see British Museum Occasional Paper no 50). However, brass could be created directly by melting copper in a crucible, adding some roasted zinc ore and charcoal, then quickly covering with a lid to stop the zinc escaping. Some of the ore would decompose and some of the zinc thus released would dissolve in the copper. It was crude, but it worked, giving a reasonable brass, and in fact variants of the process were still practised in Britain up to about 150 years ago. The process seems to have begun in the first half of the first millennium BC somewhere in the Near or Middle East, and over the next few centuries there are occasional references to oreichalkos, 'mountain' or latterly 'golden copper', in the Greek literature of those times.

Only a very few actual pieces of early brass have been identified from the Middle East, and none so far from Europe north of the Alps. Hence the importance of the Syon Reach sword.

Now that its significance was understood it was necessary to undertake a little more examination. The small stamps were in remarkably good condition, and they do look very golden. We had to be sure that the foils really were ancient and that they really were of brass and not gold.

The sword itself is in very good condition, typical of many of the finds from the Thames mud and other waterlogged environments, where there is little or no oxygen. Even so there is some iron corrosion on the sword and careful examination with a binocular microscope showed clearly that the corrosion lapped over the edges of the foils, that is, the foils were in place before the corrosion had begun.

The foils themselves were completely uncorroded partly due the environment and partly due to the surrounding iron of the sword acting as a sacrificial anode. Re-analysis by X-ray fluorescence confirmed that the metal is indeed brass, containing approximately 15 to 20% of zinc, and no other metals in significant alloying quantities. The composition is striking similar to that found in some brass coins issued in Anatolia in the first century BC, and suggests that this is possibly where the brass was made.

How the brass got to North West Europe is unknown, but trade between the Mediterranean and Celtic Europe was extensive, and Celtic warriors frequently served as mercenaries in the squabbling states of the Hellenistic world.

One such warrior might have returned from the wars with some of this metal to embellish his sword. He might have been disappointed to learn that it was not gold. But, documenting as it does, the arrival of a new metal into Western Europe, we are delighted to report that the golden foils really are of brass!
