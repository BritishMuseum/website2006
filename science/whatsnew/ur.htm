Hidden Gold discovered in The British Museum

Curators and Scientists got a big surprise when x-raying a group of ‘crushed skulls' from the Great Death Pit at Ur, dating to around 2500BC. Instead of bone fragments, two stunning gold, silver and lapis lazuli head-dresses were revealed, which have drawn little attention since the objects came to the Museum over seventy years ago. One of the x-rays is shown here.

Excavations conducted at the city of Ur, in southern Iraq, by Leonard Woolley in the 1920s uncovered magnificent burials indicating the existence of a high status group within the city's population. Individuals were buried with rich grave goods made from gold, silver, carnelian and lapis lazuli. They were accompanied to their graves by attendants, both men and women. The Great Death Pit was the largest grave and held seventy-four attendants, sacrificed to accompany the main burial. Little was left of the main tomb-chamber, as it had been robbed in antiquity, but the mass human sacrifice that had accompanied this individual to their grave remained intact. Most of the attendants were female and were richly attired. Next to each was a bowl and it has been suggested that the attendants had died by drinking poison.

The two head-dresses had been registered as crushed skulls (with fragments of jewellery), and appeared rather dull, rather shapeless masses with no identifying features. During the excavations Woolley poured wax over objects to hold them together during excavation and transport to London, but this has the effect of completely obscuring the detail, as the wax has since blackened. The objects were x-rayed to try to identify the teeth belonging to the occupants of the grave, which would have indicated their age at the time of death. In fact, there were no teeth, only gold and silver made into fabulous jewellery. The experts are delighted with the find as it will allow them to apply modern techniques to investigate the way that the head-dresses were made. The Museum has other head-dresses of this type but these have been heavily restored, while the re-discovered ones are nearly complete and have not been touched since they came out of the ground. They are therefore closer to their original condition and can thus tell us a great deal about technology 4500 years ago.
