The composition of the Lycurgus Cup

We can get a good idea of what causes a glass to be coloured by analysing it chemically. Chemical analyses of the Lycurgus Cup show that it is similar to most other Roman glass. It consists mostly of silicon dioxide, with some sodium and calcium oxides. It is a soda-lime-silica glass, just like many of our modern windows, jars and bottles.

Chemical composition:
Silicon dioxide in Lycurgus cup = 73%
Silicon dioxide in Modern Glass = 70%
Sodium oxide in Lycurgus cup = 14%
Sodium oxide in Modern Glass = 15%
Calcium oxide in Lycurgus cup = 7%
calcium oxide in Modern Glass = 10%

So why is it coloured? In fact the glass contains very small amounts of gold ( about 40 parts per million) and silver ( about 300 parts per million)

The discovery that glass could be coloured red by adding a small amount of gold powder is often credited to Johann Kunckel, a German glassmaker in the late seventeenth century. Could the Romans have been using gold to colour the Lycurgus Cup, over a thousand years before?
