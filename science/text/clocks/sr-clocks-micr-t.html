Metal Microstructure

We can look at the microstructure of an ancient metal object to find out how it was made. It is usually too small to be seen with the naked eye, but if prepared properly, can be clearly seen with a Microscope.

Metal is cast by heating it until it melts, then pouring it into a mould. As it cools, tiny crystals grow in the liquid and form a special microstructure. The crystals form branching shapes, called dentrites, like those in the photograph. The photograph was taken in a scanning electron microscope and shows an area only half a millimetre across.


If a solid lump of metal is hammered, to shape it into a piece of jewellery for example, the microstructure is deformed. The metal soon becomes hard and can take no more hammering without cracks appearing. To overcome this the metal has to be heated before it can be hammered more. This heating and hammering process is repeated when a goldsmith shapes an object. It causes the dendrites to recrystallise, losing their branching shapes. Heavy hammering causes a banded effect to occur, as shown here.
