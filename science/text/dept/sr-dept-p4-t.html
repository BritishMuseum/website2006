Department of Scientific Research: About the Department

The skills of the early craftsman, his contacts and markets can be brought to light through an understanding of how an object was made, who made it, and where it was produced. For example, pre-Columbian craftsmen in South America used techniques to make jewellery which were quite distinct from those used in Europe. South American people also developed special skills to manufacture their whistling pots. The examination of medieval enamels has demonstrated that old Roman glass was re-used to produce the colours, one thousand years after it was made. The techniques of scientific examination can also be applied to the more recent past. For example, microscopy allows us to see how the clockmakers made use of the advances in steelmaking in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries to improve their clocks and watches. The work of the department also includes the examination of biological material from excavations, to evaluate ancient diets and environments, as well as the assessment of treasure from discoveries throughout England.

It ranges across the collections, and demonstrates the great ingenuity and diversity of human behaviour, as well as underlying common themes.
