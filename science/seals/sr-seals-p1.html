Mesopotamian Cylinder Seals

Cylinder seals were first made in Mesopotamia, modern-day Iraq, soon after 3500 BC. Paper had not been invented, so documents were written in cuneiform (wedge-shaped characters that provide pictorial symbols for words and syllables) on clay tablets which were then wrapped in clay envelopes. The seals were used to make an impression (or 'seal') in the soft clay, to indicate that the message on the tablet was genuine. In the same way, important documents are still sometimes 'sealed' by making an impression in bright red sealing wax. People continued to use cylinder seals to 'seal' ancient documents for about three thousand years. The seal shown here is just over 3 cm long.
