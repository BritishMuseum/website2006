Thermoluminescence

Thermoluminescence, or TL, dating is largely used as a method of determining when pottery was last fired. It is possible because of a property of certain crystaline materials, such as quartz and felspar, which occur naturally in clay. When such crystals are exposed to natural background radiation, from the minute quantites of uranium, pottasium and thorium found in all soils, and from cosmic rays, energy from that radiation is transferred into a crystal lattice and trapped there. If the crystals are then heated, this trapped energy escapes in the form of light. Unfired clay from the soil contains a lot of such energy, built up over geological time, but when pottery is fired all energy escapes, zeroing the TL clock. The trapped energy slowly builds up again, at a rate depending on the amount of radiation in the area and length of time since firing. The energy trapped since firing can subsequently be released and measured in the laboratory and the amount used to calculate the time elapsed since the pottery was last fired.
