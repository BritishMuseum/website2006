Voices of Southern Africa
14 November 2000 - 28 January 2001
Room 35
Admission Free

This November, the British Museum will host a showcase of contemporary art from Southern Africa. The exhibition has been conceived in collaboration with the Gasworks Studio and the Triangle Arts Trust and brings together the work of nine artists: six from South Africa, two from Zimbabwe and one from Mozambique.

The works on display have been produced since the mid-80s to the present, marking a period of intense socio-political struggle, transition and transformation in the region. Mozambique gained independence from Portuguese colonialism in 1975 and played an important role in the fall of (then)Rhodesia's white minority government in 1980. South Africa held its first democratic elections in 1994, following decades of resistance to the apartheid regime. However, all three countries have since experienced internal strife and the path to independence has been far from smooth.

The artwork crosses the boundaries between rural and urban, local and global, defying attempts to easily categorise the continent into cohesive styles and traditions. If they cohere, they do so in momentary flashes, in the overlap of competing histories, politics and market places and in the everyday contact between individuals. Each work may be approached as a glimpse into the biography of individuals, communities and even nations.

Powerful pieces such as Jane Alexander's Harbinger with Street Cadets: Wish, Walk, Long, Loop, play on themes of apocryphal prophesy in the guise of innocence, highlighting the apprehension about the uncertain future of South Africa's young democracy. Reinata Sadhimba's Pregnant woman holding her stomach combines elements of personal memory and experience (she lost six of her eight children to the Liberation War in Mozambique) with social commentary (on drought, poverty and war). Jackson Xidonkani Hlungwani's statue Ngungunyani Warrior combines elements of his local history with Judeo-Christian ideology and the natural world with that of the supernatural. Despite different views of history and politics, parallels nevertheless emerge between the artists particularly in their belief in the ability of art to change individuals and society. Voices of Southern Africa therefore testifies both to despair and to hope.

The Gasworks Studio in London was established by Robert Loder who together with Sir Anthony Caro, initiated the Triangle Workshop for artists in New York in 1982 which was later used as a model for a series of ongoing international workshops and exchange programmes throughout the world, including Africa. In association with the exhibition, and with the generous support of BP, the British Museum has funded three South African artists to take up residencies in London.

About the Artists

Tubayi Dube ( b.1930, Gwanda District, Zimbabwe ) studied sculpture at the Chirodzo Art School in Harare from 1956-7 and then at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts in Copenhagen from 1969-70. He works mostly with 'Lusaka-marble' and local hardwoods to produce predominantly figurative sculpture. He is also an accomplished potter and his work has been exhibited in Africa and abroad, for example at the Commonwealth Institute in London.

Claudette Shreuders ( b.1973, Pretoria, RSA ) studied at the Universities of Stellenbosch and Cape Town. Her portraits are carved from indigenous wood and combine elements of Western religious icons with West African blolo and colon figures. Her work is starkly autobiographical and confronts the complex ambiguity of ( white ) identity politics in South Africa, questioning what it means to be 'African' post-colonialism. Initially her work focussed on people known to the artist, Shreuders' latest work increasingly searches for iconic, archetypal characters. Her work has been widely exhibited and she has participated in residences in London and Kenya.

Noria Mabasa, ( b.1938 Tshigalo, Ramukhumba, RSA ) after a brief period in Soweto in the early 50s, Mabasa returned to the Northern Transvaal and began to incorporate themes from local Domba initiation practice in her clay sculptures. She has always tried to utilise the world around her, often including people she meets or hears about in the media in her work. She has participated in several international exhibitions and workshops including the africa95 exhibition at the Yorkshire Sculpture Park.

Boira Mteki ( b.1946 d.1999, Harare, Zimbabwe ) was one of the founding sculptors at Frank McEwan's National Gallery Workshop. By the early 60s he began carving in hard materials such as limestone and granite, encouraging other artists to move beyond working with conventional soapstone and steatite. Best known for his monumental 'hard-stone' heads which emphasise strength of character, his works have been extensively collected internationally.

Reinata Sadhimba ( b.1945, Mueda District, Northern Mozambique ) participated in the Liberation war of the 60s against Portugal as a carrier of arms for FREMILO. Encouraged by a dream she turned to pottery to help her through this traumatic period. Her pottery incorporates dreams and draws on the narrative Shetani ( spirit ) inspired imagery of Makonde wood sculpture. In the early 80s she began to incorporate beadwork and basketry into her pottery, which became increasingly figurative. Her work has been exhibited across Africa and as part of the africa95 programme in England.

Jane Alexander ( b. 1959, Johannesburg, RSA ) and studied Fine Art at the University of the Witwatersrand. She is currently a senior lecturer at the University of Cape Town. From the 80s, her haunting apocalyptic photomontages and life-size figures have powerfully reflected the violence and paradoxical relations of an emerging nation. Her surreal 'corpses' are built up from plaster on metal frameworks, their pallid 'skin' tinted with oil colour and embedded with objects she has found ( animal bone, horn, lace etc ).

Jackson Xidonkani Hlungwani ( b.1923,Nkanyani, Gazankulu, RSA ) was taught the art of woodcarving by his father. Ordained in 1946, Jackson has spent the last 40 years building a stone palace to God, following a series of revelatory dreams. This sanctuary, New Jerusalem, is where the artist preaches and sculpts. By the late 80s Jackson was a household name, using the sale of his works as a means to spread his vision to the cities. He is about to embark on a new project, 'the church for women' which along with 'New Jerusalem' aims to unify all nations, both black and white.

Johannes Maswanganyi ( b.1948, Msengi, Gazankulu, RSA ) learnt his trade by carving household objects. By the mid 60s he was making figurative shaped medicine containers, in keeping with Tsonga carving practice, many of which are decoratively carved by burning into the surface of the wood. He is also known for his high-gloss painted carved caricatures of religious and media personalities and politicians, from Christ to Nelson Mandela.

Paul Tavhana ( b.1930, Dzanani, Northern Province, RSA ) began sculpting in 1953 at the Siloam Hospital. He later studied carpentry in Pretoria and since the 1970s he has worked on commissions for the ( then ) Venda Government to carve drums, gun and snake staffs.
