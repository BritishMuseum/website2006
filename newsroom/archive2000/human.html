Human Image
Joseph Hotung Great Court Gallery
7 December 2000 - 11 February 2001
Admission free

The Joseph Hotung Great Court Gallery, a major new space for the Museum's special exhibitions, will be inaugurated by a spectacular thematic introduction to the whole range of the Museum's collections based around representations of the human face and body. Over 100 objects drawn from each of the ten curatorial departments will be displayed alongside one another for the very first time, deliberately contrasted and juxtaposed to stimulate thoughts about the intentions of their makers and the use to which the images were put. The people and cultures represented will range over 14,000 years, from the Upper Palaeolithic of North West Europe to ancient Egypt and the Near East; to Benin, contemporary Africa, the Maya and the Aztecs, early Britain and the Mediterranean, China, India, Australia, Jamaica and Japan.

Life-size sculpture drawn from across world religions and cultures forms a focal point of the exhibition, dating from a modelled clay head of 8000 BC from ancient Jericho to modern portrait sculpture by Elisabeth Frink and contemporary work by the Nigerian artist Sokari Douglas Camp. These full-scale works will be displayed against a backdrop of other media where the human face or figure is depicted, including ceramics, bronzes, hanging scrolls, banknotes, recent hand-held sculpture, and drawings by Leonardo, Michelangelo, Rembrandt, Picasso and Matisse as well as the Inuit artist, Parr. A colossal bronze sculpture by the contemporary European artist Igor Mitoraj will also be included. The first exhibition to examine representations of the human form on such an ambitious scale, Human Image will set out to challenge received understandings and to provoke debate surrounding the role and purpose of the contemporary museum exhibition. No single linear narrative will be presented. Instead the show will be organised around 10 suggestive themes - Creation, Devotion, Perfection, Power, Abstraction, Drapery, Guardians, Others, Portraits, Inscription - intended to offer a series of overlapping and intersecting ideas which will enable the works to be read from a number of perspectives. The theme of Inscription, for example, can be taken as one way into the exhibition and the range of its artefacts while offering leads into other sections. It will examine the decoration and transformation of the human face and body to communicate fundamental ideas of status and identity both in the ephemeral forms of cosmetics, paint, jewellery or clothing and through permanent modification including tattoo, scarification and the use of lip plugs. Body paint and modification demonstrate a pervasive idea that the natural human body is incomplete and requires improvement to attain social and physical perfection. Recurring themes of idealisation, realism and abstraction will be contrasted and juxtaposed throughout the exhibition. Human Image will show that depicting and defining the body have been and remain a fundamental concern in the communication of ideas and information across all world cultures

The accompanying catalogue Human Image, edited by the exhibition's curator J.C.H.King, will be available from the Museum and Great Court bookshops, price £12.99 . With over seventy full-colour illustrations and an introduction by the Director of the British Museum, Dr Robert Anderson, the catalogue will be available at the Press View at the special price of £10.

A related conference Human Image: Interpreting and Transforming the Body will take place in the Clore Education Centre in the Queen Elizabeth Great Court, 17-19 January 2001. To be chaired by Marina Warner, the conference will concentrate on two principal themes - ways of seeing and interpreting the body in world cultures and the manner and meaning of body art.
