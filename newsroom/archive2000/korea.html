Korea Foundation Gallery
From 8 November 2000
North Wing
Admission Free

Korea, which has been very much in the news recently, has a long and illustrious history. As Korea becomes a major figure in the world economy there has been a flowering of scholarly interest in its culture. On 8 November, the British Museum will open a new permanent Korea gallery, devoted to its collection of Korean artefacts, which is widely regarded as the best in Europe. The art and archaeology of Korea, from the Palaeolithic to the present day, will be on display including fine examples of stone sculpture, paintings, printed books, screens and folk art as well as ceramics, lacquer ware, gold and bronze.

A central feature of the new gallery will be a reconstructed scholar's studio or sarangbang, part of a traditional Korean house, designed and built by the renowned Korean architect Shin Young-hoon. Korean master craftsmen will assemble the studio in July and traditional style Korean furniture will be installed in September.

One of the most exciting exhibits in the gallery, obtained through the Hahn Kwang-ho Purchase Fund, is a plain white porcelain 'full moon' jar dating to the 17th - 18th century. The design of the jar is quintessentially Korean, showing the strict Confucian appreciation of undecorated vessels as well as the tendency for many Korean ceramics to be slightly asymmetrical or imperfect in shape. This natural, unaffected quality was greatly admired by British potters (such as Lucie Rie ) and by the Japanese, who voraciously collected such ceramics from early times, often using them in the Tea Ceremony. Another interesting item, on loan from the Whipple Museum of the History of Science, Cambridge University, is one of the only two known surviving 18th century Korean astronomical screens. The screen shows how Western scientific ideas exerted an influence on Korea through the relationships of Korean envoys with the Jesuits at the court of Peking. Another screen, recently acquired by the Museum using the Hahn Kwang-ho Fund, depicts a hunting scene in a mongol style. Buddhism was adopted from China in the 6th century and its influence is represented by rare pieces such as an illuminated manuscript written in gold of the Amitabha Sutra dating from 1341 and a 13th century sutra box inlaid with mother of pearl, one of only eight known surviving boxes of its type.

A separate section of the gallery will be devoted to rotating displays of contemporary Korean paintings, prints and ceramics purchased in recent years, with the aim of showing how living artists are transforming ancient styles and techniques.

The new gallery has been made possible by the generosity of the Korea Foundation, an arm of the Korean government and by donations from the Museum's first Korean patron, Dr Hahn Kwang-ho CBE.
