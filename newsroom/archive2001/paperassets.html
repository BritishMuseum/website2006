Paper Assets - Collecting Prints and Drawings 1996-2001
4 May - 16 September 2001
Prints and Drawings Galleries (Room 90)
Admission free

The British Museum's Department of Prints and Drawings houses one of the largest and most representative collections of western graphic art in existence. Freely available to any member of the public to study and enjoy in the department's students' room, they amount to some three million items and range from some of the earliest examples of western art on paper to leading-edge work produced within the past year. New acquisitions are the lifeblood of these superb collections and provide an ongoing source of inspiration to scholars, researchers and students, as well as to contemporary artists, designers and the general public.

Every five years the department 'blooms like a yucca plant' according to one past review, and presents a selection of the work acquired during the intervening period through purchase, gift and bequest. For this new exhibition, around 200 items will be on display from more than 3000 which have entered the collection since the beginning of 1996. Covering the period from the end of the 15th century to the present day, they will encompass grand Old Master prints and drawings by European artists such as Lucas Cranach, Hans Baldung and Jacques-Louis David, new additions to the Museum's important collection of German Romantic prints, and a range of modern material including a magnificent study by Picasso for the seminal Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1906-7).

The exhibition will also present important work by a broad range of contemporary artists including Georg Baselitz, David Hockney, Antony Gormley, Bill Woodrow, Richard Wilson, Kiki Smith, Langlands and Bell, Jake and Dinos Chapman and Cornelia Parker. One of the featured works by Gormley will be an installation drawing for his famous Gateshead sculpture Angel of the North, while part of the Chapman Brothers' contribution will be a selection from their powerful series of etchings Jake and Dinos' Disasters of War - a project inspired by the graphic masterpieces of Otto Dix and Goya which are some of the most consulted works in the department.

A separate section of the exhibition will reveal a vigorous tradition of popular and satirical printmaking and draughtsmanship from the 17th century to the present day including a notable body of work by Ronald Searle inspired by, and named after, Hogarth's famous series of engravings The Rake's Progress - another mainstay of the department's print collections. By contrast, selections from the world's definitive collection of cigarette cards, which now belongs to the Museum through the bequest of Edward Wharton-Tigar, will feature a fascinating array of sporting heroes and the stars of stage and screen from the end of the 19th century to the mid-20th century.

Roughly half the acquisitions are the result of bequests and gifts (many from the artists themselves) and - though government funding still underpins purchases - many of the remainder depend on the generosity of donors, in particular the Heritage Lottery Fund, the Arcana Foundation, the National Art Collections Fund, the British Museum Friends and the newly formed Patrons of Old Master Drawings.
