The Print in Italy 1550-1620
27 September 2001 - 6 January 2002
Prints and Drawings Gallery (Room 90)
Admission free

The extraordinary growth of printmaking in early modern Italy had a huge impact on the history of European art and on the exchange of images and ideas in western culture generally. The period between 1550 and 1620 saw the emergence of large-scale commercial print publishing in Italy for the first time and a prodigious increase in the quantities of prints being produced. From the most exquisitely rendered mythological and devotional images, which were the artistic match for any painted equivalent and as eagerly collected, to maps, allegories, erotica, records of antiquities, and 'low-life' depictions of street brawls and the tradesmen and artisans who crowded Italy's urban centres, the range of subjects and ideas represented in prints of this period was enormous. Sold at fairs and festivals, pasted on tavern walls, collected in albums, displayed outside print shops and framed in people's homes, printed imagery became increasingly visible to every rank of Italian society and central to the way its members pictured the world and their place in it.

With over 150 examples from the Museum's unrivalled collections and a number of important loans, The Print in Italy will be the first exhibition to present a broad and representative survey of printmaking activity during this period. It will explore how and why Italy became such a dynamic centre for print production through the activities of domestic and international practitioners of the calibre of Giorgio Ghisi, Federico Barocci, Annibale and Agostino Carracci, Cornelis Cort, Aegidius Sadeler and Francesco Villamena. Divided into three main sections, the exhibition begins by examining the actual processes of printmaking, from the way that designs were prepared through the making of the plates to the printing and packaging of prints for sale. In the second section, the theme is the types of people - designers, engravers, printers and print dealers - who undertook the publishing of prints. Finally, the focus is on places: the ways in which the printmaking business operated within particular cities. Rome and Venice were by far the most important, but Bologna and Siena are examined as well.

There have been two guiding principles behind the selection of works. One has been to represent the range of subjects and the different media - engraving, etching and woodcut. The other has been to make the maximum possible use of contemporary written evidence. Documentary materials such as letters, contracts, payments, records of court cases and biographies can all illuminate the methods and motivations of printmakers and a great deal can often be extracted from what is inscribed on the plates themselves. Using such supporting information, the exhibition will argue that large-scale commercial print publishing encouraged creative diversity rather than conformity and that small-scale production continued to flourish alongside, resulting in an extraordinary variety of high-quality work. Titian, for example, directly employed Cornelis Cort to engrave eight plates after his compositions and by the 1560s printmaking was attracting an unprecedented level of critical interest from writers and collectors. The exhibition will also show that prints that have long been classified as merely 'reproductive' of other artists' designs are better understood as complex and creative graphic interpretations which were often highly prized in their own right.

A fully-illustrated catalogue, The Print in Italy 1550-1620 by Michael Bury, with biographies of around 100 people in the print trade, accompanies the exhibition.
