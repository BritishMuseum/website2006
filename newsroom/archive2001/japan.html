Japan 2001 at the British Museum
May 2001 - March 2002

100 Views of Mount Fuji
Japanese Galleries
11 May - 29 July 2001
Admission free

Mount Fuji is renowned worldwide as Japan's highest and most perfectly-shaped mountain. Serving as a potent metaphor in classical love poetry and revered since medieval times by mountain-climbing sects of both the Shint and Buddhist faiths, Fuji has taken on many roles in ancient Japan. This exhibition will explore a wide range of manifestations of the mountain in more recent visual culture, as portrayed in 100 works by Japanese painters and print designers from the 17th century to the present. Featured in the exhibition will be the full set of colour prints by Hokusai from his most famous series Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji (c.1830).

Souvenirs in Contemporary Japan
BP Ethnography Showcase
13 June - 30 September 2001
Admission free

This showcase will look at how the ephemeral souvenir and its mass consumption are used to highlight and emphasise local and regional identity. The exhibition will examine Japanese tourism, looking at the way that the Japanese travel, the souvenirs they make and sell and the acquisition and exchange of those gifts. Objects such as amulets, plastic foods, post-cards, photographs and other more conventional items will be used to explore the historical background of travel in Japan and the themes of pilgrimage, regional distinctiveness and the visual representation of place. The imagery and cultural references used on souvenirs will also be examined inviting the visitor to think about Japanese cultures whilst reflecting on their own presence in the British Museum.

Shinto: The Sacred Arts of Japan
Japanese Galleries
5 September - 2 December 2001
Admission charge

Following the success of Arts of Buddhism in 1985 and Hinduism in 1992, this important loan exhibition will be devoted to the extraordinary range of art and artefacts associated with the Shinto religion. One of the flagship events of the Japan 2001 festival, the exhibition will show material borrowed by Japan's Agency for Cultural Affairs from over 70 shrines, temples and museum collections, ranging from the ceramics of the mysterious Jomon culture of 10,000 BC to the magnificent sculptures and paintings of the Heian and Kamakura periods.

Light Motifs: an Aomori Float and Japanese Kites
Wellcome Trust Gallery
14 November 2001 - March 2002
Admission free

Kite flying in Yokaichi and the Nebuta festival in Aomori, as with many other events in Japan, have their roots in rituals associated with fertility and rice agriculture and bring together local communities in the preparation and celebration of a lively event. Messages to the gods are gummed to the back of many kites while festival floats are used as an invitation to the gods to join in the festivities. A variety of Japanese kites will be drawn from the Museum's collections to display on the ceiling of the gallery while a large float from the Nebuta festival will provide a striking illuminated structure. Nebuta are giant lanterns, up to 5m high and 7m wide, which depict dramatic three-dimensional scenes. During the first four weeks of the display a Nebuta will be constructed in the gallery allowing visitors to watch the progress of the work and to interact with the artist and his team.
