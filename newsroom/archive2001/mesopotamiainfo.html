Mesopotamia Further Information

Mesopotamia consists of ten chapters, each covering a different aspect of ancient culture. Each topic is presented in three ways. The ‘Story' tells an interactive story relating to the topic; in the ‘Explore' the user can investigate the topic in their own way and at their own pace; the ‘Challenge' section involves an animated activity, designed to test historical skills- and to be fun. The ten chapters are:

Geography: read about the heroic adventures of Gilgamesh to discover the natural resources and landscape of ancient Mesopotamia, explore a collection of different maps and become an ancient Mesopotamian farmer, struggling to grow your crops for harvest.

Gods, Goddesses, Demons and Monsters: read a Mesopotamian creation story, explore an illustrated encyclopaedia of gods, goddesses, demons and monsters and help the gods collect their belongings and find their way home to their own cities.

Time: read about the adventures of famous archaeologists and their rediscovery of the ancient Near East; explore the time line of Sumerian, Babylonian and Assyrian history; organise objects found on an archaeological excavation into chronological order.

Writing: follow one cuneiform sign through the development of ancient writing, explore the world of a Mesopotamian scribe and join together broken clay tablets to decipher an ancient text.

Tombs: experience the highlights of Leonard Woolley's expedition to the Sumerian city of Ur; explore the Royal Tombs he discovered and play one of the oldest board games in the world, found by Woolley in a grave at Ur.

Ziggurats: learn about ziggurats and why the Sumerians built them; explore the evidence for the Ziggurat of Ur and then try to build your own ziggurat.

Astronomy: learn about the importance of astronomy in ancient Mesopotamia; explore the library of a Babylonian astronomer and then predict the movements of planets.

Trade: join a merchant to learn about trade and see your story unfold as you help make decisions; find out about methods of transport and try your hand at bartering to see how good you are as a Babylonian merchant.

Palaces: read about King Sennacherib who built an amazing ‘Palace without a Rival', explore the palace of King Ashurnasirpal II and decorate a room using Assyrian wall reliefs.

Warfare: read about the dramatic Siege of Lachish, explore the wall reliefs from the palace of Ashurnasirpal II and help an archaeologist identify weapons from excavated remains.

The Staff Room area helps teachers get the most out of the site and supports their classroom teaching of Mesopotamia. Resources include background information, discussion topics, printable worksheets and follow-up activities. It also provides paths through the site, which give teachers maximum flexibility according to how much time is available. The Staff Room is designed as an expandable resource, to which we can add resources according to demand.

Responses to the Ancient Civilizations Series

Ancient Egypt (www.ancientegypt.co.uk), the first of the Ancient Civilizations series, was launched in September 1999. In its first year it had almost 300,000 visitors and is currently running at over half a million page hits per week. Mesopotamia at The British Museum

The British Museum's collection from the Ancient Near East is one of the most comprehensive in the world. For some years the Museum's curators and educators have been working together to ensure it attracts the attention it deserves, both from educational audiences and from the general public, and curators from the Department of the Ancient Near East have been fully involved in creating the Mesopotamia website. Other recent initiatives have included study days for A level students, in association with the British Association for Near Eastern Archaeology, and INSET object-handling sessions on ancient cuneiform for teachers in local London boroughs. The forthcoming exhibition, Agatha Christie and the Orient (9 November 2001 - 17 March 2002) will remind British audiences of an important period in the archaeological re-discovery of the Near East.

The epic story-teller, Ben Haggerty, is well-known for his performances of the Epic of Gilgamesh. Gilgamesh may have been an actual king ruling in the city of Uruk, in southern Mesopotamia, around 2700 BC, but legendary stories gradually gathered around him. By 2000 BC there are five surviving Sumerian stories about him; in the second millennium these were reformulated into a great Babylonian epic; the best-known version of which comes from the Assyrian royal library at Nineveh in the seventh century BC.

The Royal Game of Ur is a version of the ‘Game of Twenty Squares', one of the most popular board games of the ancient world. The original game board is displayed in Room 56. It was found by Leonard Woolley in the cemetery at Ur, in southern Iraq: the wood had decayed but the inlay of shell, red limestone and lapis lazuli survived in position. It dates from c.2600-2400 BC.

We do not know the exact rules of the game, but from references in ancient documents, we know that it was a race game for two players. Pieces were allowed on to the board at the beginning only with specific throws of the dice, and we know that rosette spaces were lucky. The gaming pieces for this particular board did not survive, but other gaming pieces made of inlaid shale and shell were excavated at Ur, as were tetrahedral-shaped dice.
