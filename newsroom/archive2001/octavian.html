A Rare Roman Reunion

An exquisite brown agate engraved gem dating from 35-25 BC depicting Octavian as the god Mercury - so important that the government put a temporary bar on its export - has been bought by the British Museum with the help of a £96,000 grant from art charity the National Art Collections Fund (The Art Fund), and the British Museum Friends.

The purchase is particularly important as the gem can now be reunited with its pair, already in the Museum's collections, an almost identical gem which probably depicts Octavian's sister Octavia as the goddess Diana. The two gem will go on display together for the first time in over 300 years as part of the upcoming exhibition Cleopatra of Egypt: From History to Myth, which opens on 12 April. Octavian defeated Cleopatra and her lover Mark Antony at the battle of Actium, 31 BC and it has been suggested that the gems may have been commissioned to mark this famous victory.

The piece, which measures just 4.5cm by 2.9cm, was bought for £120,000, a price that reflects its extraordinary rarity and value. From 1781 it had formed part of the Duke of Marlborough's renowned ancient gem collection. In 1909 it was bought at auction by Victorian art benefactor A.C. Ionides, and since that time it has been in private hands.

The engraving is attributed to gem-cutter Solon, one of the Greek artists employed by Octavian from around 40BC onwards. His work best reflects the transition of Greek Hellenistic to Roman Imperial art and the piece is described by British Museum curators as one of the craftsman's very finest. David Barrie, Director of the Art Fund said "Opportunities to acquire pieces of this quality and importance to do not come along often. In securing this exquisite jewel, the Art Fund and the British Museum are reuniting the portraits of the Emperor and Empress after hundreds of years apart."

Notes to Editors

This brings the total of Art Fund grants to the British Museum to over £200,000 in 2000. These grants have gone towards the purchase of works ranging from Picasso proofs, a medieval gold figurine found on a Hertfordshire farm, and a magnificent carved head of the Empress Livia as the goddess Ceres. Another major grant was recently given for the purchase of the Spratt Aphrodite, a fine marble sculpture dating from the second century BC, found at Knossos in Crete.

The National Art Collections Fund ( The Art Fund ) is the UK's leading art charity. It has over 100,000 members, and in 2000 gave £4.4million to museums and galleries across the country for the purchase of works of art. The money is raised from membership subscriptions, donations and legacies: the Art Fund is independent of Government and receives no public funding. Visit the website at www.art-fund.org
