Mellon Foundation Award For Digitisation Of Silk Road Paintings

The British Museum's Department of Oriental Antiquities is delighted to announce an award from the Andrew W Mellon Foundation which will enable it to create a new digitised database to make its outstanding collection of around three hundred paintings from Cave 17 in the Caves of the Thousand Buddhas, Dunhuang, accessible both to members of the public and to scholars working in the field of Chinese Central Asia.

This collection was acquired by Marc Aurel Stein (1862 - 1933) early this century during his second expedition to Chinese Central Asia (1906-9). The generous grant will enable us to create a database of the paintings which Stein acquired from Dunhuang.The database will be accessible within the Museum and will be added to the Mellon International Dunhuang Archive (MIDA). It is planned that it will also be available on the internet and integrated to the British Library's International Dunhuang Project (IDP).

The MIDA is a unique scholarly resource including two and three-dimensional images of the Dunhuang Buddhist caves - which are decorated from floor to ceiling with murals and painted statues - linked with digital images of a selection of the artefacts and manuscripts found at Dunhuang and now stored in institutions worldwide.

MIDA complements the IDP at the British Library, a highly-praised digital resource which is reuniting on the web (at idp.bl.uk) the tens of thousands of manuscripts accidentally discovered in 1900 in a secret library cave at Dunhuang. The addition of the British Museum's collection of highly important paintings, mostly dating to the 9th and 10th centuries AD, will enhance both databases and further understanding of this unique collection of material which is of outstanding importance in the history of Chinese Buddhist art.

A permanent display of objects from Dunhuang in our exhibition galleries cannot be permitted for conservation reasons and viewing items from the collection has had to be limited because of their fragility. Although the major portion of the collection has been published in works of reference, these publications are now out of print. The Mellon grant for digitising the British Museum's collection will therefore enable a whole new audience to become familiar with this material and to access it in a more detailed fashion than is physically possible with the paintings themselves.

The funding from the Mellon Foundation will prove valuable to scholars, educators and everyone interested in the history of the Silk Road and its culture. It is hoped that after the successful digitisation of the paintings, the textiles and other three dimensional material belonging to this collection in the British Museum will be digitised in similar fashion.
