Struck on Gold: Coins of the Mughal emperors of India
10 May - 9 September 2001
Gallery 69a
Admission free

Mughal coins are among the splendours of the British Museum collection. This display and 'The Mughals adorned' in the John Addis Islamic gallery coincide with the exhibition of Mughal jewellery from the al-Sabah collection 'Treasury of the World: Jewelled Arts of India in the Age of the Mughals'. Each of these displays offer a different insight into the art and culture of the Mughal period. Coinage is used to examine the origin of the great Mughal leaders, the spread of their empire, and the different currency and dating systems that were used. Also included is an album of Indian miniatures showing scenes from a Mughal mint. The Mughal emperors struck coins of startling beauty and sometimes enormous size, featuring elegant inscriptions in Arabic script, verses of Persian poetry and signs of the Zodiac. Unusually for Islamic coins at this time Mughal coins often depicted portraits of the emperors. The coins on display feature portraits of Emperor Akbar and several of Emperor Jahangir in a variety of poses, undoubtedly struck as presentation pieces.

These coins were well documented by visitors to the Court and by the emperors themselves. A contemporary historian in his chronicle wrote: 'In the sixth year of the Emperor Jahangir's reign (1611), he gave his orders that a piece of gold weighing one tola (the weight of a gold coin) stamped on one side with the image of Padshah (king), and displaying on the other figure a lion surmounted by a sun should be given to favourite Amirs or most devoted servants, and that they were to wear it respectfully on the sash of the turban or on the breast front as a life preserving amulet'. In 1584 the Emperor Akbar established a new religion (Din Ilahi or Divine Faith) and a corresponding new era, based on solar years and using Persian month names. Solar months are conventionally represented by signs of the Zodiac and Jahangir used these on a series of gold and silver coins that were probably also used for display purposes. In his diary written on the 23rd day of the Ilahi month of Farwardin, regnal year 13 (i.e: 20 March 1618) he says:

'Previously to this, the rule of the coinage was that on the face of the metal they stamped my name, and on the reverse the name of the place and the year of the reign. At this time it entered my mind that in place of the month they should substitute the figure of the constellation which belonged to that month; for instance, in the month of Farwardin the figure of a ram, and in Ardibihisht the figure of a bull. Similarly, in each month that a coin was struck, the figure of the constellation was to be on one face, as if the sun were emerging from it. This usage is my own, and has never been practised until now'.
