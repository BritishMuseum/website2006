British Museum: Need to Balance Books

The British Museum is considering measures to balance its budget in the face of a projected £3 million short fall in income, Museum management will announce to staff today (Tuesday October 2).

Plans include:

  • cutting £1 million off the building fund for fabric maintenance
  • a recruitment freeze
  • suspending the proposed £80 million Study Centre project pending re-evaluation.

"In the last ten years the Museum has seen a 30% fall in the buying power of government funding (grant-in-aid)," stressed Museum Managing Director, Suzanna Taverne. "That's £10 million a year in hard cash terms."

This was despite the fact that the Museum is both a major magnet for the country's tourist industry and a serious provider of education to scholars, adult learners and around 1500 school children every school day.

On top of the loss of government grant, the Museum had been hard hit by the decline in tourists following the foot and mouth crisis. This situation was likely to be made much worse following recent terrorist attacks, Ms Taverne added.

The £100m Great Court, which opened last year after the country's most successful fundraising campaign produced £61m - more than matching lottery cash - had lead to a dramatic 50% increase in UK visitors. Private income - from visitor services such as catering and retail - had doubled per head but this was not enough to compensate for the fall in visitor numbers coupled with the persistent erosion of the public support.

"We, at the British Museum valiantly held out against previous government pressure for the introduction of admission charges," said Ms Taverne. "The result is that we were penalised twice. Firstly, we lost the revenue we would have earned by charging.

Now, to add insult to injury, we are missing out on a pay off which could be worth some £8m to us which the Department of Culture, Media and Sport is giving to museums and galleries that used to charge," she said. "That's a real double whammy."
