Sainsbury African Galleries
Great Court Opening
March 2001
Admission free

The Sainsbury African Galleries will mark the return to Bloomsbury of the Museum's magnificent African collections after nearly 30 years at their temporary home at the Museum of Mankind in Piccadilly. Occupying a space of approximately 850 square metres and divided into five principal areas, the new galleries will be an integral part of the Great Court project and will provide a substantial and permanent exhibition space for one of the finest collections of African art and artefacts in the world.

The great strength of the collections lies in their chronological and geographical breadth. Comprising over 200,000 objects, they encompass both archaeological and contemporary material from northern and sub-Saharan Africa and Madagascar, offering an unparalleled comparative archive of textiles, ceramics, metalwork and wood sculpture, that includes both unique masterpieces of artistry and objects of everyday life. Highlights include the magnificent brass head of a Yoruba ruler from Ife, Nigeria, Afro-Portuguese ivories of the 15th and 16th centuries, Asante goldwork from Ghana, the large corpus of Benin brass castings, as well as selected pieces from the Talbot collection of masks and carvings from southern Nigeria, and from the Torday collection of Central African sculpture, textiles and weaponry.

The initial display of over 600 objects will be drawn from across the entire continent. Traditionally under-represented regions such as the Maghrib, Northeast Africa and Madagascar will be featured strongly. There will be a significant number of objects collected recently by Museum staff in the field, highlighting the modern as well as the historical life and art of Africa. In addition to the rich collections from ancient kingdoms such as Benin and Asante, the work of some of Africa's foremost contemporary artists will be displayed, emphasising both their innovative qualities and the dynamism and continuity of many cultural and artistic traditions. Arranged thematically, there will be sections examining wood sculpture, masks and masquerade, pottery, brass-casting, forged metalwork, textiles and items of personal adornment.

The displays will not attempt to present a comprehensive overview of African art and culture. Instead, by focusing on certain aspects of African cultural life, past and present, it is hoped that the diversity and vitality of the continent's artistic achievements will be revealed. Video presentations and slide sequences within the different sections will provide a context for some of the key objects on display and demonstrate contemporary usage. Situated within the Great Court, the Sainsbury African Galleries will mark a significant stage in the gradual return of the Museum's Ethnography collections to Bloomsbury.

They will be linked to the Mexican and North American galleries on the north side of the Museum and in the future to galleries for Asian, Central American, European and Pacific cultures

.January 2001

The exhibition will be accompanied by the book Africa: Arts and Cultures, edited by John Mack, London: BMP, 2000. Gallery guides and other teaching materials will be published as part of the African education programme.
