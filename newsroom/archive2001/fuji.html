100 Views of Mount Fuji
11 May - 29 July 2001
Japanese Galleries (rooms 92-94)
Admission free

Mount Fuji is renowned worldwide as Japan's highest and most perfectly-shaped mountain. Serving as a potent metaphor in classical love poetry and revered since medieval times by mountain-climbing sects of both the Shint and Buddhist faiths, Fuji has taken on many symbolic and cultural roles and continues to exert a powerful influence particularly on Japanese visual culture. The first of four shows that the British Museum is staging as part of the Japan 2001 festival, this exhibition explores a wide range of manifestations of this most inspirational of mountains as portrayed in 100 works by Japanese painters and print designers from the 17th century to the present.

The exhibition begins with an examination of the more traditional Kan and Tosa schools which served the military and court aristocracy during the Edo period (1600-1868) and goes on to chart the emergence of influential new styles which were beginning to filter into Japan from China and Europe via the trading port of Nagasaki. Some Japanese artists, for example, looked to the Chinese scholar-painting (bunjinga) tradition as an escape from orthodoxy, while others championed Western-style naturalism as a more accurate means to record the landscape. By the end of the Edo period a dozen or more schools of painting were operating in parallel, catering to different classes and cultural groups.

The rapid growth of cities and the unique 'townsman' (chnin) culture of consumption and pleasure-seeking which accompanied it - particularly in Edo (modern Tokyo) - was also to prove highly influential and was mirrored in the paintings and colour woodblock prints of the Ukiyo-e ('Floating World') school. But by the late 18th and 19th centuries urban artists of many schools were heading out from the cities in search of new landscape subjects and Mount Fuji presented a ready symbol both for 'Nature' and for the nascent ideology of Japan as a modern nation-state. The exhibition also examines the way that new currents of empiricism and subjectivity have enabled Japanese artists of recent centuries to project a surprisingly wide range of personal interpretations onto what was once regarded as such an eternal, unchanging symbol.

Drawn entirely from the Museum's rich collections, 100 Views of Mount Fuji will present traditional paintings of the Kan, Sumiyoshi and Shij schools alongside the more individualistic print designs of Utagawa Hiroshige (1797-1858)and Katsushika Hokusai (1760-1849), including the complete set of colour prints and designs from Hokusai's famous series 'Thirty-Six Views of Mt.Fuji' (c.1830). The final section will include modern and contemporary work by artists such as Maekawa Sempan (1888-1960), Munakata Shik (1903-75) and Hagiwara Hideo (b. 1913) that indicate that Mount Fuji still retains a dominant position in the cultural psyche of Japan.

A fully illustrated catalogue '100 Views of Mount Fuji' by Timothy Clark accompanies the exhibition (100 colour plates, £19.99 paperback original).
