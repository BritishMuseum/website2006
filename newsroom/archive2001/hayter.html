New Ways of Gravure: The Prints of S.W. Hayter (1901-1988)
27 September 2001 - 6 January 2002
Prints and Drawings Gallery (Room 90)
Admission free

The British-born artist Stanley William Hayter was one of the most influential figures on 20th century printmaking. As a printmaker he demonstrated the creative possibilities of engraving, a technique that ever since the 16th century had been principally associated with reproductive work rather than an artistic means of original expression. After abandoning a career as a research chemist and geologist for the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company, Hayter moved to Paris in 1926, where the Polish artist Joseph Hecht introduced him to engraving. Hayter shortly afterwards established a workshop in Paris, later called Atelier 17, that became legendary for its innovative approach to printmaking and for the collaborative spirit it fostered. Taking its name from 17 rue Campagne-Première, the address to which the workshop moved in 1933, Atelier 17 became a magnet for an extraordinary range of diverse artists, including Max Ernst, André Masson, Yves Tanguy and Joan Miró, who were interested in exploring Surrealist ideas of automatism and the subconscious through their prints. Hayter, who was closely associated with Surrealism and supported the Republican cause during the Spanish Civil War, advocated engraving as the most appropriate technique for expressing these ideas.

With the outbreak of the Second World War and the closure of Atelier 17 in Paris, Hayter, after a brief period in Britain working for a camouflage unit, moved to the United States where he re-established Atelier 17 in New York in 1940. This second phase of Atelier 17 saw a new generation of American artists, including the future Abstract Expressionists Jackson Pollock and Robert Motherwell, flocking to Atelier 17 attracted by Hayter's cosmopolitanism and by the arrival of emigré European Surrealists from Paris. Colour began to play an increasingly important part in Hayter's prints at this time. His initial training as a research chemist led him to investigate the different viscosities of inks and to develop a revolutionary method of colour printing that allowed for the simultaneous printing of the engraved or incised areas as well as from the surface of the plate - a technique that avoided the necessity of a separate plate for each colour. Hayter's technical innovations at Atelier 17 were propagated through his many publications, including his widely used book New Ways of Gravure (first published in 1949 and revised in 1981) which became an overnight classic

Hayter's activities as a technical innovator, writer, exhibition organiser and influential mentor have tended to overshadow his own achievements as an artist. In a tribute to the artist's centenary, this display of some forty prints offers an overview of his development and contribution as a printmaker. In 1988, a few months before Hayter's death, the British Museum purchased his personal archive of his prints, including many working proofs and variant states, detailing his career up to 1960. The selection on view covers the early years in Paris during the 1930s, the sojourn in New York in the 1940s, and the period in Paris from 1950 until the mid-1970s where Atelier 17 was re-established for the third time.
