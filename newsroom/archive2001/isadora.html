Homage to Isadora Duncan from East and West

Workshop 21 September 2001 - Performance 22 September 2001 - Admission Free
Isadora Duncan was first inspired by her research into Greek antiquities at The British Museum to become the pioneer of Modern Dance. As part of the Japan 2001 celebrations, renowned performers from New York, Japan and London will present a centennial dance event in honour of Isadora at the Museum, as well as an innovative public workshop exploring Isadora's legacy alongside contemporary and Eastern traditions. Both the events will celebrate Isadora's achievements and continuing influence by intertwining classical and contemporary dance traditions from both East and West.

Dancing the Divine: Myth, Movement and Metaphor, public dance workshop, Clore Education Centre, 11:00, Friday 21 September 2001. Admission free but advance booking required. Presented by Jeanne Bresciani and Michiyo Sato of New York's Isadora Duncan International Institute (IDII). Comfortable clothing recommended! Further information and reservations from the Education Department on 020 7323 8511/8854.

Homage to Isadora Duncan from East and West, dance performance and discussion, BP Lecture Theatre,17:30 - 20:00, Saturday 22 September 2001. Admission free (No booking required). The evening starts with a performance by Michiyo Sato, acclaimed dancer-choreographer and ambassador to Japan for the IDII. Michiyo will be performing "Hikaru: Emerging light" using Isadora's famous 'myth-movement' approach interweaved with movements inspired by Amaterasu, the primordial Japanese Sun Goddess. Wearing an authentic reconstruction of a traditional costume from 3rd century Japan (designed by the Asian costume scholar Eiju Kobori), Michiyo will be accompanied by acclaimed San Francisco composer and violinist Aenea Mizushima Keyes. For the second performance Michiyo will be joined by Jeanne Bresciani, the Artistic Director of IDII, in an interpretation of some of Isadora's original choreographies including Schubert's "Ave Maria", Chopin's "Narcissus", Gluck's "Dances of the Furies", Wagner's "Liebestod" and Scriabin's "Revolutionary Etude". Finally, there will be a panel discussion, "Isadora Duncan and the Universal Language of Movement", where the dancers and composer will be joined by internationally renowned London artist, David Medalla, who will show video footage of his own performances.

Isadora Duncan herself was influenced by traditions from both the East and the West, and the East and West pay tribute to her continuing inspiration at these dance and workshop centennial events. The artists have featured widely in the US media in particular and their work has been seen in venues around the world including the United Nations building in New York, the Lincoln Center, Carnegie Hall, the National Museum Of Dance in Saratoga Springs as well as the International Arts Festival in Baltchik, Bulgaria and the Tama Art University Museum in Japan, which holds the largest grouping of art and archival treasures relating to the life of Isadora.
