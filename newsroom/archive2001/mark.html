From Alexander to Mark Antony: Images of Power on Ancient Coins
11 January - 6 May 2001
Room 69a
Admission Free

'The crown will find an heir: great Alexande
Left his to the worthiest; so his successor
Was like to be the best.'
The Winter's Tale, Act 5, Scene 1

The great figures of Greek and Roman history from Alexander the Great to Mark Antony are familiar across the globe. Rarely, however, does the opportunity arise to observe the way they were depicted in their lifetimes. Statues are rare, and paintings have not survived. Very often the only way to see the finest images of these once powerful individuals are on the coins minted during their lifetimes or shortly after their deaths. These depictions are particularly meaningful as they reveal the way in which these leaders wanted to be seen. Thus silver coins of Mark Antony from 30BC show him wreathed with ivy, a reference to the Greek god of wine, Dionysus. Antony liked to be flattered by being hailed the 'new Dionysus'.

Alexander the Great's conquest of the known world ( 336 to 323 BC ) ensured that forever after his image was instantly recognisable and highly influential in the power-games of his successors, the Greek rulers of the Ancient Near East and Egypt. King Lysimachus who controlled Turkey and the Balkans after Alexander's death, used the image of Alexander on a gold stater to emphasise his status as Alexander's legitimate heir. Even Rome's most potent men like Pompey, Julius Caesar, Mark Antony and Augustus sought to emulate Alexander's world-conquering achievements, and modelled their careers and public images on his. A silver denarius minted after Pompey's death in the late 40s BC by his son Sextus reveals that Pompey styled himself 'Pompeius Magnus', Latin for 'the Great' in direct reference to Alexander.

Drawing on the unrivalled collection of the British Museum, now supplemented by the recent C.A Hersh bequest of 8,000 Greek and Roman coins ( an intended gift by American Friends of the British Museum ), this exhibition reveals the fascination of the image of Alexander the Great for the rulers of the ancient world. Gold and silver coins produced with superb skill preserve the wonderfully evocative portraits of leaders who once bestrode the world and whose fame has stood the test of time.
