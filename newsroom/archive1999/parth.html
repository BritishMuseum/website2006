A British Museum Conference
30 November - 1 December 1999

On 30 November and 1 December 1999 the British Museum will hold a conference about the controversial cleaning of the sculptures of the Parthenon in 1937-38. An international gathering of experts will examine the sculptures and assess the results of the cleaning, and discuss related issues of conservation of works of art.

In preparation for the conference, the Museum is circulating documents relating to the cleaning. These papers represent the fullest record to date of the subject, including transcripts of unpublished archive documents and of many newspaper articles published at the time.

The previous press coverage of the episode, and numerous references to it since, have concentrated on the scandal that ensued in the 1930s, which has overshadowed the question of what actually was done to the sculpture. The British Museum is committed to a free and open discussion of this question.

Previous speculation about the consequences of the cleaning has greatly exaggerated the degree to which the sculptures changed as a result. Interest focuses on the so called patina, which appears as orange-brown stains on some surfaces today. Estimates of up to 80% of removal of this patina in the 1930s are very wide of the mark. The real figure is more like 10%.

By the time the Parthenon sculptures entered the Museum in 1816, the greater part of their surface had already been corroded by natural weathering over more than 2000 years. The cleaning of the 1930s should not be seen as an isolated incident, but needs to be set against the background of the long history of the sculptures in the Museum. It should also be put into the context of what has happened to other similar sculpture, both in Museum collections and on standing monuments in Greece.
