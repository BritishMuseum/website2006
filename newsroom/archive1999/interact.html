Museum Launches Interactive Learning
Generously sponsored by NTT (East) Japan

Where would an Egyptian jeweller find sources of gold and precious stones?
How many double-decker buses could fit inside the Great Pyramid?
What spells do you need to venture into the Egyptian underworld?

From Thursday 23 September 1999 you can find the answers to these and many other questions on the British Museum's new Ancient Egypt web site. Sail up the Nile in search of amethysts and carnelian, find a way into the depths of an Egyptian temple and meet the goddesses Tawaret and Hathor face to face.

Ancient Egypt has been created by the British Museum's Educational Multimedia Unit specifically for schoolchildren in years 5 and 6, and for their teachers. It presents information about life, culture, beliefs and practices in ancient Egypt using objects from the British Museum’s collections, animation and 3D models. Each topic is structured in three ways:

  • narrative story
  • a self-guided explore
  • an on-line interactive challenge

There is also a substantial "Staff Room", containing resources for teachers, including background information, discussion topics, worksheets and classroom activities.

Ancient Egypt is the first of a series of web sites which the British Museum Education Department plans to develop on Ancient Civilizations. Already warmly welcomed at trials in local schools, it uses new technology to bring the Museum's collections and its educational expertise to a wider UK and world-wide audience. The Museum looks forward to using its interactive learning programme to engage in a new relationship with all its educational users: schoolchildren, teachers and family learner alike.

The Ancient Civilizations series of web sites are generously sponsored by Nippon Telegraph and Telephone Corporation (NTT EAST), Japan. Originally NTT EAST approached the Museum wishing to commission an educational web site on ancient Egypt. The Museum proposed a project which NTT EAST agreed to fund. Translated into Japanese under the guidance of Japanese Egyptologists, Ancient Egypt will also be launched in September as part of NTT EAST's educational provision to Japanese schools. The British Museum is very grateful to NTT EAST for entering into this mutually beneficial and creative partnership.

The web site is accessible at: www.thebritishmuseum.ac.uk/education
For further information , please contact emu@thebritishmuseum.ac.uk
