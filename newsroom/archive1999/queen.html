Egyptian Queen to Return to Egypt

The sculpted head of an Egyptian queen, dating to the 13th century BC, is to be returned to the Egyptian Government, thanks to investigative work by British Museum experts. The sculpture (the so-called Head of Meryet), which was smuggled out of Egypt in the early 1990s, is thought to be a representation of Nefertari, principal queen of Rameses II.

The Egyptian authorities had for several years sought the sculpture's return from the current owner through the courts, but their case was challenged with the claim that the head is a fake and not a genuine antiquity. The British Museum provided crucial evidence which proved that the sculpture is in fact original.

At the request of the Egyptian Government, the sculpture was examined in detail (as described overleaf) by specialists in the Museum's Department of Egyptian Antiquities who concluded that, on stylistic grounds, the queen's elaborate and exquisitely carved wig was ancient. A piece of metal on top of the head was the remains of a fixing for a feather headdress in bronze or gold. The face, however, had been altered in more recent times and the inscription on the back of the sculpture was a modern addition.

The queen's head was next viewed microscopically and under ultra-violet light by the Museum's Stone Conservation Section. Under magnification the surface lines of the face and neck indicated a patchwork of very small pieces of stone set in adhesive, acting as the filling between larger pieces of worked stone. Under the UV light the lines fluoresced, confirming the suspicion that the face was not original.

The queen's face had in fact been made up from a core of marble drilled out of the head and ground down to create material from which to remodel the features. The new face was attached to its ancient surrounds and then worked to give it a weathered and damaged appearance. It is likely that the original face remains masked by the queen's modern features.

Dr Robert Anderson, Director of the British Museum, said, "We are delighted to have played a part in the return of this sculpture to Egypt. We have excellent relations with the Egyptian Government and continue to enjoy collaboration through excavation, conservation, training and the sharing of knowledge."

HE Mr Adel El-Gazzar, the Ambassador of Egypt, said, "The handover of the Head of Meryet is a manifestation of the mutual cooperation between our two governments and their fight against the illegal and sinister trade of cultural artifacts."

Visual Examination

The head, with a small part of the shoulders and chest, has been broken from a slightly less than life size statue. The stone appears to be a grey and white marble with a visible crystal structure which is especially noticeable on the polished surface of the face and neck.

On the face and neck there is a network of lines which resemble natural cracks or joints in the stone creating a patchwork effect. The lines across both eyes are greenish brown as are those stretching from under the damaged nose to the chin. The lines on the cheeks and neck are light grey. Similar lines are not visible on other parts of the object. On the weathered break surface of the base there is a circular cut line, diameter 85mm, which seems to indicate a core drilling. However, the exposed surface of the stone inside this cut line seems to match that of the remainder of the base.

The hair, headdress, necklace and rear inscription panel do not display any significant features which might be indicative of restoration.

Under Magnification

The lines on the face and neck are clearly made up of very small pieces of stone set in an adhesive and are the filling between larger pieces of worked stone. The adhesive setting is especially noticeable in the greenish brown lines. In the polished stone at both sides of the neck there are a number of unnaturally straight 'flaw' lines which meet at unexpected angles. These are the result of pieces of worked stone being set in the neck close to the junction with the hair and necklace. Similar lines can be seen at the sides of the face. Adhesive is visible around the cut line on the base.

Under Ultraviolet Light

The network of lines on the face fluoresce indicating a material which contrasts with the surrounding stone. This is likely to be the adhesive setting. The face and neck appear as a patchwork of sections of stone. There is scattered minor fluorescence visible on other parts of the object. The adhesive in the cut line on the base also fluoresces.

Conclusion

The face and neck of this head have been restored using pieces of stone adhered to an ancient surface. The restoration has been set in to the surrounding ancient stone head. The ancient surface may survive under the restoration. The added stone has been worked to produce the current appearance. The origin of the stone used in the restoration is uncertain, but it may be from a core extracted from the base.
