The Chase Manhatten Gallery Of North America
Opening 26 June 1999
Admission free

The British Museum is home to one of the finest and most important North American collections outside the United States and Canada. From the Great Plains to the Pueblos of the Southwest, from the northern forests to the Arctic shores, they give a vivid insight into the lives and cultures of North America's indigenous peoples both before and after the arrival of Europeans. Now, a generous donation to the Museum by The Chase Manhattan Bank has allowed the creation of this exciting new gallery devoted to the arts and culture of Native North America.

The inaugural exhibition in the new gallery, entitled First Peoples, First Contacts, emphasises the complex cultural diversity of Native North America through the Museum's rich historic collections. Surveying ancient North America, from the earliest Paleoindian hunters 10,000 years ago, the displays then focus on initial European contact and colonisation, through to cultural renewal and the creativity of contemporary Native art. Historic and modern artefacts are juxtaposed to emphasise the vibrancy of ancient traditions and vitality of artists working today.

Native and European contact was part of a prolonged process, extending for centuries and occurring over the vast continent from Newfoundland to California, Florida to Alaska. This complex contested history is illuminated in the new Chase Manhattan Gallery with the earliest Museum collections of unique masks and ceremonial regalia, superb clothing and sculpture. Native individuality is emphasised. Prominent leaders such as the Mohawk Theyanoguin, who visited Queen Anne in 1710, and Hilis Hadjo, the Creek prophet allied to Britain during the War of 1812, are represented by the objects brought to London as gifts. 18th century California is introduced with shell and feather jewellery, and superb basketry used in acorn meal preparation, collected from the Spanish mission at Santa Barbara in 1793.

From the Pacific Northwest come a great range of carved ceremonial regalia - masks and feast dishes - employed in the celebration of lineage and status by prominent leaders. The Inupiat, Inuit and other Arctic peoples are represented by early examples of the carefully engineered skin clothing required for survival, and by hunting equipment of economical design and sophisticated purpose.

The Cree and Dene of the Canadian Subarctic contribute embroidered moccasins and birchbark articles acquired by fur traders, prepared like the fur for the London market by generation after generation of skilled Native women.

The story of the buffalo hunting peoples of the Plains is told through the Blackfoot, with exhibits of costume and war records. The near extermination of the buffalo and the arrival of agriculture and non-Native farmers, is explained with unique objects associated with leaders such as Red Crow, Louis O'Soup and Poundmaker, who resisted encroachment and turned successfully from hunting to ranching.
