British Museum Acquires Remarkable Roman Drinking Cup

The Museum is delighted to have acquired a unique silver Roman drinking cup, notable both for its exceptional quality and for the scenes of homosexual lovemaking with which it is decorated in relief. Although heterosexual erotic scenes are relatively common in Greek and Roman art, few homosexual scenes have survived. The cup was probably made in the reign of the emperor Nero (AD 54-68), when Greek culture enjoyed increased importance in the Roman world. It originally had a pair of vertical handles rising above the rim. Some of the finely engraved or chased details of the scenes were probably once gilded. The foot is of solid silver. A separate plain inner liner facilitated drinking and cleaning.

There are two scenes on the decoration of the cup, each set indoors with two males sharing a mattress. Their ages and status are carefully delineated, suggesting that the young people were members of elite families of the Greek-speaking eastern provinces of the Roman Empire. The musical instruments shown on the cup (a lyre and double pipes) and the wreaths worn by the youths suggest that they had received an education derived from classical Greek principles.

One story about the origins of this exceptional work of art has it that the cup was found in Palestine with coins of the emperor Claudius (AD 41-54). About one hundred years ago the cup was acquired by the American collector and patron of art Edward Perry Warren, then resident in Sussex. More recently it has been loaned by a private owner for public exhibition in the Antikenmuseum, Basel and the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York.

It is likely that the cup would have gone abroad had it not been acquired by the British Museum.

The purchase of the Warren Cup for the British Museum has been generously supported by the Heritage Lottery Fund, the National Art Collections Fund and the British Museum Society, together with various anonymous donors and other funds and bequests.

The acquisition of this rare and precious cup, the finest of its kind ever to appear on the market, allows the vessel to be interpreted among contemporary luxury objects in the Museum's extensive collections of Greek and Roman antiquities. The cup will be displayed in the Wolfson Gallery of Roman Antiquities (Room 70). Admission is free.
