Landmarks in Print Collecting
Connoisseurs and Donors at the British Museum
10 September - 28 November 1999
Room 90
Admission free

Prints have been collected by connoisseurs since the beginnings of printmaking in the 15th century. Throughout this long period of time, people's interests and concerns have changed dramatically, and different methods and subjects of printmaking have come into and fallen out of favour.

The British Museum holds one of the largest collections of prints in the world, which forms the national collection of the United Kingdom. It has been steadily built up since the foundation of the British Museum in 1753, by the combined efforts of numerous private individuals and the state. By studying how this was done, we can understand both the changing interests of print collectors, and the dynamics of the creation of a great public collection.

This exhibition focusses on ten donors or episodes in this long history, beginning with Sir Hans Sloane, the founder of the British Museum, and continuing with such great benefactors as the Rev. C.M.Cracherode, Felix Slade, Baron Cheylesmore, William Mitchell, George Salting and Campbell Dodgson. Special sections are devoted to the rise of interest in the earliest prints of the 15th century, to Dutch etching and to mezzotint. The exhibition has previously been seen in the Museum of Fine Arts, Houston, and three other museums in the United States.

It is accompanied by a substantial book, Landmarks in Print Collecting, edited by Antony Griffiths, with ten essays by five authors and numerous appendices of previously unpublished documents. This is the first such extensive study ever published of the development of any part of the British national collections and is available from the Museum shops price £25 softback and £45 hardback.
