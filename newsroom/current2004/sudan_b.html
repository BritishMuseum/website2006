SUDAN PAST AND PRESENT

British Museum launches Sudan Programme

See also: Sudan: Ancient Treasures - starts 9 September 2004
See also: Sudan past and present
A programme of exhibitions and activities

 


In recent months, the eyes of the world have been on Sudan. It has never been more important to understand the different civilisations and the complex history of this, the largest country in Africa, for millennia the point of contact between central Africa and the Mediterranean world, and with a long and close connection to the UK.

The Sudanese collection in the galleries of the British Museum is the most important and comprehensive collection outside Sudan. It is a collection that allows many insights into the country's diversity of people and culture. The British Museum is therefore planning a programme to help give visitors a deeper understanding of Sudan - a world which can be fully understood only through the past.

Sudan Past and Present is the theme of a programme starting in September with a series of Gallery Talks, Thursday evening lectures and public debate throughout the Autumn on subjects including Religion, Culture, Ethnicity and Mahidism in modern Sudan as well as the history of the region. There will be special displays of objects from the South and West, including Darfur.

In September, a long-planned archaeological exhibition Sudan: Ancient Treasures will open, tracing the history of Northern and Central Sudan from the time of Palaeolithic to the 19th century. This loan exhibition would normally have had an admission charge but, in response to the current situation, the British Museum will make the exhibition free and instead ask for donations to be made to work in the region by Oxfam and Save the Children. It was originally hoped that this exhibition would coincide with the signing of peace between the North and South of Sudan but recent events make it important to widen the context of the programme.

It is, however, important to look beyond the immediate crisis to the longer term future. Initial discussions have taken place to assist in the cultural reconstruction of the southern Sudan, once the anticipated Peace Agreement has been signed. The British Museum has a long engagement in Sudan. It played a part in the foundation
100 years ago of Sudan's first museum and has long been involved in joint training programmes. The British Museum has been greatly involved in putting together the international teams which will carry out rescue to archaeological sites before the damming of the Fourth Cataract of the Nile and the subsequent flooding in 2008.

As part of its Africa project, the British Museum hopes to assist in the rebuilding of museums in southern Sudan and to arrange training in Africa and the UK for southern Sudanese curators and students. £1,000,000 funding for an Africa-wide project (£500,000 from DCMS and £500,000 from the British Council) was announced by the Prime Minister in December 2003 on the occasion of the 250th anniversary of the British Museum.

About Sudan

The Nile valley has been populated for nearly a million years. Sudan is the largest state in Africa, a country of immense diversity. In the north its deserts are among the most arid places on the planet while in the far south there is lush tropical vegetation.

The ancient Egyptians traded in Sudan for cloth, wine, spices and slaves. During the reign of the Emperor Justinian, many Sudanese kingdoms were converted to Christianity by missionaries from Constantinople, while the sixteenth century witnessed the spread of Islam. A different Western Christianity was introduced in the late 19th and 20th centuries.

Although closely connected to the UK until independence in 1956, Sudan remains relatively little known in this country where it has long been in the shadow of its northern neighbour, Egypt. The forthcoming exhibition and Public Programme hopes to present a fuller and more accurate view.
