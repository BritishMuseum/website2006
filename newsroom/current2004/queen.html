Glasgow, Sunderland, Leicester - and Baghdad
The British Museum goes on the road…

As part of a major new initiative underpinning its role as a museum for the whole nation, the British Museum is taking its collection on the road. Through 'Partnership UK', the largest and most wide-ranging of any partnership scheme between a national institution and other museums around the country, new acquisitions and established icons from across the collections will travel on short and long-term loans to communities around England, Scotland, Northern Ireland and Wales. The aim is to make the Museum's unparalleled collections accessible to the widest possible public throughout the country.

The first loan will be a recent acquisition, the spectacular Queen of the Night, a 4,000 year old terracotta relief of a Babylonian goddess of the underworld, which has been acquired with the support of the Heritage Lottery Fund, the National Art Collections Fund and the British Museum Friends among others. This masterpiece of ancient Iraqi art will travel with Neil MacGregor, Director of the British Museum, and Museum experts for a series of 'weekend breaks' to Glasgow, Sunderland, Leicester and the Horniman Museum in south London. This 'national roadshow' will allow visitors to view this extraordinary object and listen to talks by experts, experience Mesopotamian storytelling and participate in workshops. The relief will then be loaned for extended periods to Cardiff and Birmingham from September 2004 until April 2005. Subject to local security and stability, the Museum then hopes to loan the object to the National Museum in Baghdad.

The tour and its accompanying programme, funded through the generosity of the Dorset Foundation, has been devised in close consultation with the partner venues.

Neil MacGregor, Director of the British Museum commented:
'The Queen of the Night is a timely reminder that what is now Iraq was once the cradle of civilisation. Writing was invented here and Mesopotamia, the land between the Rivers Tigris and Euphrates, was home to Sumerians, Babylonians and Assyrians. The astonishing remains of these ancient peoples are an important part of the world's cultural heritage and through this roadshow and future activity, the British Museum hopes that people around the country will have a chance to discover more about the past of a country so much in the present news.'

Similar tours are planned for other recent acquisitions, details of which will be confirmed later in the year. The exhibition Buried Treasure: Finding Our Past is also part of this programme, the exhibition will complete its run at the Museum on 14 March and will then tour to Cardiff, Manchester, Newcastle and Norwich, allowing some of Britain's most important archaeological finds to be seen across the country.

Notes to Editors

  • The 'weekend break' tour for Queen of the Night is as follows:
    20 - 21 March Burrell Collection, Glasgow
    27 - 28 March Sunderland Museum and Winter Gardens
    16 - 21 April Horniman Museum, London
    15 - 16 May New Walk Museum, Leicester

She will then travel to the National Museum and Gallery of Wales, Cardiff between 7 September - 28 November 2004 and Birmingham Museum and Art Gallery between 1 December 2004 - 19 April 2005.

  • The Queen of the Night is a large (49.5 cm by 37cm) plaque of baked clay, probably from a shrine, from Babylonia (central Iraq) modelled in high relief dating to 1800 - 1750 BC. It depicts a curvaceous winged naked lady, standing on lions and flanked by two owls. Various theories as to her identity have been put forward, the most likely is that she represents Ereshkigal, Ishtar's sister who ruled over the underworld. She is an important link to the reign of the Old Babylonian King Hammurabi (1792 - 1750 BC), one of the most famous rulers from the Ancient Near East. Only two major works of art have survived from his reign, the Queen of the Night and the Code of Hammurabi, a stone inscribed with a law-code - the most complete early system of laws to have survived - which is part of the collections of the Louvre.
  • The Queen of the Night came to England in the 1920s but has remained in private hands until her purchase by the Museum in 2003.
  • The acquisition costing £1.5million was supported by the Heritage Lottery Fund, the British Museum Friends, the National Art Collections Fund, the Friends of the Ancient Near East, The Sir Joseph Hotung Charitable Settlement and The Seven Pillars of Wisdom Trust.
  • Partnership UK is one of the major new initiatives underpinning the British Museum's commitment to making its collections visible throughout the UK; to working with partners to build new audiences around the UK; to using its collections and expertise to complement and enhance those of the partner museums to the benefit of the community as a whole and to learning from these partners in turn.
