  Status Symbols: identity and belief on modern badges
22 July 2004 – 16 January 2005 Gallery 69a Admission free
 

The British Museum houses a little known but important collection of badges. The humble badge has been used throughout the centuries to signify political allegiance, to draw attention to social injustice, to express support or disdain for the monarchy and, more recently, as a symbol of international protest against war and regime change. A selection of these potent symbols will go on display in the Museum this summer.

The exhibition includes badges from all over the world ranging from the mass produced to the individually crafted, the official to the subversive, the familiar to the strange, the humorous to the serious. All are powerful statements of the diverse ways in which people define themselves and others. As well as examining attitudes to individual and group identity, the exhibition explores the origin, use and subversion of symbols and shows how badges can create dialogue between conflicting views.

The first section of the exhibition examines badges which express identity and a sense of belonging. Either through fixed characteristics which are seen as intrinsic aspects of the self - national or ethnic origin, sexuality, disability - or a group identity through shared political beliefs, professions, social status and club memberships. Examples on display include trade union badges such as Solidarity pins and NUM’s ‘Coal Not Dole’, a ‘Panther Power’ Black Panther badge, an ‘Indian Resistance’ badge from the 70s, a ‘Love Maggie’, early 80s badge and the ever-iconic Blue Peter badge.

The second section looks at belief, the issues people feel strongly about and the statements they make. These badges often refer specifically to other opposing badges and slogans, allowing a debate to continue without speaking. Thus a suffragette hunger strike medal will sit alongside a badge of the ‘National League For Opposing Woman (sic) Suffrage’, celebration Royal Jubilee badges will be opposed by a ‘Don’t do it, Di !’ pin. Badges of iconic leaders such as Gandhi and Nelson Mandela will be displayed alongside instantly recognisable protest symbols, from the CND logo to the contemporary slogan ‘Don’t Attack Iraq’. Finally, the limitations of badge sloganeering are shown in the satirical and enigmatic ‘Gay Whales Against Racism’ and a badge which proclaims ‘Badges Are Not Enough’.

The exhibition is accompanied by a book, ‘Badges’ by Philip Attwood which explores the use of the badge in different cultural contexts. £7.99, paperback.
