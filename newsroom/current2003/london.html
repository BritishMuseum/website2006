London 1753

23 May - 23 November 2003
Room 90
Admission Free

In the middle of the 18th century London, with a population of 700,000, overtook Constantinople and Paris to become the largest city in the western world. It was the capital of a country grown rich and powerful through international trade. This exhibition looks at London from the crisis of the 1745 Jacobite Rebellion to the early years of the reign of George III, when the end of the Seven Years War in 1763 saw the creation of the first British Empire. It was in this London that the British Museum was founded in 1753, and this exhibition is part of the celebration of the 250th anniversary.

It was the London of William Hogarth, Samuel Johnson, Henry Fielding, David Garrick and Giacomo Casanova; the London of Horace Walpole, Thomas Chippendale and the young Robert Adam; a London of aristocrats, politicians, merchants, artisans, philanthropists as well as a London of prostitutes and gin addicts. It was a city of enormous contrasts; beside Hawksmoor's great new church of St. George's Bloomsbury lay the degenerates of Hogarth's Gin Lane. Fine houses were covering the open fields of Mayfair while the City gates still bristled with the heads of traitors. The newly-rich had an appetite for consumer goods which meant that industries like silk-weaving, jewellery, porcelain manufacture and print-publishing flourished alongside the more essential trades involved in servicing the growing population: the great food-markets, the water companies, the night-soil men. The old systems of control and support - the City guilds, the Church of England - could not keep pace with rapid expansion and those who fell through the net turned to the new hospitals for the sick and abandoned children, to the Methodist movement and - perhaps most often - to crime and destitution.

Over three hundred objects will illustrate London in this period; from prints by Hogarth, watercolours by Paul and Thomas Sandby, and drawings by Canaletto (who was in London for nine years from 1746 ) to fine examples of porcelain made in Bow and Chelsea, as well as Chinese export ware brought across the world by the ships of the East India Company. Also on display will be a first edition of Dr Johnson's Dictionary, watches and jewellery, silks, coins, medals celebrating military victories and ephemera such as a set of spurs for fighting cocks, condoms, shop signs, handbills and trial admission tickets - all of which give a flavour of the everyday world of production and consumption that was the background to Georgian artistic achievement. The Foundling Museum will lend a group of tokens left with babies taken into the care of the Foundling Hospital - pathetic mementos ranging from a carved hazelnut to a delicate finger ring and John Rocque's vast map showing the extent of the capital will be lent by the British Library.
