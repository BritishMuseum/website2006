THE BRITISH MUSEUM AND THE PARTHENON SCULPTURES

Sunday 3 August 2003

The report in today's Sunday Times seriously misrepresents the nature of the British Museum's discussions with Greek colleagues. The frequently stated position of the Trustees has not changed and is not subject to 'secret talks'.

Conversations with Greek colleagues have focused not on the possible movement of the sculptures but on other areas of academic collaboration. These discussions have also covered the significant loans the British Museum is making of Greek antiquities and European graphic art worth millions of pounds to exhibitions to be held next year in association with the cultural Olympiad in Athens.

We repeat that the British Museum's position on the Parthenon Sculptures remains unchanged. The Trustees cannot envisage any circumstances under which they could accede to the Greek Government's request for the permanent removal of the sculptures from London to Athens.

Every loan request to the British Museum is considered carefully on its merits. Many artefacts within the collection are indeed loaned to other museums every year. However, for conservation and reasons of public policy many loan requests cannot be met. In addition, the Trustees do not normally consent to the loan of objects which are considered to be central to the collection's purpose. To loan such pieces would result in disappointing the five million or so visitors who come to the museum ever year.

We are in regular contact with our Greek archaeological colleagues and are discussing with them possible loans as well as new proposals for an international collaborative academic project on the sculptures of the Parthenon, to involve all museums holding sculptural material from the building. We are also discussing other ways in which British and Greek scholars and museum professionals could share expertise, and work together to their mutual benefit. There is to be a major international conference organised by and taking place in the British Museum in November on the subject of Greece under Roman Rule and many Greek specialists will be present.

The Museum does not deny the importance of the sculptures for Greek national heritage (the sculptures in the British Museum constitute only about half of the surviving pieces from the Parthenon; most of the rest are still in Athens). It does, however, claim that they have gained a wider meaning within the comparative context provided by the Museum's displays of cultures as diverse as ancient Egypt, Rome, Africa, and China. It is this rich diversity which allows the unique historical and artistic significance of the sculptures to be fully grasped by the visitor. They are, like all great works of art, the patrimony of all humanity.

Now more than ever the world needs museums comprising exhibits representing all cultures, to reveal the unity and connectedness of human history. The Parthenon Marbles are an integral part of that story. The Parthenon Sculptures should remain on public display, entirely free of entry charge, within the British Museum.
