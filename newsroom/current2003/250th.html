The British Museum's 250th Anniversary

The Museum of the Mind: Art and Memory in World Cultures
17 April - 7 September 2003
Room 35
Admission free

Memory is essential to a sense of identity. Without it we would have no sense of ourselves as individuals and no coherence as communities. To launch the British Museum's 250th anniversary programme, this special exhibition draws on the universal range of the Museum's incomparable collections to explore how the creation of objects has contributed to sustaining individual and collective memory. Founded in 1753 and therefore in existence for longer than most nation states, the British Museum itself will also be celebrated as a site or 'theatre' of memory for its many millions of visitors. Key objects on display include masterpieces from ancient Iraq, Egypt, Japan and Mexico, an exquisitely worked medieval reliquary made to display a relic from Christ's Crown of Thorns, the wax death mask of Oliver Cromwell, poignant Victorian 'love tokens' made by departing convicts for their sweethearts, gun-shaped coffins from modern Ghana and contemporary work by Osi Audu, David Hockney, Jock McFadyen, Susan Hiller and Peter Doig all recently acquired by the Museum and displayed for the first time. Particular highlights will be a specially commissioned Day of the Dead ofrenda or shrine created by the contemporary Mexican altar-maker Eugenio Reyes Eustaquio, which focuses on the BM's founder Sir Hans Sloane as its 'ancestral' image, and 'The Memory Machine' - an interactive sound installation by Cathy Lane and Nye Parry which will be played back at the entrance and exit.

London, 1753
23 May - 23 November 2003
Room 90
Admission free

In the middle of the 18th century London overtook Paris and Constantinople as the largest city in the world with 700,000 inhabitants. Trade flourished, building boomed and the newly-rich indulged in their favourite pastime, shopping. But London was a city of contrasts, rapid expansion meant fine houses, fancy silks and luxury imports on the one hand, foundling hospitals, crime, alcoholism and destitution on the other. In the midst of this tumult, Parliament passed the British Museum Act and brought into being the first national public museum. Over three hundred objects will give a flavour of London at this time; prints, drawings, jewellery, ceramics and medals will sit beside ephemera such as shop signs, trial admission tickets, condoms, foundling mementos and a set of spurs for fighting cocks. A first edition of Dr Johnson's Dictionary will be lent for the exhibition as will John Roque's vast map showing the extent of the capital in 1753 - an area which roughly equates to today's congestion charge zone.

Medicine Man: The Forgotten Museum of Henry Wellcome
26 June - 16 November 2003
Room 5
Admission free

The pharmacist and collector Sir Henry Wellcome (1853-1936) saw the entire compass of human culture and history through medical eyes, and amassed one of the world's largest but least known museum collections to capture his vision for posterity. Over 60 years after his death, this major exhibition organised by the Wellcome Trust Foundation will reunite for the first time an extraordinary range of objects from the heights and depths of Sir Henry Wellcome's gargantuan collection to provide a fascinating and unanticipated insight into the "culture of medicine". The Wellcome Trust Gallery
Opening September 2003
Room 24
Admission free

The next step in the return of the Museum's ethnographic collections to Bloomsbury, this major new gallery of world cultures opening in our anniversary year will present an anthropological perspective on ideas and practices associated with well-being and misfortune. From the rituals and ceremonies of Papua New Guinea to the masquerades of Bolivia and the "scare devil" performances of the Nicobar Islands, the gallery will provide in-depth case studies of four very different festivals associated with well-being from across the globe. Other displays will include star objects from the collections ranging from the colossal Easter Island statue to the enigmatic rock crystal skull, as well as contemporary art commissions and familiar everyday items. Through contrast and comparison, they will highlight that while encountering misfortune, illness and death is a universal human phenomenon, explanations, treatments and allocations of responsibility can be critically different across cultures and time.

Treasure: Finding Our Past
21 November 2003 - 14 March 2004
Room 35
Admission charge

The first major national exhibition of British archaeology in over 20 years, Treasure: Finding Our Past will show how much archaeological discoveries, often made by ordinary people, have revolutionised our understanding of our history. Magnificent finds such as the Mildenhall and Snettisham Hoards will be on display alongside more recent discoveries of the Winchester Hoard and the extraordinary Bronze Age gold cup from Ringlemere. The exhibition is also a celebration of the success of the 1996 Treasure Act and the Portable Antiquities Scheme which has led to a huge increase in the amount of ancient finds reported. The British Museum is working in close partnership with the National Museums and Galleries of Wales (Cardiff), the Manchester Museum, the Norwich Castle Museum and Tyne and Wear Museums on this project and after the initial run in London the exhibition will then tour the four partner museums in 2004 and 2005. This reciprocal partnership will allow visitors to see objects from these museums for the first time in London as well as making some of the most important treasures in The British Museum available to a wider audience across England and Wales.

Enlightenment: Discovering the world in the18th century
Opening November 2003
The King's Library (Room 1)
Admission free

The restoration of the King's Library and the opening of the new permanent exhibition Enlightenment: Discovering the world in the 18th century will form the centrepiece of the Museum's 250th anniversary celebrations. The King's Library formerly housed the library of George III - now transferred to the British Library in St Pancras - and is the earliest part of the present museum building. A Grade I listed interior, it was constructed in the 1820s and remains an unspoilt architectural gem, the finest and largest neo-classical interior in London. By the end of 2003, this unique space will be fully restored and reopened as 'Room 1' of the British Museum. The accompanying exhibition - an intellectual complement to this historic and spectacular visual setting - will focus on the Enlightenment in Britain during the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, the great age of discovery and learning into which the Museum itself was born in 1753.

The Enlightenment was a period of intense activity devoted to the study and interpretation of the natural world, the past and other civilisations. Classification, collecting and deciphering were all important stages on the way to understanding the world and the new exhibition will show how the British Museum was fundamental to this process and itself one of the Enlightenment's most potent achievements. The Act of Parliament with which the Museum was founded drew upon the Enlightenment's universal ideas to proclaim that all arts and sciences were connected and to make freely available the Museum's collection of 'natural and artificial [man-made] rarities' for the enjoyment and education of all. The exhibition examines this formative period in the Museum's history and explores how understanding of the past and present became more systematically organised, how voyages of discovery revealed new parts of the world and how archaeology, art history and ethnography became new rigorous disciplines. By displaying nearly 5,000 objects from the reserve collections of the Museum, along with substantial loans, particularly from the institutions that later sprang from it, the Natural History Museum and the British Library, the exhibition will enable visitors to examine Enlightenment collections and ideas as they were experienced at the time.

Providing a new understanding of the Enlightenment in Britain, the King's Library and its accompanying exhibition will also act as a magnificent introduction to the Museum as a whole. Nowhere else in the Museum will such a broad range of the objects from its collections be visible. At the same time their presentation in an unusual and historic manner will complement and contrast with the Museum's other galleries so that visitors, as they enter and enjoy this wonderful space, will be prompted to reflect on the way that our understanding of the world has changed over the last 250 years.
