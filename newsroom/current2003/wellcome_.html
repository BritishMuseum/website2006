The Wellcome Trust Gallery
Opening October 2003
Room 24 Admission free

This major new gallery of world cultures, opening in our anniversary year, presents an anthropological understanding of the diverse ways people ensure well-being. Everywhere, people make exuberant and constantly developing responses to the difficulty of life. People face illness, trouble and suffering, and deal with it through faith, ritual, and specific restorative techniques. While in some places well-being is understood as a matter of the individual human body, in many places both well-being and illness are seen as a consequence of relationships and community. Such relationships can be with other people, with spirits and other beings, or even with the landscape itself. Four case studies in the gallery focus on well-being and community, looking at relationships maintained through festival in the south-west Pacific, relationships with the landscape maintained through carnival and offerings in Bolivia, relationships with the animal world in North America and practices used drive away evil in the Nicobar Islands, Bay of Bengal.

However people understand well-being, in most places there are a range of culturally acceptable ways both of averting harm and in identifying its causes and treatments. Individuals select their own course of action from these options, in dealing with their own personal circumstances. The exhibition also investigates some of the diverse ways in which people protect themselves from anticipated harm, diagnose the causes of their illness and trouble, and treat their problems. It also considers how people deal with death through burial and mourning, and how they provide for the dead in their afterlife with goods and with festivals to cheer them, and how in some places people draw on the power of the ancestors to assist them in the world of the living. Material in these sections includes amulets to protect infants from Iran and India, paper 'luxury' goods from Malaysia, burned as gifts to the dead, Ghanaian coffins, and contemporary Native American materials for treating and comforting those suffering from AIDS. Biomedical approaches to illness are investigated through an installation by Susie Freeman, David Critchley and Liz Lee, Cradle to Grave, which presents two life histories in medical paraphernalia.

If the Wellcome Trust Gallery addresses these questions, it also presents some of the great objects of the ethnographic collections. Objects such as the Easter Island statue, the Tahitian mourner's dress collected by Captain Cook, and the infamous 'crystal skull' are all included in the exhibition. All contribute to answering the question of how we all deal with the real things that happen in our lives.
