The Past Of Iraq: Understanding the country's great archaeological heritage
A series of free gallery talks and lectures at the British Museum
27 March - 3 April 2003

In the last few weeks there has been a surge of visitors to the Mesopotamian and Assyrian galleries of the British Museum. Starting this Thursday 27 March and over the next few weeks the Museum will host a series of evening gallery talks and lectures on the treasures of Iraq and experts in the field will be available to answer questions on these and the future of Iraq's past.

What is now Iraq was once the cradle of civilisation. Writing was invented here and Mesopotamia, the land between the Rivers Tigris and Euphrates, was home to Sumerians, Babylonians and Assyrians. The astonishing remains of these ancient peoples are an important part of the world's cultural heritage and they are coming under increasing scrutiny as a result of the present political situation.

The British Museum has on display the greatest collection of Mesopotamian antiquities outside Iraq. The collection includes iconic winged human-headed bulls and stone bas-reliefs that were found in the palaces of the Assyrian kings at Nimrud and Nineveh, the Royal Library of Ashurbanipal and Sumerian treasures found in a royal cemetery at Ur of the Chaldees.

At the present time humanitarian concerns are uppermost, but there are also worries about the threat posed by armed conflict to Iraq's important archaeological heritage. The British Museum has long-standing relationships with Iraq's archaeologists and museum curators, and at the appropriate time will be ready to help the Iraq Department of Antiquities to preserve the cultural heritage of Mesopotamia in whatever way it can. The British Museum is also intending to establish scholarships for Iraqi archaeologists to study in the British Museum, and the Museum is proposing to continue with its plan to supply casts of cuneiform tablets to Mosul University.

Biographies

John Curtis is Keeper of the Department of the Ancient Near East at the British Museum. He has directed excavations at eight different sites in Iraq on behalf of the British Museum, including Nimrud and Balawat.

Dominique Collon is a curator in the Department of the Ancient Near East at the British Museum. She is the leading world expert on Mesopotamian cylinder seals.

Christopher Walker is Deputy Keeper of the Department of the Ancient Near East at the British Museum, in charge of the collection of cuneiform tablets. He has a special interest in Babylonian mathematics and astronomy.

Irving Finkel is a curator in the Department of the Ancient Near East at the British Museum. He specialises in Babylonian magical, medical and literary texts.

St John Simpson is a curator in the Department of the Ancient Near East at the British Museum. He has excavated extensively in Iraq and specialises in the Parthian and Sasanian periods.

Sally Irving is a curator in the Department of the Ancient Near East at the British Museum. She is particularly interested in the Sumerian treasures from Ur of the Chaldees.
