The Museum of the Mind: Art and memory in world cultures

Sponsored by BP
17 April - 7 September 2003
Joseph Hotung Great Court Gallery
Admission free

Memory is essential to a sense of identity. Without it we would have no sense of ourselves as individuals and no coherence as communities. To launch the British Museum's 250th anniversary programme, this special exhibition draws on the universal range of the Museum's incomparable collections to explore how the creation of objects contributes to sustaining individual and collective memory. Founded in 1753 and therefore in existence for longer than most nation states, the British Museum itself will also be celebrated as a site or 'theatre' of memory for its many millions of visitors.

The exhibition starts with a consideration of the aides-mémoire produced in different cultures, from the wooden models of wave patterns made by Micronesian sea-farers to the mnemonics of the Inca and the 'memory coins' of 18th century Europe. A section on 'living memory' looks at how people seek to influence how they are remembered, or, in some cases, have erased or usurped the memory of others. Images of people, particularly religious figures, who are recollected and represented as if alive include icons of the Eastern Church, the Hinton St. Mary mosaic - the earliest known representation of Christ - and a huge stone footprint from Burma modelled on one believed to have been left by the Buddha. Acts of commemoration are explored by comparing funerary artefacts from ancient Egypt and Rome with sculpture from the Pacific Islands, and imagery from modern Mexico, India and Iraq. A final section examines the objects brought back by medieval pilgrims and participants on the Grand Tour and leads into a consideration of photography and other forms of everyday memorabilia and souvenirs collected and treasured today.

Key objects on display include masterpieces from ancient Iraq, Egypt, Japan and Mexico, an exquisitely worked medieval reliquary made to display a relic from Christ's Crown of Thorns, the wax death mask of Oliver Cromwell, poignant Victorian 'love tokens' made by departing convicts for their sweethearts, gun-shaped coffins from modern Ghana and contemporary work by Osi Audu, David Hockney, Jock McFadyen, Susan Hiller and Peter Doig all recently acquired by the Museum and displayed for the first time. A particular highlight will be a specially commissioned Day of the Dead ofrenda or shrine created by the contemporary Mexican altar-maker Eugenio Reyes Eustaquio, which focuses on the BM's founder Sir Hans Sloane as its 'ancestral' image and echoes the Museum's architecture in its own spectacular construction. Visual archive material will also address the Museum's history and collected reminiscences by staff will be juxtaposed with those of exhibition visitors who will be invited to record their own memories of the Museum on The Memory Machine - an interactive sound installation by Cathy Lane and Nye Parry which will be played back at the entrance and exit.
