The British Museum Displays Exceptional Textile Collection from Afghanistan
12 September 2002 – 9 January 2003
Admission free

The British Museum will be displaying highlights of a magnificent collection of 50 Lakai Uzbek textiles from Afghanistan and Pakistan. The collection covers a broad range of forms - bed pile covers, shield-shaped pouches, square panels and a horse cloth, a horse head cover as well as a tent band and bag dating from the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Particularly notable is a striking marriage canopy featuring silk thread embroidery on woven cotton and wool flannel. Some of the pieces incorporate twisted silk thread tassels with coloured glass beads and metal sequins.

The embroideries of the rural Lakai reflect their nomadic heritage - relatively small textiles for tent decoration and/or storage, and animal regalia. These items were dowry goods made during the period of the bride’s seclusion just prior to her marriage. The motifs and overall design of the embroidery are visually striking: highly abstracted and dynamic designs reminiscent of spiralling suns and insect forms, and frequently in asymmetric compositions.

Traditionally all Uzbek peoples - urban, rural and nomadic - made embroidery although urban traditions are better represented in European and American decorative arts and ethnography museums. Few examples of Lakai embroidery have been acquired for public collections in the UK.  One reason that this material has been little collected is that until very recently Lakai textile traditions were relatively unknown to western audiences. They quickly developed an active market from the 1970s, but it is exceptional for a large and extensive collection of such high quality to become available.

The collection was built up by the collector and dealer Pip Rau in Afghanistan (largely Kabul) and Pakistan (Peshawar) over the last three decades. Most of the textiles were acquired from dealers in the 1970s with smaller piecemeal acquisitions since then. The purchase has been made possible through the support of the National Art Collections Fund (the Art Fund) and The British Museum Friends. The collection was purchased for ,34,000, with ,26,000 coming from the Art Fund and ,8,000 from The British Museum Friends.  

Since the mid-1990s, The British Museum has been steadily developing its Central Asian textile holdings. It now has one of the finest collections of felt textiles from the region in the world, and further examples of the textile crafts of Central Asian nomadic peoples have been acquired to complement this material.
