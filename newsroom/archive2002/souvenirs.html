Souvenirs in contemporary Japan
14 June - 13 January 2002
BP Ethnography Showcase (Room 36)
Admission free

As part of the Japan 2001 Festival, this exhibition will look at the phenomenon of domestic tourism within Japan and the distinctive role of the contemporary Japanese souvenir in highlighting and emphasising local and regional identity. The displays examine the way the Japanese travel, the kind of souvenirs they make and sell, the acquisition and exchange of gifts and their display in the home. Objects such as amulets, plastic foods and phone-cards will be used to explore the historical background of travel in Japan and the themes of pilgrimage, regional distinctiveness and the visual representation of place. The imagery and cultural references used on souvenirs are also examined, inviting visitors to think about Japanese cultures whilst reflecting on their own presence in the British Museum.

There is, of course, nothing typically Japanese about the souvenir. The practice of acquiring objects through which the ephemeral memory of places and events is consumed by travellers is a phenomenon shared by many world cultures. However, a distinctive element of domestic tourism in Japan is the importance of the relationship between the traveller and those who remain at home - a relationship negotiated through such gifts as food, trinkets or crafted objects. For the gift to be a souvenir, it also has to be recognised as conveying the particularity of a place as well as the traveller's experience of it. Authenticity is not only what the traveller may look for in choosing a souvenir but is also an active part of how communities project themselves towards others. In the context of domestic tourism, visitors are thus outsiders within their own country, and the host community aims to provide a distinctive and memorable experience of the visit. One of the most striking elements of travel within Japan is the highly developed services including a particularly complex market of souvenirs.

The exhibition will explore these issues through four interconnected themes. An examination of goods on sale for visitors to temples and shrines across Japan illustrates the continuing role of ritual and the importance of pilgrimages in the history with, for example, 'the three famous views' of Matsushima, Miyajima and Amanohashidate. Finally, it is suggested that souvenirs are not just a chosen encapsulation of the memory of place within an object. As gifts, they are also reminders of relationships between different people and between people and communities. It is in these terms that they are described in the exhibition by many Japanese as they comment on favourite souvenirs on display in their homes.

Visitors will be able to take away their own souvenir of the exhibition in the form of a specially created stamp, similar to those found at Japan's major tourist attractions, which is applied to the gallery guide. Or via an authentic, shocking pink, Japanese photo booth which produces a sheet of photo stickers as a permanent reminder of the exhibition.
