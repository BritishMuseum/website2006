Brief Lives: Changing Currencies in Western Europe
Sponsored by Publicis, the communications group that launched the €uro
21 February - 8 September 2002
Gallery 69a
Admission Free

Changing money is one of the most tangible features about crossing a border into a foreign country. It makes us aware that we are no longer at home, that some things really are different abroad. But within most of Western Europe this is an experience that will not be on offer for very much longer. For here, in its birthplace, the age of the national currency seems to be drawing to a close. On 1 January 2002 the Euro will supplant twelve of the world's major currencies in the countries of the Eurozone. By the end of February, the old coins and notes in these countries will no longer be legal tender. These important elements of national difference are soon to disappear from the daily lives of 300 million Western Europeans, maybe forever.

This new exhibition marks the passing of some of the most important currencies in history, including the French franc, the German deutschmark, the Italian lira and the Dutch guilder. It looks at their life-stories through coins and banknotes, and finds them to be surprisingly brief. For none of the currencies in question is much more than 200 years old. The French franc was introduced in 1794; while the mighty deutschmark is a spritely fifty-something.

Also on display will be the new, international coins and notes of the Euro which millions of Britons will meet and use for the first time on their summer holidays on the Continent.

The central contention of this exhibition is that the national currencies of Europe are modern inventions, like the nations that made them. In this country we like to think our state, and our currency, are somehow different, that they have been around forever, that giving up the pound would somehow mean the end of Britain. But in reality the pound has had a far more complex history than we tend to imagine. For example, the first pound note was not issued until 1797, while the Queen's head on English banknotes, which seems to have become such a point of national pride, dates back no further than 1960.

There are of course hard economic questions to be answered before Britain decides whether or not to join the Euro. What this exhibition reveals is that the phenomenon of national currencies may be nothing more than a passing phase in the fast-moving history of Europe.

To accompany the exhibition, The British Museum and the Institute of Historical Research have organised a conference, which will be held on Wednesday 5 June 2002 at the University of London. Changing Currencies of Europe will feature contributions from high profile bankers and historians and is also sponsored by Publicis.
