Reflections of Glory: The medallic art of 16th century Italy
13 June - 22 September 2002
Room 90
Admission free

The British Museum's collection of late Renaissance medals is one of the finest in the world and yet an exhibition has never before been devoted to these intriguing objects. This exhibition, which includes works by such celebrated artists as the goldsmith Benvenuto Cellini and the sculptor Leone Leoni, examines the development of the art form during this crucial period in its history, and looks at how artists set about making medals, the messages they communicated and how the medals themselves were used by the people who commissioned them. A final section is devoted to the appeal that these works of art have exerted on British collectors over the centuries, from the antiquarians and Grand Tourists of the 17th and 18th centuries, to art lovers of more recent times.

The medal, first created in the princely courts of Renaissance Italy, was the 16th century transformed into a recognisably modern form. Developed in the 1430s as a means of conveying personal statements about princes and members of their courts, by 1600 it was being mass produced as an instrument of state propaganda. Central to this development was the introduction of the striking process. Whereas earlier medals had been produced by casting, many 16th century medals were made by the increasingly sophisticated technique of striking. Cellini, one of the first artists to use the screw-press, produced delicately worked struck medals for Pope Clement VII, two of which are featured in the exhibition. At the same time the continuation of the casting tradition is exemplified by the large cast works of such artists as Leone Leoni and Jacopo da Trezzo. Jacopo's exquisitely chased cast gold medal of Mary Tudor, made during a visit the artist made to London in 1554, is one of the highlights of the show.

Some of the medals in the exhibition were made to highlight the virtues claimed by the individuals portrayed on them. Others commemorated events, ranging from the building of the Uffizi in Florence to the Spanish conquest of America and the St Bartholomew's Day massacre in France. This last medal, produced in Rome and showing Pope Gregory XIII on one side and on the other a sword-wielding angel advancing of the bodies of the slain, was to fascinate and appal British collectors in equal measure.

A two volume catalogue Italian Medals c.1530-1600 in British Public Collections by Philip Attwood will be published by The British Museum Press.
