This exhibition in the Prints and Drawings gallery is a selection of smaller exhibitions. Below are details of each of the smaller exhibitions.

Piranesi's Carceri ('Prisons')
Perhaps best known for his virtuoso etchings of ancient and modern Rome, Giovanni Battista Piranesi (1720B1778) also opened up new vistas in the world of fantasy architecture, breaking loose from practical restraints to allow free rein to his powerful imagination. His architectural fantasies develop out of his early training as a theatrical designer in his native Venice, but he invests the genre with a dark brooding menace that is entirely new. The exhibition concentrates on the artist's celebrated Carceri ('Prisons') series of etchings. These images of dark cavernous spaces traversed by vertiginous walkways have a nightmarish quality that gripped the imagination of Romantic artists like William Wordsworth and Thomas de Quincey. Their influence can still be felt in cinematic visions of dystopian cities of the future.

The Satires of Richard Newton, 1777-1798
Richard Newton worked as a print designer and etcher for seven years from the age of fourteen until his early death. They were the years of revolution in France and high anxiety among the British ruling class. Newton saw the world around him through the eyes of a young man angry at injustice, but bursting with ebullient, bawdy humour so that his criticism takes the form of grotesque and hilarious caricature of greedy aristocrats and pompous politicians. In addition to a number of new acquisitions, works on display will include his famous depiction of inmates and visitors at Newgate prison, his unique view of the interior of a shop in Oxford Street selling satirical prints, and a scurrilous caricature of 1797 showing the Pope kissing Napoleon's backside after his victorious campaign in Italy with Prime Minister, William Pitt, lining up to follow suit.

German illustrated broadsides: from the 15th to the 18th century
Illustrated broadsides are single sheets of paper printed on one side with woodcuts or engravings and text. These were sold for use in a variety of contexts: for information, instruction, contemplation, and entertainment. Displayed in both a public and domestic context, the broadsides were often pinned or stuck to walls or even furniture. Though many thousands of copies were printed in several European countries (especially Germany), their unusually large size and fragility have meant that very few have survived. The British Museum has between 600 and 700 German examples - a large, highly important but little-known collection in which all sheets are rare and many unique. They date from the fifteenth century, quite soon after the invention of printing with movable type, until the eighteenth century. A few major artists and authors produced broadsides, but many of the images and texts are anonymous. Sheets will be chosen to illustrate a number of themes including Religion, Death, City life, Peasants, Women, Jews, Witches, and English history.

Hans Sloane's Collection in Print and Drawings
Sir Hans Sloane (1660-1753) was the most tireless and all-embracing collector in Britain in his lifetime. A doctor by profession, when his collections passed into national ownership as a result of his will in 1753, they formed the basis of what are now three great institutions - the British Museum, the Natural History Museum and the British Library. This display concentrates on his collection of prints and drawings which, from the insects of Maria Sybilla Merian to the flowers of Jacob van Huysum, largely reflect his central concern with plants and natural history.
