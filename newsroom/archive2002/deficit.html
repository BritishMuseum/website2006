Measures to Address Museum Deficit

The British Museum has today announced a series of measures designed to achieve a balanced budget and place its finances on a sound footing for the future.

In January the Museum explained that plans for cost reduction were being drawn up to deal with a structural deficit. A systematic exercise in examining the costs of running The British Museum has been carried out over several months. Close attention has been paid to what can be referred to as those "core values" which relate to the collections and their presentation to the public and which must be sustained.

Addressing an impending deficit of £5 million in 2004/05 is a difficult task. Proposals from management were fully discussed by the Board of Trustees on 27th April and approved for implementation. The reduction in expenditure will be made as soon as possible in a phased manner for full realisation within two years. It will include a reduction in staff levels (around 150 out of a total of some 1100), which will be carried out by a combination of recruitment restrictions, restructuring, normal staff turnover, voluntary redundancy and - as a last resort - compulsory redundancy. In future the Museum expects to undertake a greater proportion of its programme through the use of project-based staff.

Museum opening hours and daily access to galleries will be maintained. Some existing gallery areas will be developed for new types of behind-the-scenes activity. A reconfigured department of Education, Information and Learning Resources, which will incorporate new media, will maintain the Museum's position as a primary resource for schools and lifelong learners.

The departments of Conservation and Scientific Research will be brought together to deal with fundamental aspects of preservation and the properties of materials. Project-based staff will be recruited to deal with some specific needs such as the presentation of objects in exhibitions.

The comprehensive review introduces cost-reduction measures in nearly every area of the Museum's activities, including support services such as information systems, operations and marketing. Longer term efficiencies will result from investment in a new control centre and CCTV. Rationalisation of the Museum's estate will include relocation of staff from Burlington Gardens and certain perimeter properties by 2004.

The cost reduction planned has been conducted by the Museum's Accounting officer, Christopher Jones, who said, "Although we have made good progress in improving income generation and efficiency of operations over a number of years, we have - like other museums and galleries - found that this has not been enough to deal with the increased costs of greater public access, the downturn in the London Tourist market, and particularly the challenges of a government grant which has reduced in real value.

"We greatly regret the inevitable effects of the cost reduction on our staff, who are being fully briefed. The details of job losses in specific areas will now be subject to formal consultation with staff and the Trade Unions. We shall work hard to ensure that the impacts of decisions are fully considered and that - under the new Director, Neil MacGregor - the Museum can enter its 250th anniversary year in 2003 with confidence.

"We are determined that our 5 million visitors can continue to enjoy inspiring and educational visits," added Mr Jones. "New galleries will become available next year, temporary exhibitions remain lively and intriguing, access beyond our walls will increase through the internet, and loans to museums elsewhere in the UK will reach higher levels than before. In all these ways, The British Museum will continue to advance its position as a source of authority concerning the ancient and modern world."
