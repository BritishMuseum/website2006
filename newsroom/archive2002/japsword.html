A new Japanese Sword for The British Museum

The British Museum has recently purchased a fine early Japanese sword blade from a gallery in Tokyo with the generous assistance of the National Art Collections Fund (the Art Fund). The cost of the sword was £75,774, of which the Art Fund contributed £45,775 with the remainder coming from the Brooke-Sewel bequest.

The shape of the blade is typical of the earliest perfected curved swords which were developed around the middle of the Heian period (AD 794-1186). The blades is signed on the tang with the name Sueyuki. Although it is suggested that Sueyuki was one of the Aoi school of Bitchu Province, there are no other known works by this smith and his school and dates cannot be known with certainty.

There are two sword-cut marks on the blade, one on the back and one on the cutting edge, indicating that it has been used in combat, possibly in the wars between the Taira and Minamoto clans in the 12th century which led to the formation of the first samurai government at Kamakura in 1185.

Japanese sword blades are characterised by a visible grain like that of wood on the highly polished surface, and a crystalline ‘hamon’ ( literally ‘badge of the blade’) line of different–textured hard steel along the cutting edge. In these qualities lies the beauty of the sword, which has been valued and studied as a work of art for over a thousand years in Japan.
