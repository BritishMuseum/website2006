Arts of Japan: A Selection from the Permanent Collections
Room 92
27 March - 14 July 2002
Admission Free

This exhibition presents a selection of Japanese arts dating from 1000 BC to the 20th century and is a celebration of recently conserved artefacts and works of art which have not yet been seen in their restored states. There are displays of archaeological material, ceramics, Buddhist and secular sculpture, lacquerware, paintings, woodblocks and the arts associated with chivalry. In the Konica Gallery there is a display of Ukiyo-e prints and illustrated books which focuses on the period 1800 -1820 and includes works by Utamaro, Toyokuni and Hokusai.

From the Nara period (AD 719 - 794), arms and armour were given special attention. This tradition carried on well into the nineteenth century. The art of the Japanese sword is represented in the exhibition by a number of blades and mountings, which are traditionally displayed separately. These have recently been polished and conserved in Japan through the generosity of the Peter Moores Foundation. The Japanese sword is made is made of steel folded many times to create a strong laminate. Painstaking polishing by specialist artisans have revealed the folding grain of the steel, the textures of the surface and the crystalline structure of the blade's hardened edge.

During the past few years many of the Japanese Department's grandest paintings have undergone extensive repair in the Hirayama Studio for the Conservation of Eastern Pictorial Art at The British Museum. Some of these were acquired in such poor conditions that they could not be publicly displayed. These include the screens depicting horses, in the chivalry section of the exhibition, and the giant fan, in the painting section. The giant fan, one of the largest folding fans ever made, was completed in 1893 and has ribs of over two metres long.

Paintings from the later Edo period (1600 - 1868) and Meiji era (1868 - 1912) also feature strongly in the exhibition. During the Edo period artists took inspiration from the traditional schools of China of Japan, while others sought stimulus in contemporary Chinese and even European art.

The works by Utamaro and Toyokuni relate to the illustrated book Ehon Taik-ki, a fictional life of the dictator Hideyoshi (16th century). These were originally suppressed and still bear censorship seals which modern scholars have used to identify them. Hokusai, famous for his 36 Views of Mount Fuji, experimented with a Western influenced manner of depicting spatial recession. The depictions of landscapes in the exhibition are some examples of this experimentation. Hokusai was also prolific in the genre of highly dramatic illustrations for popular fiction, typically Japanese illustrations of Chinese adventure stories, examples of which also appear in the exhibition.
