A New Gallery: Prehistory Objects of Power
Opening 28 March 2002
Room 36/37
Admission free

One of the defining characteristics of being human is the creation of objects, first seen some 2.5 million years ago at Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania. Artefacts gave instant advantage in the quest for food and security, but these functional objects progressively took on other meanings and values. They came to symbolise human control over animals and the environment and signify relationships with other people and the spiritual world. This new exhibition illustrates the varied ways in which prehistoric objects, both the mundane and the exceptional, could be involved in the exercise of power and control from the earliest times up until the end of the European Bronze Age (800 BC).

From as early as 1.5 million years ago, during the Lower Palaeolithic period, flint tools of exceptional size were created, such as the Edmonsham handaxe. Size in itself had begun to matter for personal prowess, irrespective of the object's utilitarian value. By the Upper Palaeolithic (35,000 years ago), ornaments were being suspended on body and costume and the first inescapably non-functional objects had appeared. From this remote time on, humans were both creators of artefacts and authors of their meanings. The Upper Palaeolithic also saw the development of sophisticated art represented by a number of exhibits including the extraordinary Montastruc ivory spearthrower in the guise of a mammoth. The artistic focus on animals highlights both human dependence on, and spiritual engagement with, the animal kingdom.

One of the gallery's thematic sections - 'The Pursuit of Perfection' - explores the human desire to invest artefacts with aesthetic qualities that go beyond the sheer utilitarian and includes the ripple-flaked dagger from Volgu, France, highly polished stone maceheads and the Sintra gold collar. The enigmatic carved 'drums' from Folkton, sculpted balls from Scotland and the splendid bronze horn from Dunmanway are used to make the link between high craftsmanship, status and spirituality. Such aspirations also helped drive the search for exotic materials such as callaïs (variscite), amber or jet.

Objects often served to convey messages to supernatural forces - gods and ancestors - through funerary offerings, as in the Barnack grave reconstruction, or votive deposits, as in the Lockington and Arreton hoards. The ritual disposal of valuables and the perpetual drive to assert status gave impetus to the accumulation of wealth in later prehistory after the domestication of animals, bolstered by the discovery of metals. Bronze Age artefacts such as the famous Oxborough dirk, which was discovered by chance in 1990 set vertically in the soil, illustrate this later development.

Many of the gallery's messages come together in its iconic centrepiece, the magnificent ceremonial gold cape from Mold, north-east Wales, restored anew specially for this exhibition.
