Science: the story of a revolution
Thursday 24 November, 18.30

In the 17th century, the invention of longitude inspired the foundation of the British Museum, and new, scientific frameworks of knowledge changed the face of the world forever.

In this special lecture, writers John Gribbin and Lisa Jardine discuss the critical moments and defining characters of the age.

£5, concessions £3

Many people across the world hold celebrations of harvest and renewal at around this time of year.

 


Stone bust of the Maize God Maya, AD 600-800 from Copán, Honduras


Silver-gilt dish showing a banquet scene from
Tabaristan, Iran, 8th century AD


Wooden model of a man
ploughing with oxen from
Egypt, Middle Kingdom,
2040-1750 BC

In South America, traditional cleansing rituals and feasts mark the corn harvests. The Maize God of Maya civilisation personified beauty, abundance and prosperity. Popol Vuh (or Dawn of Life), a Maya sacred text, tells how the gods created man from yellow and white corn.


Next Thursday, Muslims will celebrate Eid al-Fitr, the festival of fast-breaking when Allah's blessings are cherished, shared and enjoyed. Prior to the celebrations, members of the community pray and distribute alms. The dish to the left proclaims ’Do not abandon hope, long though the quest endure’.

While Egyptian farmers depended on the flooding of the Nile Valley for newly fertile land, harvest was not just the concern of the living. During the First Intermediate and Middle Kingdom periods, magic Shabti models representing things like ploughing, baking and brewing were typically placed in tombs to ensure that the dead had food for eternity.
