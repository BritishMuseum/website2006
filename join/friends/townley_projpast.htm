Today and Tomorrow

The Contemporary Arts and Culture Programme is an exciting initiative that aims to demonstrate that the artefacts in the British Museum are not only documents that tell us about the past, but also a powerful source of creative inspiration. An important objective is to attract a wider audience, especially among young adults, by collaborating with other organizations.
Contributions from the Townley Group were crucial for a number of significant projects. We continued the SciArt project 'Eternal Artefact'? which involved the Portuguese artist Joao Penalva and the Conservation Department. In January 2001, to mark the inaugural exhibition in the Hotung Gallery, we held a one-day conference, 'Human Image Right Now'!. The panel discussion was chaired by art critic Sacha Craddock and involved artists Antony Gormley, Sarah Lucas and Gavin Turk, as well as dancer/choreographer Michael Clark, who is researching gesture in sculpture during his residency at the Museum. In May 2001, a sell-out performance called An Evening with Tracey Emin was staged to accompany the exhibition Cleopatra. In September 2001, as part of Japan 2001, the Japanese dancer Michiyo Sato celebrated Isadora Duncan's inspiration from the Parthenon sculptures.
We are working with the Henry Moore Institute on a new outreach initiative to display objects from our collections in Leeds. The highly successful exhibition The Sculpted Word (June-September 2001) was followed in December 2001 by Unidentified Museum Objects, which focused on works that have generated curiosity and speculation. In association with the Arts Council, we collaborated with designer Tom Dixon as part of a major international conference which was held in February 2002.

At the Cutting Edge

Chinese Jade-Carving Technique

The pilot phase of this project showed that the approach we used to identify methods of engraving Mesopotamian cylinder seals can be successfully applied to carved Chinese jade. It has been possible to make moulds in dental wax not only of fully exposed surfaces, but also of the hidden 'internal' surfaces of deeply carved details. These surfaces would have been difficult to finish and polish, and often retain valuable information. Examining the moulds at high magnification reveals characteristic features, from which we can interpret the methods used by ancient artisans.
We have shown that tool marks are commonly present on jade artefacts from the Neolithic to the modern era. Whilst sharply pointed tools may sometimes have been used, evidence for chipping or flaking (seen on many cylinder seals) has not been found on jade. Instead, the marks suggest the use of abrasive tools, including handheld saws, files and rubbers, rotary drills and, on later pieces, the jeweller's wheel. Because nephrite jade is such an exceptionally tough material, it is likely that chipping would not have been successful.
We were seeking support to study jades from the Early Dynasties and early Imperial China, a period that may have witnessed the introduction of the jeweller's wheel. Our interpretations will be supported by experiments to replicate the tool marks observed on the artefacts. The findings will have relevance for dating and authenticity, complementing stylistic approaches, and will provide us with new insights into the influence that technology may have had on style in the carving of Chinese jade.

A Screen on Screen

The Museum is fortunate to have a remarkable six-fold screen painted by the early nineteenth-century Japanese artist Utagawa Toyharu. The scene depicts courtesans and their apprentices gathered together in a room in the famous Tamaya House in the Yoshiwara pleasure quarter of the city of Edo. However, the condition of this unique screen had been causing concern for some time. The Department of Conservation was therefore delighted when master restorer Mitsuhiro Abe was able to include the screen in his programme of conservation of work during 1998.
The Sumitomo Foundation has generously funded the restoration project as well as the recording of the conservation process on video. Support of the Townley Group made possible to turn this video record into a film that illuminates for the general public the painstaking and skilled work of conservation. With the addition of historical and archival footage from Japan, and with professional editing, we can tell the fascinating story of this magnificent screen and show how the Museum is preserving objects for future generations to enjoy.

The Tomb of Senneferi at Luxor

Without the support of the Townley Group in 1999, the expedition to this important tomb would not have achieved the impressive results that were gained from the initial sorting and evaluation of excavation material. Sub-projects included the conservation of papyrus and textiles, the study of the wonderful assemblage of pottery from the burial of Senneferi, and the coffins of the various families who re-used the tomb between 1050 and 600 BC.
A second frenetic month of investigation completed the study of the pottery, with numerous large and small vessels reconstructed and documented. An unexpected discovery was several attractive cartonnage mummy-cases of two or three generations of people who used the tomb in the 22nd dynasty. The papyri and textiles in the tomb were almost completely conserved. Extensive photographic documentation was made, and many details of the scenes and architecture checked.
Although we are not able to take the finds out of Egypt, all the objects in the tomb complement and enhance comparable material in the British Museum, which often does not have precise provenance, and enable more exact dating to take place. It is now only a matter of a short season to complete the study of the human remains in the tomb, and to tie up various loose ends.
The Townley Group made it possible for the excavation of this fascination monument to be completed.

From Alexander to Mark Antony:
Images of Power on Ancient Coins

Identifiable statues of the historical characters of antiquity are rare and few paintings have survived. The finest portraits of many of the great kings and emperors of the ancient Greek and Roman worlds are to be found on their coins. Alexander the Great's face was instantly recognizable everywhere in the world known to the ancient Greeks, and highly influential in the power-struggles of his Greek successors. Later, powerful Romans such as Pompey, Julius Caesar, Mark Antony and Augustus not only sought to emulate Alexander's world-conquests, but also modelled their careers and public images on his. The gold and silver coins produced with superb skill by anonymous craftsmen have given us wonderfully evocative portraits of these individuals and helped their fame to survive the test of time.
Drawing on its own unrivalled collections, recently supplemented by the C.A. Hersh bequest of 8,000 Greek and Roman coins, the spring exhibition of the Department of Coins and Medals reveals the obsession with public image among the rulers of the ancient world.
The Townley Group sponsored a series of lectures that enabled renowned experts to explore these themes further:

Thursday 1 February 2001: Robin Lane Fox (New College, Oxford)
Alexander, image and impact

Thursday 29 March 2001: Andrew Stewart (University of California at Berkeley)
The visual impact of the image of Alexander

Thursday 26 April 2001: Chris Pelling (University College, Oxford)
I want to be great too! The Romans and Alexander

Hands on History

When the Department of Conservation had the opportunity to record on film the conservation of the Museum's remarkable painted screen by Utagawa Toyoharu, it seemed just too good a chance to miss. Courtesans of the Tamaya House provided the perfect combination of a lavish painting, intriguing art history and a fascinating conservation process.
Since its release in March 2001, the video Secrets of the Screen has attracted an enormous amount of interest, and has been enthusiastically received by a wide variety of audiences. Importantly, it has managed to achieve a successful balance between entertainment and education. It is now on sale in England and America and has been shown in schools, colleges and universities. The Department of Conservation is very grateful to the Townley Group for its contribution towards this important project.
The response to Secrets of the Screen has been such that the Department is now considering producing a series, provisionally titled Hands on History, which would be broadcast on television internationally. We are currently negotiating with a distributor, which would enable us to maximise global exposure and recoup production costs. The titles under development are Anglo-Saxon Gold: The Sutton Hoo Treasure, Egypt and Eternity: The Conservation of Egyptian Antiquities, and The Spirit of Papuya: The Conservation of Oceanic Gulf Artefacts. This is a wonderful opportunity to show a wide audience the behind-the-scenes work of the Department and illustrate how well the British Museum cares for its collections. The Townley Group contributors provided invaluable support for this exciting work.

Andrew Thompson

Reaching for the Stars

Making Early Scientific Instruments Accessible

There is no doubt that scientific instruments are among those objects most difficult to explain in a few words on a gallery label. However, in recent years we have tried to make the British Museum's fine collections more accessible to people of all backgrounds. The 1998 exhibition Humphrey Cole: Mint, Measurement and Maps in Elizabethan England, which was supported by the Townley Group, has had considerable influence on public appreciation of these instruments and their context. The exhibition was followed by instrument-focused gallery talks and behind-the-scenes visits for the general public and Friends. The response has been hugely encouraging and shows how important it is for curators to share their enthusiasm for the objects and communicate personally, rather than just through the written word. We want to make it possible for the casual visitor to have this kind of experience without needing to book a visit at a specific time. Our aim is to have a trolley-table in the gallery, on which to show replica instruments along with a range of informative material. Specially trained volunteers will staff the project, which is aimed at the general public, as well as at visually impaired visitors, and will offer the opportunity to touch, handle and explore this exciting group of objects. The set-up will be very versatile and could be used for events in other locations or for specific audiences. We are asking for funds to purchase a range of replica-instruments made by the renowned Swiss instruments-maker Martin Brunold; a trolley-table and folding chairs; an angle-poise lamp and a magnifying glass; signing and information material; and a comments book.

Amounted requested: £3,000

Silke Ackerman
