Return to Cnidus
A sanctuary lost and found

In 1997 a Greek sanctuary site, first excavated by Charles Newton in 1857-9, was rediscovered in the ruins of the ancient city of Cnidus, S.W. Asia Minor. The British Museum had acquired a quantity of Hellenistic sculpture from the old excavation, and recent rediscovery of the sanctuary site was an important event in the modern understanding of this sculpture. The excitement of finding the site, which had remained lost and all but forgotten since Newton's time, prompted the suggestion that the British Museum re-excavate it.

The offer was made by Professor Ramaxan Özgan of the University of Konya in Turkey, who with colleagues and students has himself excavating at Cnidus since 1988. The British Museum excavation is run in partnership with our Turkish colleagues. The Museum side is funded largely by the Townley Group, and there have so far been one short survey season in 1999 and three very successful excavation seasons, 2000-2002. One more summer season is planned for 2003.

The Masson Project
Discovering ancient Afghanistan

From 1833-37 Charles Masson (alias James Lewis) was employed by the British East India Company in Afghanistan. Initially his brief was to explore the ancient sites, but after 1835, when his true identity as a deserter from the Company's army was revealed, he was forced to work also as a British 'newswriter' or spy, in return for an official pardon. He amassed a huge collection of coins from the site of Begram, near Kabul, and recorded or excavated about 50 Buddhist monuments in the Kabul-Jalalabad region. When the Company's India Museum closed in 1878, many of the Buddhist finds and some of the coins were transferred, without documentation, to the British Museum. The bulk of the coins were sold at auction, but the residue of some 10,000 coins (including over 6,000 from the Masson's collection), were uncovered in the India Office Library in 1995, and transferred on permanent loan to the British Museum.

The Masson Project evolved from the realisation that Masson's own detailed, illustrated records survive, principally in the India Office Collections of the British Library. These are being used to identify and document the finds in his collection, and also to produce an archive of all his records of the archaeological sites, many of which no longer exist. Townley support for the Project over the past five years has, among other things, provided a selection of 112 photographs of Masson's sketches and plans of these sites for the archive. Work continues on identifying and cataloguing the Masson material in the Museums' own collection and conserving, sorting and registering the coins in the India Office loan collection.

Opened on 12 September 2002 and running until 9 January 2003 the exhibition Discovering Ancient Afghanistan: The Masson Collection, in Gallery 69a. Benefiting from research undertaken for the Masson Project, this brings together Masson's finds with his archaeological record and illustrations of the sties, for the first time since he sent the collections to the East India Company in the 1830s.

Amount requested: £22,000

Project co-ordinators: Joe Cribb & Elizabeth Errington

Searching the Sands
Discovering Roman Shipwrecks

Fishermen who work the waters of Herne Bay off Kent have for centuries been finding Roman pottery in their nets that comes from the wreck of a Roman ship. Scholars have collected and studied this pottery since the 18th century (Charles Townley himself collected many of the vessels), but the wreck of the Roman ship has never been found. Indeed, no Roman shipwreck has ever been found in the waters around Britain.

The British Museum, working with the University of Southampton, began a three-year project to locate this Roman shipwreck in 2000-2001. Funding from the Townley Group and the British Museum has enabled us to survey a large area of the seabed (covering 28 square kilometres) and send divers down to check more than 80 possible targets. The divers have found pottery on the seabed that must come from the wreck, enabling us to narrow down where to search to a much smaller area. More work in 2002 and 2003 will use a new technological breakthrough made by the University of Southampton that allows archaeologists to look beneath the seabed to find wrecks buried in the sand.

We are also creating a detailed catalogue of all the pottery that fishermen have recovered from the wreck in order to gain a better picture of what Roman cargo coming to Britain contained. We have already tracked down more than 350 complete vessels from the wreck in museums across the country. This is a large enough sample to tell us much about the cargo, and to suggest that it must have originally contained thousands of vessels.

In the final year of the project we hope to complete our surveys to locate the wreck and also complete our study of the existing pottery from the wreck ready for publication and public representation.

Amount requested: £3,000

Project co-ordinator: J D Hill
Department of Prehistory & Early Europe

Who Buried the Treasure?
Developing the archaeological context

Many of the finest objects in the British collections came to The British Museum through Treasure Trove. In recent times, archaeologists from the Museum have assisted in the recovery of material and made records of the site and precise context of the find. Older discoveries were simply brought to London and the sites remained mysterious spots on the map, awaiting proper exploration. This project is revisiting several sites where important archaeological material was found. The intention is to record the sites and findspots with far more precision and to explore their wider context in the landscape. Several smaller Museum-sponsored projects are included, and the assistance of the Townley group ensures that more extensive research and fieldwork is done beyond the absolutely necessary recovery of treasure.

The Bodmin-Rillaton Project, Cornwall
Here we re-explore the famous, but unexplored barrow on Bodmin Moor that produced the wonderful early Bronze Age gold Rillaton Cup in 1839.

The Ringlemere Project, Kent
This centres around the new discovery of another rare gold cup, found late 2001 in Kent in a rich prehistoric landscape.

The Mildenhall Treasure Project, Suffolk
A new exploration of the famous late Roman Treasure site that came to light during the Second World War.

Hallaton, Leicestershire
This remarkable treasure site found in 2001-2, contains several discrete hoards, including at least 1000 Iron Age coins and possibly a Roman Auxiliary helmet.

Townley support for these projects means that The British Museum can respond quickly and effectively to new Treasure sites and explore the old. Partnerships with British colleagues ensure that sites are fully researched and finds are interpreted in fully understood archaeological contexts. This will make the museum displays of the future much more interesting and relevant. The importance of Treasure at the British Museum is demonstrated in 2003 by the major exhibition, Treasure: Finding our past, and the BBC's programme series about the British Museum's Treasure work.

Amount requested: £10,000

Project co-ordinator: Caroline Malone
Department of Prehistory & Early Europe

The 'Smirke Plans'
The original architectural plans for the British Museum

The 'Smirke Plans' are the architectural plans and drawings commissioned by the Trustees of the British Museum from three successive and distinguished museum architects: George Saunders (1762-1839), Sir Robert Smirke (1780-1867) and Sydney Smirke (1798-1877). These important public records, part of the National Archive, chart the development of the Museum site from c.1803 to c.1867, showing the progress of the Museum building from the first extension, Saunders' Townley Gallery, through Sir Robert Smirke's spectacular neo-classical quadrangular building, to Sydney Smirke's famous Round Reading Room.

The project is primarily one of conservation as the drawings, which are exquisite, have suffered much wear and tear over the years. Since the 1930s the drawings, of which there are approximately four hundred, have been mounted and bound within five large, unwieldy albums, now held within the Museum Archive Store Room. Although the albums have provided some protection to the drawings, this method of guarding has made them particularly vulnerable to handling and, in the past, many of the drawings have suffered. Therefore, access is now restricted and for reasons of conservation and security they have been microfilmed. However, this medium is unable to convey their quality and content.

This project will enable the Museum to secure the future preservation of the 'Smirke Plans' and enable this internationally acknowledged rich historical and architectural resource to be accessed by a much wider audience. It would also provide an opportunity to undertake a systematic photographic record of the conserved plans and drawings in preparation for future digitisation projects. Therefore, public access would be improved on two levels: the originals, conserved and freed from the constraints of the bound volumes, would be readily available for temporary exhibition here and at outside venues; the reproduced images would be available to the widest possible audience (as an experiment, a small selection of these images may now be viewed through 'COMPASS').

The total cost of the project is £33,305 to fund the conservation and mounting work required. To date, we have raised £13,000 and we are extremely hopeful that we will be able to find the remaining funding to enable this important project to proceed.

Amount requested: £10,000

Project co-ordinator: Christopher Date

Kom Firin
Excavation of the site in the Western Delta

The first season at Kom Firin in the Western Delta, made possible through last year's donation from the Townley Group, is due to take place from 3-30 September 2002. The results of this survey season (a limited excavation is also planned) will be presented to The Townley Group on the evening of 10 November.

Immediately following the September season, a strategy for a second season will be formulated, in particular the identification of a suitable area for more concentrated excavation of Late Period levels (c.700-400 BC). The present aim is to investigate the relationship between the temple and settlement area, in the northwest corner of the site. It is also hoped to include remote sensing in the 2003 season, probably magnetometry, to attain a better understanding of the type of structures surviving beneath the surface of the tell.

Amount requested: £7,000

Forum Novum
Continuing excavation at this important Roman site

In the summer of 2000, excavations near a mausoleum uncovered part of a cremation tomb dating to the time of Julius Caesar (c.50 BC) filled with pottery, metal objects and fragments of a decorated funerary couch. The tomb itself was obviously of someone important (funerary couches burnt with the body are rare - and it could be that the adjacent monument commemorates the deceased). Very importantly, it is the earliest structure we have identified from our excavations. Could we now be looking at the monument of one of the founding fathers of the Municipium of Forum Novum?

Subsequent Georadar scans revealed the nearby mausoleum to be a major monument, the focal point of a large triangular area, delineated by substantial walls bounding both the baths and the villa. The mausoleum is placed in a large exedra, a semicircular enclosure. Only a few weeks after this exciting development we received the shocking news that someone had completely razed the remains of the mausoleum. We cannot yet be sure what damage has been done to the cremation tomb and other areas.

We return to the site in spring and autumn 2003. Our priorities are to gain information on surviving elements of the mausoleum, the cremation tomb and the laid-out area. We shall continue uncovering the important late Roman levels behind the church and extend the Georadar survey, which has been proven by recent events to be so important.

Amount requested: £12,000

Project co-ordinator: Paul Roberts
Department of Greek & Roman

Desert Enigma
Decoding Nasca iconography

The huge figures drawn on the desert landscape of the Nasca Valley in southern Peru nearly 2,000 years ago are one of the world's great archaeological puzzles. The British Museum's collection of more than 300 extraordinary Nasca pottery vessels is a key part of a project that is starting to unravel the significance of these striking works of art.

In the second year we have completed the photography and rollout drawings of our vessels and moved on to record the collections held in Oxford (Pitt-Rivers Museum), Cambridge (Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology), Ipswich and Manchester, as well as an important collection in Gothenburg, Sweden. We are steadily building up an archive of sufficient range and depth to undertake a full iconographic analysis. We now have drawings of some 250 objects, each taking from three to six hours to draw depending on the complexity of the iconography. The Townley Group has helped cover the costs of the detailed photography required and enabled us to engage Garth Denning, a specialised graphic artist, to make roll-out drawings based on the photo collages created for each vessel.

Next year we will begin recording the key Nasca collections held by museums in Stuttgart, Munich, Berlin, Zurich and Madrid. These are essential in order to compile an inventory of a representative range of figures, symbols and motifs, which will form the basis for reconstructing the Nasca pantheon in its entirety. This will form the basis for a future exhibition and also form part of the Virtual Museum in Action, which is designed to introduce a much broader audience to one of the most fascinating cultures in the Precolumbian Americas.

Amount requested: £8,000

Project co-ordinator: Colin McEwan
Department of Ethnography

The Wendorf Collection
Conserving a body of violent evidence

The Wendorf Collection of 61 skeletons from the Sudan, dating to 10-12,000 BC, has recently been acquired by the British Museum and appears to represent the earliest known evidence of warfare. To date no detailed study of this very important collection has been possible - concretions of soil and other deposits and previous attempts at restoration are obscuring detail, preventing analysis and oesteological study.

Conservation work will allow vital evidence to be revealed. Cleaning will uncover for the first time the full evidence of cut injuries to the bones. The resins, adhesives and fillers, which were applied soon after the excavation in the 1960s in an attempt to repair the broken bones and skulls, have now discoloured and collapsed. By removing these, joining the broken bones and re-constructing the skulls accurately and with materials, which will not degrade over time, a wide range of analysis and study will be possible. This unique collection will at last be able to tell its story of the lifestyle, health and apparently violent deaths of a people from the distant past.

The support of the Townley Group will allow investigative work on a small sample of the collection to be carried out, which can then form the basis of a rolling programme, subject to further funding.

Amount requested: £4,000

Project co-ordinator: Allyson Rae
Department of Conservation

The Arunachal Project
Recording historical data and documenting change

Arunachal Pradesh in the far northeast of India has been little documented, mostly on account of political instability over the last 100 years. The Arunachal Project linking the Museum with SOAS and both with institutions in India, is concerned to record historical data (some of which is in UK institutions), as well as to document change in this remote tribal region. The Project aims to: one - record religious traditions, especially in the north and west of the state where Tibetan culture flourishes, two - add to the Department of Oriental Antiquities' contemporary collections and three - study the background, often dense with meaning, for objects in the collections which were acquired in the past without context.

The overall project has been awarded an ESRC grant for five years to support basic costs, for example, international flights, post-doctoral research fellowships, library costs, for UK and Indian participants. However, what we particularly lack at present is money to cover the costs of collecting, and it is for this essential element for which the Townley Group would provide support. The amount requested of £4,500 would be used as follows: £1,500, collecting of contemporary religious materials, especially where context can be recorded; £1,000, special transport to enable visiting remote parts; £1,000 interpreter costs, once you move beyond the reach of Hindi, the need for local languages becomes important; £1,000 Packing and transport costs for the collection from Arunachal Pradesh to London.

In February the generous support of the British Museum Friends enabled a brief reconnaissance trip. With the help of the Townley Group the hope is to now move to the next stage of the project.

Amount requested: £4,500

Project co-ordinator: Richard Blurton
Department of Oriental Antiquities

Ashurbanipal's Library
Examining the Babylonian component

Ashurbanipal, King of Assyria 668-627 BC, himself a trained scholar, devoted much effort to ensuring that his Royal Library in Nineveh contained as up-to-date and extensive a collection of cuneiform literature as possible. Where manuscripts were lacking he commandeered them from libraries in Babylonia to the south. These documents, in tricky Babylonian script, were recopied in the Palace chancery by the best scribes in the country. His library, now in the British Museum, contained about 25,000 tablets of all sorts.

We know there are about 3,500 Babylonian tablets from Nineveh. Our project will re-examine this corpus to establish exactly which compositions are involved and which of them can be identified as reflecting Ashurbanipal's collecting activities.

This project is now particularly timely. The Iraqi Ministry of Higher Education and Research has approached the British Museum for practical support in developing a new Institute at Mosul dedicated to the development of Iraqi Assyriology with a particular focus on the study of Ashurbanipal's Library. The Museum is publicly committed to responding positively by providing replicas and academic support. We hope that this policy will help to reduce the risk of future restitution claims. Our project fits centrally within this programme.

We would like to ask the Townley Group for support to employ a post-doctoral Assyriologist for a period of up to six months to work with us on this project.

Amount requested: £11,000

Project co-ordinators: Christopher Walker and Irving Finkel
Department of Ancient Near East
