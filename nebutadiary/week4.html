Archive diary

Week 4

Thursday 6 December 2001

Today, again, most of the team is involved in covering with paper the waves and the Kannon figure at the back, while Takashi Kitamura begins decorating the front. With the addition of ink and charcoal lines, details of the features of the human figure and the horse are beginning to emerge from the plain white paper sculpture. The black ink is used to outline the design, for example differentiating between the different parts of Minamoto no Yoshitsune's clothing and adding dynamism to the cloth pattern by indicating the folds in the sleeves. The charcoal provides the lines which will be followed in the application of molten paraffin wax. Many of the patterns on the clothing are made with a stencil initially cut out of paper (so that it can be wrapped around the shapes of the nebuta) or thin card. Takashi Kitamura has brought a couple of Japanese pattern reference books and uses them as a source of inspiration. For example, the crest pattern on the right arm sleeve and trousers showing gentian blossoms (identified with Yoshitsune's family crest) is combined with five bamboo leaves to form the sasa-rind which became a common decorative pattern for garments during the Edo period in Japan. With most of the electrical work out of the way, Michiko Funahashi and even Katsutoshi Funahashi join the papering team. In Aomori, tasks are quite clearly divided between those with relevant experience in the different stages of the construction. The papering would be done mostly by women and the Funahashi would not normally be involved in anything but the electrical wiring. However, with the pressure of time and a small team, the construction of the nebuta at The British Museum is an occasion for all to join in.

 
Friday 7 December 2001

One of the most important processes in the construction of a nebuta started today. This involves melting paraffin wax and applying it with thin brushes to the paper covering the wire structure. It is a form of resist-dye technique, but, unlike batik for example, the wax is absorbed by the paper and will remain part of the finished construction. The wax is used to separate the colours but also to create translucent areas. The lighting inside the nebuta is turned on and off to assess the overall effect as the wax decoration progresses. With relatively low levels of lighting in the gallery space, the back of the float is lit up most of the day simply to help the papering team in their work. Tomoko Kitamura prepared the first batch of wax, tipping a shallow layer of white pellets of unscented wax into the small metal buckets which were left to heat on the hot plates. Once the wax is liquid, it is carried over to where the work is being carried out on the nebuta. The two husband and wife teams, Takashi and Tomoko Kitamura and Akira and Reiko Kitamura, together with Maximilian Marshall, set to work. The wax is painted on with slim paint brushes, following the lines of the charcoal drawings already in place. As soon as the wax in the bucket cools and becomes too stiff to be painted on, another bucket is handed over. A routine is soon in place: Katsutoshi Funahashi is enlisted to watch over the melting wax and to bring over a replacement bucket of warm wax whenever required by the other five perched on the scaffold boards.

 

 

 
Saturday 8 December 2001

While paper was still being applied to the back of the float, the application of the wax continued with the same routine established yesterday. Whilst most of the wax is being applied with brushes, today Tomoko Kitamura is using a brush with a small wad of cloth at the end of it to create the dots of the pattern on the clothing. The wax appears as dark grey lines on the white paper, but the effect is reversed when the paper is lit from behind with the wax pattern standing out brightly. By the end of the day, the wax and ink lines were completed on the figure of Minamoto no Yoshitsune. A small group of young people came to visit the nebuta construction site in anticipation of the lantern workshop they were attending in the Museum towards the end of the morning. Throughout the afternoon, they walked through the gallery on their way home, carrying lanterns they had made with willow (or withy) and tissue paper. This combination of withy and tissue paper is more commonly used in Britain to create lanterns. Similarly in Japan, lanterns of the nebuta type were made with slit bamboo before wire became widely available in the first half of the 20th century. In the Aomori region, bamboo is still used for smaller nebuta, and in particular during workshops at which children learn the basic methods of nebuta construction. However, to achieve the light effects for which the nebuta are so famous, Aomori artists prefer a thick fibrous paper to tissue paper. This also makes the float stronger to sustain the vigorous handling during the procession.

 

Sunday 9 December 2001

The smell of wax has cleared by the time the team set to work again in the morning. Although this particular wax is said to be odourless, by the end of each day, the smell of hot wax has invaded the gallery. Several regular visitors who have been monitoring progress of the construction on a weekly basis commented on the multisensorial experience of the exhibition as they have watched the nebuta grow, handled the paper and now smell the wax. Minamoto no Yoshitsune and his complex costume design having been finished, work begins on the horse. This is a much less complex design of wax lines: only a number of near complete circles are painted with the wax to create the mottled effect of the horse's coat. Meanwhile, one side of the gallery has become a focus of activity as all the poster paints are brought out and the mixing of bright colours begins to decorate the clothing of Minamoto no Yoshitsune. The first sections to be decorated are parts of the armour and the design of red crosses that represent the network of cords holding together the articulated plates that make up the armour. Miyuki Oda has been called away from the papering team to help with the painting. In the afternoon, Takashi Kitamura draws the ink lines on the face of Yoshitsune and the eyes in particular. This is one of the most crucial aspects of the overall effect of the nebuta float. The gaze is what will give life to the design. During the festival, when the floats are in movement, judges and spectators will assess their quality partly by the way in which the gaze of the main human figures hits the crowd as the floats are rotated and moved through the procession.

 
Monday 10 December 2001

The Nebuta Team are having a day off and will be back at work tomorrow.

 

  

Tuesday 11 December 2001

The focus on the front of the float is now on the colour and the team is spread out evenly on both sides of the nebuta. Part of the team is at the back with Maki Fujiwara, Tokuko Iwasaki, Keiko Osanai and volunteers completing the papering of the Kannon figure and the waves. The others are all around the front, working on the scaffold boards at different levels and moving fast as the painting progresses. The colours are applied either uniformly or with gradation to create depth. This is achieved by means of a wide flat brush: one side is dipped into thicker paint and the other into a diluted version of the same colour. The brush is then applied flat against the paper thus creating a colour line graded in its width. The choice of colour is determined by the painting which Takashi Kitamura did to depict his chosen design for this nebuta. This painting, together with history books on the design of Japanese medieval armour, are regularly referred to by Takashi and Akira Kitamura in coordinating the work of those applying the colours.

 

 

Wednesday 12 December 2001

Bar a few holes where the scaffold boards are still resting on the internal structure of the nebuta, the outer wire form of the nebuta is now completely covered in paper. The papering team and volunteers are relieved to see this stage finished and are looking forward to moving on to other tasks. This has been by far the most demanding aspect of the construction, with an average of 10 people working at it intensively for over 10 days. Each new process hides the preceding stages of construction: now that the wood and wire structure is hidden by the paper, questions from visitors are increasingly focusing on trying to understand how the nebuta is held up on the inside. One child thought that perhaps it was filled with air... With the addition of colour, the difference in light effects achieved through the variety of light-bulbs in place is increasingly visible. The fluorescent tubes highlight the cold colours such as the blue of the sword blade or the water. Similarly, behind the paper covering the front details of Minamoto no Yoshitsune's helmet, there are both fluorescent tubes and small clear bulbs, illustrating a range of metals from a warm bronze to bright gold although the paper itself is all painted with the same vivid yellow. Some further wax work is taking place, in particular in the decoration of the waves. Akira Kitamura uses a small brush which he aims towards the paper and flicks against the edge of a small tin in which he has poured a little molten wax. Flecks of wax are thus dispersed over the paper, particularly at the top of the waves. Where drops of wax have fallen, the blue of the paint will not hold when it is applied. The drops of wax thus remaining white will become realistic flecks of foam as though dropped from the top of the overhanging wave.
