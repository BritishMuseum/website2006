Archive diary

Week 5

Thursday 13 December 2001

Today, Akira Kitamura has started air-brushing and begins with the horse's mottled grey coat. He is in charge of this aspect of the painting on all the larger parts of the nebuta, the horse's coat and the water. As always, the lead artist (in this case his twin brother, Takashi Kitamura) will be painting the face on the front. But before any air-brushing can take place, all the nearby areas have to be protected. Sheets of newspaper are laid over the lower parts of the structure below where the air-brushing will take place. Other sheets are cut to size around details that need protecting, such as the horse trappings that have been painted red, or the eyes and nostrils of the horse. Once this is in place, and Akira Kitamura having achieved the right balance of colours in the paint for the air-brush, he sets to work putting a first thin layer and assessing the overall effect before continuing. This is applied with the lights off on the inside of the nebuta, but he explained that the aim is to achieve a balance with the layers of air-brushed paint, such that whether lit or not from the inside, the colour would look approximately the same. Meanwhile, now that the back scene is entirely covered in paper, Takashi Kitamura begins to outline the ink and charcoal lines on the Kannon figure. Particular concentration goes on the face and the eyes which he carries out by eye. For his choice of patterns on the robes and the crown, Takashi Kitamura refers to a book on the Buddhist iconography of Kannon.

 

Friday 14 December 2001

Once the nebuta is complete, it will be lifted onto a platform on Sunday. The platform has to be sturdy enough to carry the weight of the float (over a ton) and yet light enough to be carried and slipped into place once the nebuta is lifted off the floor. Four equal sections were planned and the pieces of wood were brought into the gallery today for assembling to begin. This is also the last day of wax work. Maximilian Marshall is put to work on the horse's hooves. These have been loosely painted with strokes of black ink, creating the lines of the horn and given an added shine for when the float is lit through the application of wax over the ink. Meanwhile, throughout the morning Tomoko Kitamura was working on the intricate patterns of the Kannon figure. She is one of the most proficient wax painters and carries out the more delicate work. By the afternoon, she is joined by most of the other women to paint the details of the clothing. Only the skin of the Kannon figure is left white as Takashi Kitamura will be adding the colour there with the air-brush. In the early afternoon, he completes the face of Minamoto no Yoshitsune. With the careful application of air-brush, Yoshitsune acquires a rugged skin with a healthy pink glow and a shadow of a beard on his cheeks and chin. Although there is still one day to go to finish the nebuta form before it is lifted onto its platform, once Takashi Kitamura finished Yoshitsune's face, there was an overall sense of completion and of drawing to a close. Most of the scaffold boards were withdrawn and the stepladders stacked to one side. The wave and foam which emerges below Yoshitsune's extended arm holding the sword had been created separately and was now brought right up against the figure. Finally, at the end of the day, the entire team and volunteers lifted the back scene to bring it together with the figures of Yoshitsune and the horse and fix it to the main base of the float. Where gaps were left, paper was stuck on and the left-over bowls of colour called upon to patch the design.

 

Saturday 15 December 2001

The remaining two sections of the platform were assembled today, and all four are now aligned in front of the nebuta in preparation for being slipped under it tomorrow. As the nebuta was visibly nearing completion, more and more visitors were enquiring about the lifting: how would such a fragile, yet heavy structure, weighing between a ton and a ton and a half, be lifted onto a platform? Day 32 and this is the last opportunity to work on the sculptural form of the nebuta. In the morning, details of the clothing of the Kannon were protected with newspaper to allow for the air-brushing of the bare skin of the figure. The delicate pink that was to achieved required several layers, to the point that the paper itself began to wrinkle under the spray (though it did straight out as it dried!). Akira Kitamura worked on the waves, applying with the air-brush the different shades of blue that will hint at the depth of the sea and size of the waves. The afternoon was spent putting the final touches to the front of Minamoto no Yoshitsune's costume. The breast-plate of his armour was decorated with fine details in a dark blue, using stencil patterns and requiring a whole team of people around Akira Kitamura while he used the air-brush: two people to help hold the stencils and place them in perfect geometry to each other as they were moved across the paper surface; one person to hold a lamp to improve the lighting conditions under the shadow of the head looming above; one person to ensure the flex of the air-brush did not get tangled; two people to continue mixing the colours and replenish the air-brush whenever Akira Kitamura paused in his work. Finally, he added touches of red with a paint-brush to highlight some of the details in the pattern. Takashi Kitamura stood back from the float to assess the overall completed float: at 3pm today, the nebuta was finished at last. It was an emotional moment for the team and volunteers...

 

Sunday 16 December 2001

This was the one day in which any problem could have meant the destruction of the four weeks of work carried out until now: the nebuta had to be lifted into the air and its platform slid into place under it. Organising the lifting of the nebuta on museum premises had been one of the most challenging aspects of the project during the exhibition preparations and was put in the hands of the Unusual Rigging Company. Having closely consulted with Takashi Kitamura during the planning stage, the aim was to send a team of two or three men to build a goal-post shaped gantry on either side of the float and hoist it up by means of chains. This was at odds with the way the lifting is done in Japan where some 50 men simply count to three and lift it up onto a wheeled platform. The equipment arrived at 7am and by 9.30am it was erected and ready for the lifting to begin. Chains descended from the gantry and were attached to steel spigots inserted into the hollow beams that had been in place under the base of the nebuta since the beginning of the construction. Six chains on either side were being cranked up slowly and evenly. The entire Nebuta Team, museum staff in readiness to help insert the platform sections, and visitors passing by all watched anxiously as the nebuta creaked, sagged and swayed a little as it finally got off the ground. It took some 45 minutes for it to reach the agreed height of 1m35, but the sag in the middle of the float meant that there were only 4 to 5cm to spare for inserting the platform sections. By 1pm, each of the four sections was in place and straightened and the nebuta lowered back down to rest finally on the completed platform. A sigh of relief! In the afternoon and once the lifting equipment was out of the way, the platform was decorated by the Nebuta Team. First a blue cloth was stapled around the base of the float, continuing the motif of the sea and hiding the joins between the float and the platform. The 'sponsor box' was fixed to the front, advertising as it does in Japan the names of the main partners in the project. Similarly on the small lanterns that are hung all around the two sides and the back of the platform: each of the lanterns carries the name of a sponsor, in this case with both their Japanese and English names. Boards decorated with the traditional motif of a botan, the flower of a tree peony, were finally attached on all sides. Soon, clearing and tidying the area began and surplus materials and equipment were carried away from the gallery. Katsutoshi and Michiko Funahashi were the last to finish: each of the 120 small lanterns had to be installed with its own individual light-bulb...

 

 

Monday 17 December 2001

It was a short morning of work: the Funahashi carried out the final checks on the electrical wiring of the nebuta; Maki Fujiwara, Tokuko Iwasaki, Miyuki Oda and Keiko Osanai finished clearing and cleaning the area which had been used for construction during the last four weeks. Meanwhile in the storage areas backstage, the two husband and wife teams were sorting out tools and equipment ready for shipment back to Japan. The completed nebuta is now on display in the gallery with all lights turned off, except when they are being tested. It is near impossible to guess at the complexity of the internal structure of the float, but if one looks carefully, there are just two places where one can see inside, in the central area of the float at the base of the wave and the back of the horse. Here, the paper does not entirely cover the wire and a few light-bulbs, as well as some of the wood and wire on the inside are visible.

 

 

Tuesday 18 December 2001

A reception this evening celebrated the work of Takashi Kitamura and his team. The float had been sitting in darkness since it was completed but at 20:00, the Mayor of Camden switched on the lights and the near one thousand light-bulbs inside the nebuta made its full impact on the audience. Minamoto no Yoshitsune will now be gazing down at visitors to the Wellcome Trust Gallery for a further two and half months while we all begin to plan the nebuta's future and how to get it out through the door...
