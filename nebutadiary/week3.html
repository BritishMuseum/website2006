Archive diary

Week 3

Thursday 29 November 2001

Today is a quiet day in the gallery, with fewer visitors passing through. The entire team is concentrated on covering the wire structure with paper. As Reiko Kitamura first described this stage of the process, 'one forgets time is passing'. It is an absorbing task, slow and meticulous, and relatively little conversation takes place while work continues on the papering. When working in Aomori, a radio is often playing in the background, but here some of the team members have opted for personal stereos, while others occasionally chat quietly when working near to each other. Katsutoshi and Michiko Funahashi's work involves more discussion. As all the electrics are to Japanese standard (110v), eight transformers will provide the link between the nebuta and the mains. The Funahashi thus need to keep track of the number of light-bulbs and their wattage to ensure even and consistent wiring. This is done partly by keeping detailed notes. Although the amount and type of light-bulbs is estimated early on in the project, their placing and exact number are determined by the wire form once constructed and evaluated as the wiring progresses.

 

 

Friday 30 November 2001

By the end of the working day, most of the human figure representing Minamoto no Yoshitsune was covered with paper. Constituting a white mass on one side of the nebuta, the figure looks strangely unbalanced compared to the lighter wire structures still surrounding it. One visitor described it as a ‘sad snowman toppling over' and seeming to reach out with a stick (the sword has not yet been covered in paper). Visitors' comments have changed over the last few days: the basic wood/wire structure drew people to compare it with modern art installations, but many now state a preference for the ‘pure' white form covered in paper. The paper makes it easier to understand the shapes as it defines the outer wire grid and hides the internal wood structure. It moulds the wire grid so perfectly that it looks as though the white is a fabric stretched over the wire. One of the most frequent explanations that is made to visitors is about the technique of covering each space of the wire grid with one piece of paper cut to size. This inevitably seems to draw people to ask about the paper and if they could have some of the scraps that are being thrown away. Most nebuta artists use hoshoshi, a thick fibrous paper made with one of the papermaking mulberries, sometimes used for indoor screens; in this case, the paper is an industrial variant with fire-retardant properties.

 

 

Saturday 1 December 2001

The papering continues. Takashi Kitamura has moved over to work on the horse's head while others complete parts of the figure's armour. The volunteers have been training for the best part of this week on sections that were both large and with relatively little curve in the wire, invisible to the public. This was ideal training ground, but today, some are being allowed to work on the front and the sides, including on parts of the figure's armour. In the afternoon, most of the team moved over to work on the horse. Only Miyuki Oda was working on Minamoto no Yoshitsune, having been invited to cover the face of the figure. She has been chosen for this work in recognition of her particular skills and delicate touch in the application of paper to the wire. With a number of North American visitors enquiring about the papering process, a long discussion took place today on the simplicity of the materials used to construct a nebuta. A simple combination of wood, wire, thick paper and glue brought out discussions on different carpentry techniques, how to create a smooth concrete by adding a water soluble adhesive to the mixture, and many tricks of the trade. Visitors brought their own experiences to bear on their understanding of the processes and products involved and translated for each other. For example, the glue used to stick the paper to the wire of the nebuta is a water soluble white adhesive which most people were familiar with but knew under different names depending on their country of origin: ‘PVA glue' in the UK (from its chemical description, Poly Vinyl Acetate), ‘bondo' in Japan (from the word to ‘bond' and the trademark Bondex) and the trademark ‘Elmer's glue' in the United States.

 

 

Sunday 2 December 2001

With Mondays now well-established as the day off for the whole team, the aim is often to complete a task by Sunday night so that a new phase of construction can be started on the Tuesday. Decorating the paper structure is due to begin next week, but there is some anxiety as to the tight schedule within which the construction of the float has to be completed. Today, the idea is to finish the papering of the front, i.e. the human figure and the horse, so that next week Takashi Kitamura can begin to outline here the details of the design in ink and wax while work can begin on papering the back of the nebuta. Smaller sub-teams were forming and dispersing throughout the day as the team moved across the front of the structure. By mid-afternoon, the papering on Minaomoto no Yoshitsune was nearly done as Maki Fujiwara, Miyuki Oda and Keiko Osanai continued to work around the base of the figure. Meanwhile, the rest of the team focused on the horse with Takashi Kitamura working on the horse's mane, Tokuko Iwasaki completing the details on the horse's head, Reiko Kitamura and Tomoko Kitamura working on the neck, Akira Kitamura and Maximilian Marshall on the wings and volunteers getting on with the sections they have been appointed. Finally, the electrical wiring was completed by Katsutoshi and Michiko Funahashi. Final checks will still have to be done, but all the light-bulbs are installed and the nebuta is ready to be lit.

 

 

Monday 3 December 2001

The Nebuta Team are having a day off and will be back at work tomorrow.

 

  

Tuesday 4 December 2001

The back of the nebuta is a separate wood and wire structure that can be lifted apart from the rest of the float. It actually rests on its own wood grid, itself resting partly on the main base of the entire float and partly on the floor, held up by small wedges. The nebuta are often made thus in two, three or more parts. The space between the parts makes it possible to create a walkway with scaffold boards between and around all the parts and thus allowing access to the higher details of the wire structure. The back of a nebuta usually depicts a separate but complementary scene to the one on the front. Here, the back of the nebuta depicts the Buddhist figure Kannon on a lotus flower, floating in the cusp of a wave with the sea raging around it. This echoes the theme of this nebuta, ‘Minamoto no Yoshitsune crossing the sea', and the benevolent gods that helped him escape from certain death at the hands of his brother. Takashi Kitamura is particularly known for the water effects of his nebuta and he often chooses to depict dramatic scenes involving turbulent seas and crashing waves. Here, the shape of the wave is reminiscent of early 19th century Japanese woodblock prints and in particular perhaps the most famous one titled ‘Under the wave, off Kanagawa' by Katshushika Hokusai with a boat caught in the cusp of the wave. Such cross-references in the iconography of the nebuta are part of the visual puns contained in the imagery depicted on floats in Aomori.

 

 

Wednesday 5 December 2001

During the early part of the day, most of the women on the team prepared the area for the next stage of the process. One side of the nebuta display area was to be devoted to the ink and wax: the inks and brushes were laid out and the floor protected with cardboard and the table cleared to set out the hot plates on which the wax will be melted. Meanwhile on the other side, the area was prepared for the poster paints and the following materials were set out: brushes, over 20 small pots of poster paints, plastic containers in which the colours will be diluted and mixed, scrap paper for testing the intensity of the dye, and plenty of old newspapers which will be used to protect the sculpture beneath the areas where painting work is to be carried out. Meanwhile, since many of the scaffold boards had been moved around during the papering of the front of the nebuta, Takashi and Akira Kitamura re-arranged the boards to maximise access for decorating the float. Once this was ready, most of the team returned to papering on the back of the nebuta and the water details of the front. The details of the foam were made of wire in advance in Japan and shipped over with the other parts of the nebuta at the start of the project, but the overall shape of the two waves and their positioning was determined by eye by Takashi Kitamura during construction. The water features combine both areas that are relatively simple to cover in paper towards the base of the waves, but the foam requires more time and attention. This made them ideal for training those on the team with less experience in papering. Takashi Kitamura's fame for water details goes hand in hand with a reputation for making the task particularly difficult for the papering team!
