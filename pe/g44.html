The Horological Gallery

The gallery is arranged to show the development of mechanical clocks and watches from the medieval period onwards. There are 235 watches and about 100 clocks in the exhibition, approximately thirty of which can be seen working, some striking and chiming the hours. Area 7 includes working models of all the most common escapements used in watches.

The gallery is divided into twelve separate areas and also features a special display of scientific instruments with varying themes.

Glimpse of a Universe - Scientific Instruments from Renaissance Germany

‘Germany’ before the late 19th century was not a unified country, but consisted of a changing number (at times more than 300) of semi-independent principalities, loosely organized under the Emperor of the Holy Roman Empire. 

The instruments shown in this small display were made in the late 15th to the early 17th centuries, a time when not only the Habsburg emperors, but also regional and episcopal courts were increasingly occupied with the pursuit of knowledge, and the understanding of nature and the cosmos. The instruments were mainly designed for astronomical, horological, surveying or gunnery purposes. They were all made in the Germanic tradition, commonly followed  throughout central Europe, and  particularly in centres such as Brunswick, Augsburg, Nuremberg, Vienna and Prague.

  

Spectacular Loans-in

The Baldewein Globe: a spectacular example of the clockmakerand goldsmith’s art, made in 1575 for the Landgrave Wilhelm IV of Hesse-Kassel by Eberhard Baldewein, Herman Diepel and Hans Bucher. This spectacular celestial globe is designed as a mechanical representation of the heavens and is adjustable for use at different latitudes. On loan from the Schroder Collection. 

The Burgundian table clock: one of only two surviving examples of a spring driven clock made in Burgundy during the reign of Philip the Good in the mid-15th century. On loan from the Victoria & Albert Museum, London.

The Horizontal table clock made by Jacob Zech in 1525: originally made for the King of Poland, this magnificent spring-driven table clock was made to show the hours, the position of the Sun and Moon in the Zodiac, and also indicates Bohemian hours, where the day began at sunset. It is the earliest dated clock with a fusee. On loan from the Society of Antiquaries of London.
