Lecturers

Dr. Naman Ahuja is a specialist on Indian art, with particular interests in Indian sculpture and Sultanate manuscript painting. His doctorate thesis on the iconographic and stylistic variety in early Indian terracotta sculpture was completed at SOAS (2001). He is a freelance lecturer and since 1997 he has tutored courses on Indian art at SOAS, Christies Education and the British Museum. He is currently a Research Fellow at the Ashmolean Museum, Oxford, where he is writing a catalogue of their early Indian antiquities. Naman is also a practising potter, with a studio in Delhi where his work continues to be exhibited.

Dr. Daud Ali lectures in early Indian History and lecturer at SOAS. His area of specialisation is early medieval Indian history (c. 300-1200 CE). His areas of interest include the evolution of religious practices and ideas in classical and medieval India, the growth and spread of courtly culture, and the relationship between ethics, politics and aesthetics among Indian elites in early medieval India. He has written a number of articles on these and related topics, working with both Sanskrit and Tamil sources, and has co-authored, with Ronald Inden and Jonathan Walters, Querying the Medieval: Texts and the History of Practice in South Asia (Oxford University Press, 2000). A full length study of early Indian court life is currently in press, entitled Courtly Culture and Political Life in Early Medieval India (forthcoming, [2004] Cambridge University Press).

Dr. Sabiha al-Khemir obtained an MA (with distinction) and a PhD in the History of Islamic Art and Archaeology from the University of London. Her work in the field of Islamic Art has included consultancy for the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York for their major exhibition >Al-Andalus, Islamic Arts of Spain= (1992). She also wrote and presented two documentaries on Islamic Art, broadcast by Channel Four television (1993). She has published several articles and essays and has completed a forthcoming book Figures and Figurines from the 7th to the 19th centuries, which is the first book on the subject on sculpture form the Islamic World. Her first novel Waiting in the Future for the Past to Come was published by Quartet Books in 1993. Her second novel The Blue Manuscript is forthcoming. Her own art work has appeared in several publications: she fully illustrated Le Nuage Amoureux (Paris, Maspero 1979) and The Island of Animals (London, Quartet Books, 1994). Her drawings have toured in several exhibitions including, Forces of Change (USA 1994-95) and Dialogue of the Present (UK 1999-2000). She lectures worldwide on Islamic Art and various cultural topics. She also works as museum consultant on Islamic art projects. Sabiha was tutor for the Islamic art module (SOAS-Christies) in 2000 and has been tutor of the Islamic Art module in the British Museum since 2001.

Prof. James Allan is Keeper of the Department of Eastern Art in the Ashmolean Museum, in Oxford, and specialises in Islamic Art. He teaches Islamic art in the Oriental Faculty, Oxford University. He has written books on Islamic metalwork and ceramics and has also produced a revised edition of K. A. C. Creswells's A Short Account of Early Muslim Architecture.

Dr. Meri Arichi studied Art History in London and Florence and worked at Christie's in London from 1989-93. She obtained a SOAS/Sotheby's Diploma in Asian Arts in 1994, and an MA (1995) and a PhD. (2003) in History of Japanese Art from SOAS, University of London. Her doctorate thesis focuses on Shinto -Buddhist syncretic mandalas from Medieval Japan. She has contributed to the exhibitions "Shinto: Sacred Art of Ancient Japan" (2001) and "Kazari: Decoration and Display in Japan" (2003) at the British Museum.

Professor Gina Barnes is Professor of Japanese Studies, Dept. of East Asian Studies, Durham University and specializes East Asian archaeology, especially Japanese archaeology and prehistory/protohistory. Her current fieldwork concerns Tectonic Archeology in Japan. She founded the Society for East Asian Archaeology and is a member of several related Boards and Councils. She has published many books and numerous articles.

Marie-Therese Barrett is the course Tutor and a freelance lecturer. She studied Japanese Art History in the international section of Sophia University Tokyo, and subsequently, completed an M.A. in Art History from the University of Maryland (1989). She then taught a Survey of Japanese Art both at Temple University Japan and the Tokyo American Club (1990-1999) while lecturing free-lance for a variety of cultural organizations. Since her return to London, she has taught on the SOAS- Christie's course, and lectured for the Japan Society, Asia House and the National Association of Decorative and Fine Arts Societies. She has published articles for the Japan Times, Monumenta Nipponica, the Asiatic Society of Japan, the Japan Society, and the Asian Art Newspaper.

Dr. Barbara Brend read French at Cambridge, England, but, moved by travel, she later took an M.Phil and a PhD in Islamic Art and Architecture at the School of Oriental and African Studies, London. She is an independent scholar, but has been associated with the British Museum and British Library since 1979. She has taught a course in Islamic Art at the University of East Anglia, and on Indian Painting at SOAS. Her principal research is into form and meaning in Persian and Mughal painting. Books: Islamic Art; The Emperor Akbar's Khamsa of Nizami; Perspectives on Persian Painting.

Dr. Crispin Branfoot is Course Leader of the MA in South Asian Arts in the Faculty of Art & Design, De Montfort University, Leicester (www.dmu.ac.uk/artandesign). He completed an MA and a PhD at SOAS. His teaching and research interests are concerned with Hindu, Buddhist and Jain art and architecture, especially in South India between c.1200 and 1800. His articles have appeared in many journals including South Asian Studies, The Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society, East & West and Artibus Asiae. Before joining De Montfort University in 2000, he worked at the British Museum (Oriental Antiquities), the Ashmolean Museum (Eastern Art) in Oxford, and taught the history of South Asian Art at SOAS for six years.

Prof. Brian Bocking (BA Lancaster, MA, PhD Leeds) is currently Professor of the Study of Religions at SOAS (the School of Oriental and African Studies) University of London. He previously taught at Stirling, Tsukuba (Japan) and Bath. His interests range from early Chinese Buddhist thought to Shinto iconology and modern Japanese religions. His publications include Nagarjuna in China (Edwin Mellen Press, 1995), A Popular Dictionary of Shinto (Curzon Press 1996) and The Oracles of the Three Shrines: Windows on Japanese Religion (Curzon Press 2001).

Dr. John Breen (M.A. , PhD Cantab) is senior lecturer in the Department of Japan and Korea and Chair of the Japan Research Centre at SOAS. He is co-editor of Shinto in history: the ways of the kami, Curzon-Hawaii, 2000 and has published widely on 19th century Japanese history in English and Japanese. He is editor of the forthcoming Death in Japan, a special issue of the journal Mortality.

Dr. Elaine Buck has an MA and a PhD from the University of London (SOAS) in Chinese art and archaeology. The title of my doctoral thesis is : The Eight Immortals on Yuan and Ming Ceramics. I teach Chinese art history and Chinese history for Birkbeck, the University of Reading and the WEA. In addition she lectures on Chinese art for the British Museum and the Victoria and Albert Museum.

Dr. Sheila Canby is an Assistant Keeper of the Islamic collections, Chair of the Standing Committee of the Department of Asia and Convenor of the Classical and Decorative Arts of the Islamic World. She is author of Persian Painting (1991); Princes, Poets and Paladins (1998); The Golden Age of Persian Art (1999), and editor of Safavid Art and Architecture(2002). She is a specialist in the Arts of Iran and Central Asia.

Dr. John Carpenter is Donald Keene Lecturer in Japanese Art at SOAS, University of London, and Head of the London Office of the Sainsbury Institute for the Study of Japanese Arts and Cultures. His research interests range from the history of East Asian calligraphy to early-modern Japanese painting and prints. He assisted in the editing and translating of Hokusai Paintings: Selected Essays (1994) and the forthcoming sequel volume, Hokusai and His Age. He is co-author of The Frank Lloyd Wright Collection of Surimono (1995) and Jewels of Japanese Printmaking: Surimono of the Bunka-Bunsei Era, 1804-1830(2000). Among his museum publications are essays and catalogue entries for Kunisada's World (Japan Society, 1993), Japan's Golden Age: Momoyama (Dallas Museum of Art, 1996) and Edo: Art in Japan 1615-1868 (National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C., 1998).

Dr. Jane Casey is an art historian specializing in Himalayan art . She received her graduate and undergraduate degrees at Harvard University and is author of over twenty-five books and articles on Himalayan art. She was co-curator of Sacred Visions (1998), an exhibition of early Tibetan paintings organised by The Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York; and most recently (2003), curator of Divine Presence: Arts of India and the Himalayas, the inaugural exhibition at Casa Asia (Asia House) in Barcelona .

Tim Clark is a the Curator of Pictorial Art in the Asian CulturesDepartment. He is a leading authority on Ukiyo-e art. He is author of Ukiyo-e Paintings in The British Museum; Demon of Painting; The Art of Kawanabe Kyosai and co-author of Rimpa Art; and is curated the exhibition '100 views of Fuji' in 2001 as part of the festival of Japan 2001.

Rosemary Crill, is Deputy Curator of the Indian & South-East Asian Department of the Victoria & Albert Museum. She specialises in Indian textiles and paintings, and her publications include 'Marwar Painting: a history of the Jodhpur style' Mumbai, 1999) and 'Indian Embroidery' (London, 2000).

Dr. Heather Elgood is the Course Director, Convenor and Tutor of the 'Classical and Decorative Arts of India'. Formerly Deputy Director of SOAS/Sotherby's Asian arts programme and Academic Director of the SOAS and Christie's 'Arts of Asia and Africa Programme'. Specialist in Persian, Jain, Sultanate and Mughal manuscript painting as well as the ritual arts of India. Author of Hinduism and the Religious Arts, London 1999.

Dr. Robert Elgood is a freelance writer and author with special interest in Islamic and Indian Arms and Armour. Editor of Islamic Arms and Armour, author or Arms and Armour of Arabia, and Firearms of the Islamic World. Currently working on a book on firearms of Greece and her neighbours.

Dr. Rupert Faulkner is Senior Curator, Japanese Art, in the Asian Department of the Victoria and Albert Museum. Born in Yokohama and educated in Britain, he graduated from Cambridge University with a degree in Japanese Studies in 1977. He spent the next seven years, three of them at Nagoya University, conducting research into Japanese ceramic history. His doctoral thesis on the development of Seto and Mino wares from the Kamakura to early Edo periods was accepted by Oxford University in 1988. He joined the V&A in 1984 and was involved in preparations leading to the opening of the Toshiba Gallery of Japanese Art in December 1986. He has since been responsible for the care, development and study of the V&A's collections of ukiyo-e woodblock prints, Japanese ceramics and contemporary Japanese crafts. Recent publications include Japanese Studio Crafts: Tradition and the Avant-Garde (1995), Hiroshige Fan Prints (2001) and Tea: East and West (2003). During 2001-2002 he was joint coordinator of the V&A's extensive programme of Japan 2001 events.

Prof. Geza Fehervari is a Professor Emeritus at the University of London and has since held the position of Hungarian Ambassador to Kuwait, Bahrain, the UAE and Oman before becoming curator of the Tareq Rajeb Msueum, Kuwait, which he has now retired from. Has researched and taken part in numerous excavations in the Middle East as well as being widely published.

Sylvia Fraser-Lu was born in Invercargill New Zealand, she graduated in 1964 with an MA in History from Otago University and a Diploma in Teaching from Dunedin Teachers' College. In 1967 she joined Volunteer Service Abroad (New Zealand Peace Corps) and was sent to serve for two years as a teacher in a newly established Malaysian government secondary school in Kuching Sarawak. It was there that she became interested in Asian culture and languages. Later she joined the New Zealand Department of Foreign Affairs and was sent to Beijing to assist in the opening of the New Zealand Embassy (1973 - 1974).

She moved to Taiwan in 1976 where she continued her studies in Chinese language, brush painting, scroll mounting and Chinese cookery. She also took Chinese art courses at the National Palace Museum in Taipei and began writing on Asian arts and crafts for various publications such as 'Arts of Asia', Oriental Art and Asia Magazine. Shortly after her marriage to a U.S, Foreign Service officer in 1976, the Lus were assigned to Burma for three years. While serving as principal of the International School in Rangoon, she became greatly interested in Burmese art and traveled widely collecting information on little known subjects such as weaving and embroidery, 'opium' weights, frog drums, and lacquerware which became the basis for a series of articles for Arts of Asia and later a number of books

After being reassigned to Bangkok in 1980, she was appointed principal of the Elementary Section of International School, Bangkok as position she held before moving to Japan in 1986. By this time she was serving as series advisor for Oxford University Press' 'Images of Asia' and worked full-time on publications for Oxford such as Indonesian Batik: Processes, Patterns and Places, South-East Asian Silverware and Handwoven Textiles of South-East Asia. While in Japan she also was an active committee member of the Festival of Fibers, the CWAJ Lecture Series for 1988, sponsored by the College Women's Association of Japan and she wrote the essay giving an overview of Japanese textiles in an accompanying publication.

She now works full time as a writer-researcher on Asian arts and crafts and has served as program chair for the Asian American Forum.

Dr Annabel Teh Gallop is Curator for Indonesian and Malay and Head of the South and Southeast Asia section at the British Library, London. Her main research interests are in Malay manuscripts, letters, documents and seals, and the book arts of Islamic Southeast Asia. Her publications include 'Early views of Indonesia: drawings from the British Library' (London; British Library, 1995), 'The legacy of the Malay letter' (London: published by the British Library for the National Archives of Malaysia, 1994), and 'Golden letters: writing traditions of Indonesia', by Annabel Teh Gallop with Bernard Arps (London: British Library; 1991). In 2002 she received her Ph.D. from the School of Oriental and African Studies, University of London for her dissertation 'Malay seal inscriptions: a study in Islamic epigraphy from Southeast Asia'.

Prof. Drew Gerstle is Professor of Japanese Studies in the Department of Japanese, SOAS, University of London. From 2000 he has also been Director of the SOAS/UCL AHRB Centre for Asian and African Literatures. His research interests are Japanese drama, thought and art of the Edo period. His most recent book is Chikamatsu: Five Late Plays (Columbia UP, 2001). His current project is a study of 'Kabuki culture', particularly in Osaka from around 1780-1840. One aim is to show how Kabuki actors and their performances were a catalyst for woodblock prints and surimono, and how actors participated in poetry circles. Drew Gerstle and Tim Clark are planning an exhibition at the British Museum entitled, 'Creating Celebrity: Kabuki Actors, Poets and Artists in Early 19th Century Osaka', for May/June 2005, which will hopefully travel to Tokyo and Osaka as well. Please see the website www.soas.ac.uk/EastAsia/gerstle/ for a list of his publications.

Prof. Gerald Hawting is a Professor of the History of the Near and Middle East, School of Oriental and African Studies, University of London. His research interests lie in the emergence and early development of Islam in the Middle East, and a more general interest in the history of the Middle East, especially in the period from the rise of Islam until the beginning of the 19th century. As well as several articles in scholarly journals and similar publications, he has translated into English, to volumes of the Arabic History of al-Tabari (d.923 AD). He is also the author of a history of the Umayyad caliphate (Ad 661 -750) - The First Dynasty of Islam, London: Routledge, 2nd edition 2000; and of an exploration of the idea of Aidolatry@ in Islamic thought - The Idea of Idolatry and the Emergence of Islam, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1999.

Patricia Herbert studied Southeast Asian history at the School of Oriental & African Studies and at the University of Michigan. She lived for three years in Burma in the 1970s and has made several return visits over the years. From 1975-1998 she was Curator of the Southeast Asia Collections of the British Library. Her publications include articles and books on history and manuscript art of Burma as well as an annotated major bibliography of Burma (Oxford, ABC-Clio Press, 1991). She now works as an independent scholar and researcher and is Vice-Chairman of the educational trust, Prospect Burma.

Has written books on Islamic Architecture, Islamic Art and Architecture, and Imperial Images in Persian Painting; and is co-author of a book on Islamic Architecture in North Africa. Has edited 5 books and over 100 articles. His main specialities are Islamic architecture, painting and iconography, as well as Umayyad art.

In 1986 he moved to the then India Office Library to curate the collections of Indian drawings, prints, paintings and photographs. His major interests are in Indian manuscript paintings and also in the later styles of the 18th and 19th centuries. He has published extensively in journals on all areas of Indian painting after CE1000; his chief books are Krishna, a Hindu Vision of God, London, 1980; The Art of the Book in India, London, 1982 Indian Book Painting, London, 1986; Calcutta City of Palaces, London, 1990. He is currently working on a new edition of the complete Indian journals of William Daniell..

He received his doctorate in Art &Archaeology from Princeton University in 2000, with a dissertation on the pivotal Yuan master Zhao Mengfu (1254-1322). He organised, with the British Museum, the June 2001 conference on the Admonitions Scroll attributed to the early figural master Gu Kaizhi (about 345-406), and is currently editing the proceedings for publication as a book.

Carol Michaelson is Assistant Keeper of Chinese art in the Department of Asia at the British Museum and Convenor of The Classical and Decorative Arts of China. She specialises in Ancient China, particularly the Qin and Han dynasties. Her main focus of interest is Chinese jade but she is also responsible for early bronzes and snuff bottles. She worked on the display for the Hotung Gallery for Oriental Antiquities and was a co-curator of jade, snuff bottle and archaeological exhibitions in 1995 and 1996. In 1999 she was sole curator of the exhibition Gilded Dragons and author of the catalogue. In November 2002 Carol curated and opened a long-term loan display of Chinese jade in the newly refurbished gallery 33b, now the Selwyn and Ellie Alleyne Gallery. For many years she taught Chinese art at SOAS (University of London) on the SOAS-Sothebys, and SOAS-Christies courses and has lectured extensively in the UK and abroad. For the last two years Carol has also been responsible for the digitisation of the Museum's collection of material from Dunhuang and surrounding areas. This is a long term project, being funded by the Mellon Foundation in America, with the aim of making this fragile material accessible to all on the internet.

Dr. George Michell was trained as an architect and now specialises in the architectural history of India. He has worked at many ancient sites, most recently at Hampi (Vijayanagara) in Karnataka. Among his recent books are Architecture and Art of Southern India, Vijayanagara and the Successor States (Cambridge University Press) and Royal Palaces of India (Thames and Hudson).

She organised(with Roderick Whitfield)the Treasures from Korea exhibition at the British Museum in 1984, and is author and editor of several books and many articles in professional journals and edited publications on Korean Art.

Rosemary Scott took a degree in Chinese Art & Archaeology at the School of Oriental and African Studies, University of London, where she went on to do postgraduate research into early Chinese lacquer and its relationship to other materials. In 1983 she became Curator of the Percival David Foundation of Chinese Art, SOAS, University of London, combining the running of the museum with undergraduate and postgraduate teaching and research. In 1995 she became the first Head of the new Museums Department at SOAS, responsible for both the David Foundation and the new Brunei Gallery.

In 1997 she joined Christie's as Senior Academic Consultant to the Asian Art departments. She undertakes research, publication and training as well organizing and giving lectures, seminars and courses on Asian art. She has curated a wide range of exhibitions and has researched and written numerous books and articles on Chinese ceramics, textiles and early lacquer. She has travelled extensively researching and lecturing in Asia, America, Australia and Europe. Her publications include: For the Imperial Court: Qing Porcelain from the Percival David Foundation of Chinese Art, 1997; 'The Chinese Imperial Collections' in Collecting Chinese Art: Interpretation and Display - Colloquies on Art & Archaeology in Asia No. 20, Percival David Foundation, London, 2000, pp. 19-32; 'Chinese Porcelain of the Yuan Dynasty', Serene Pleasure: The Jinglexuan Collection of Chinese Ceramics, Seattle Art Museum, 2001, pp. 18-29; and 'Introduction: Qingbai Porcelain and its Place in Chinese Ceramic History', in Qingbai Wares: Chinese Porcelain of the Song and Yuan Dynasties, Percival David Foundation of Chinese Art, London, 2002, pp. 6-14.

A teacher of meditation, he has been leading study tours to all parts of the area for many years.

Jacqueline Simcox graduated from Leeds University, with a joint BA Hons. degree: History of Art (European Art, Architecture and Sculpture) and English Literature. Dr. Jon Thompson works in the Department of Eastern Art in the Ashmolean Museum in Oxford. His special interest is in the Carpets and Textiles of the Islamic World. He has published books on carpets in the field.

Professor Roderick Whitfield is the Percival David Professor of Chinese & Asian Art at SOAS. He has published extensively on Chinese Buddhist Art, Painting and Archaeology.

Dr. Susan Whitfield is Director of the International Dunhuang Project at the British Library, organising the cataloguing and digitisation of Central Asian manuscripts in Chinese, Tibetan and other languages at the British Library, the National Library of China and other institutions worldwide. Her doctoral work at SOAS was on Tang dynasty history and she has published widely, including Life Along the Silk Road and Cave Temples of Dunhuang (co-author). Her most recent book is papers from a conference entitled Dunhuang Manuscript Forgeries. She is consultant editor on a series of Chinese art books and has translated other works on Chinese art, history and textiles.

Dr. Michael Willis is Senior Research Fellow and Professor of South Asian Studies, De Montfort University, and Visiting Curator, Department of Asia, British Museum. He has been a Visiting Professor at the College de France and at School of Oriental and African Studies, London. The author of several books on the architecture and sculpture of India, his most recent work was Buddhist Reliquaries from Ancient India (BM Press, 2000). Dr Willis is currently preparing a catalogue of the British Museum collection of north Indian temple sculpture.
