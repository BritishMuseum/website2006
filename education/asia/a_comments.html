"I attended the module on Chinese Decorative Arts and I believe this course excelled in several respects. It was particularly successful in meeting the expectations of a disparate group, all having different ambitions and backgrounds. The programme balanced well the historical context, the objects of study, their production techniques or creative processes and (so far aswe know, for the more ancient artifacts) the reason for their having been made. The course material was rich and was presented by an impressive (35 strong) teaching faculty, one of the largest I have encountered, and with very minor exceptions all excellent communicators and leading experts in their fields.

The most distinctive feature of the course, and I believe one which makes it unique globally, is the value of having expert lectures structured around objects similar to those which the students are able shortly after to view in the Museum's own galleries or in other collections nearby, or to examine in on-site handling sessions guided by first class tutors.

The course is clearly of the highest quality and fittingly reflects the reputation and international reputation of the Museum. I enjoyed it tremendously"
Clarence Eng
Retired International executive in Shell now studying Chinese Cultural History at SOAS, University of London
(China)

"The Japan and Korean module provides a wonderful opportunity to learn about the art history of Korea and Japan. The art originated from China but developed into a unique style. The course helps you to identify the difference between art from China, Korea and Japan, both through the lectures and handling sessions.

For those wishing to be a scholar in Asian art, this course provides a 'bridge' to a MA course. For those who are thinking of becoming an art dealer, this course is essential. For those who are simply interested in Asian art, you can enjoy the course (without, necessarily, the stress of taking exams!)

I used to work in journalism and finance for more than ten years. I decided to take this course as I was always interested in Asian art and I have collected Asian antiques as well. It's worth saying that the course is aimed at the "serious" student as it is very intensive and demanding, and extremely enjoyable!"
Stephanie Pakenham
Background in Journalism and Finance
(Japan & Korea)

"After living in Asia for many years, in Hong Kong, Malaysia, Singapore and Thailand, and working there as a volunteer museum guide, it was very important for me to be able to continue my interest in Asian art and history when I returned to England. I found the answer in the British Museum Diploma in Asian Art. There, surrounded by a great mix of people, some young and some like me, rather more mature, I learned about China, India and South East Asia. The latter module covers the area where my heart is, and my deepening knowledge has only driven me on to want to learn more. The course lecturers are some of the most respected experts in their fields, and sympathetic tutors helped me back on the road to study, after an absence of 35 years. One of the very best aspects, was the opportunity to handle priceless examples of ceramics, sculpture, paintings and manuscripts, which teach you far more, than pictures in a book, or images on a screen. We also travelled to New York, and Paris to view museums, which added to the experience enormously. It is intensive, and stimulating and hard work....all the things which make me highly recommend it to anyone who wants to learn under the very best conditions."
Margaret Millard
(General)

"I found the Islamic art module of the British Museum Diploma in Asian Art fascinating. The Islamic module follows the historical development of the Islamic regions and their ruling dynasties. It explores the varied art forms of the Islamic lands from the rise of the Islamic faith following the death of the Prophet Muhammad in the seventh century to the modern age. Topics covered include architecture, calligraphy, miniature painting, ceramics, metalwork, carpets and textiles. The standard of lecturing on the course is outstanding with classes being given by world renowned experts who lecture worldwide. The highlight of the module was a three day trip to Paris to the Paris mosque and the Islamic collections at the Louvre and the Institute du Monde Arabe. I would strongly recommend the Islamic module to students considering pursuing the Asian Art Diploma."
Anna O'Connell
Legal Profession
Now planning to do a PhD.
(Islam)

"What is Islamic Art? If, like me, you're also one of the more 'mature' students on a course, I guarantee you'll begin a genuinely fascinating journey. As you hunt that elusive knowledge you'll be in good company, well guided, well supported. One of your first guides will instantly challenge some stereotypes about Islam and its art. Dr al Khemir is a renowned Islamic art historian, a published author and illustrator, a motivational lecturer and an eloquent and passionate speaker. She is also a Muslim Arab woman. But, it is her inexhaustible ability to find connections between East and West, her generous spirit and charm, her own questioning intellect that inspired us to break through whatever blocks we had and really enter the spirit of learning.

The 12 weeks were fascinating. Hard work, tiring at times. Yes, there are essays and a final exam. But now when I'm asked the question 'What is Islamic Art?' I can truthfully answer that it is far more than I thought - more diverse, more extraordinary, more exquisite - from a simple white bowl adorned with black calligraphy, to the delicate tracery of an arabesque on a blue tile. The 15th century Persian poet Rumi wrote 'Inside you there's an artist you don't know about ... say yes quickly, if you know, if you've known it from before the beginning of the universe'. I would urge you to say yes to this module - it's an absolute jewel."
Christine Sharp
Former Journalist
(Islam)

"I found the Islamic art diploma a unique and truly enriching experience; more vivid, more vital - more joyous! - than my previous university degrees. Our small eclectic group began the module with little to no knowledge of Islamic art and culture, and over the allotted 12 weeks we learnt an astonishing amount. We were taught by a great breadth of scholars and specialists, each with a genuine love and enthusiasm for their subject, and each eager to share and communicate that passion with the students. We travelled to Paris and around the UK to view public and private Islamic art collections. We handled pieces of Islamic art, feeling their weight or delicacy, their coolness and texture; we 'connected' with objects that, without the course, we would only have glanced at in the cold, 2-dimensionality of books, or behind the glass of museum cabinets. I felt the great gift that the course gave us was a language, both visual and conceptual, that allowed us to see in carpets, calligraphy, ceramics, manuscripts that which we had previously been blind. Under the close personal supervision of our course tutors we began to appreciate the vibrant wealth of history, culture, philosophy, and art that resonate from Islamic art pieces.I found this also to be the case in the Indian and Chinese diplomas I took. I found the course was a tremendously valuable and enjoyable experience."
Fiona Buckee
