From the beginning of ancient Greek civilisation to the modern day, Greek myths have provoked reflection on the human condition. These stories of gods and heroes, of men and monsters once permeated all of Greek life, where they served to explain the world and the forces at work within it. These themes have been a source of inspiration to numerous artists, writers and composers throughout centuries and were explored in this year's BBC PROMS.

The Greeks would look to their family of gods not only to explain the mysterious workings of nature and the origins of life, but also, and above all, human issues, from the uncontrollable nature of human fate in the hands of arbitrary gods, to the inner depths of the human soul. In these myths, both ordinary people and super-human heroes are faced again and again with the most difficult aspects of human life: those perennial problems of passion and violence, justice and vengeance, of family conflict, and of the individual's relationship to society. More often than not, there is no happy ending, only a painful and tragic solution. The myth of the Trojan War, a tortuous 10-year conflict in which both sides are losers in the end, is perhaps the most poignantly perpetual example.

Although set in the mythical past, these stories were always acutely relevant to contemporary life. Greeks myths were never absolutely fixed but always open to re-working and re-moulding. This tradition of unfettered flexibility, and the profound and universal humanity of their themes, explains their enduring appeal.
