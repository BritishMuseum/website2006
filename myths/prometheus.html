The story of Prometheus is set right at the beginning of times, as a myth of the creation of mankind. According to the most common versions (also recounted by the Greek philosopher Plato), the gods had created mankind and other living creatures, and then asked the Titans Prometheus and his brother Epimetheus to endow all creatures with the appropriate means to ensure their survival. Man was last in line and when it came to his turn, nothing was left to give. Prometheus felt pity for the naked and defenceless humans and gave them fire which he stole from the smith god Hephaistos and the patron goddess of the arts Athena. Angry about this unauthorised gift, Zeus punished Prometheus by chaining him to a crag in the Caucasus mountains where every morning an eagle devoured Prometheus' liver only for him to grow it again during the night. He owed his ultimate rescue to Herakles.

In another version of the myth Prometheus is even more independent: it is Prometheus himself who creates man from clay and them proceeds to give them fire. He is a rebel against the gods, bringer of civilisation to mankind and someone who helps man to emancipate themselves from the gods and take fate into their own hands. As such his myth remained popular in western thought, not least as a symbol of indispensable even if ultimately futile defiance against all kinds of unjust rule and suppression.
