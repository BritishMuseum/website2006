Troy's doom was sealed when Paris was born. So declared Cassandra, the prophetic princess. King Priam and his wife Hecuba resolved to destroy their baby rather than endanger the city. But Paris, exposed to die, was rescued by shepherds. When he reached manhood he was obliged to judge which of three goddesses, Juno, Minerva or Venus was the most beautiful (Fig 1). All three offered bribes, but only Venus' offer of the most beautiful woman in the world proved irresistible. Paris awarded her the prize and in exchange Venus helped him to steal Helen, then married to a Greek king, away to Troy where Paris had been accepted back into the royal family (Fig 2).

The Greeks, enraged at the theft of Helen mustered a great army under the leadership of Agamemnon, brother of the aggrieved husband of Helen, and prepared to attack Troy. But before they could set out, in exchange for a fair wind, the goddess Diana demanded a dreadful sacrifice: Agamemnon's virgin daughter, Iphigeneia. The sacrifice was performed and the wind rose, but unknown to Agamemnon and his distraught wife, Clytemnestra, at the last moment, Diana had rescued the girl, substituting a deer in her place (Fig 3).

The Greeks then sailed to Troy. On the Greek side, Achilles proved the greatest warrior, while on the Trojan side, Priam and Hecuba's eldest son, Hector, became the chief defender of the city. But it was only after Hector had slain Achilles' beloved friend Patroclus, that Achilles actually met Hector in battle and killed him (Fig 4).

And yet Troy did not fall; the war continued. Achilles was killed; Paris was killed; but the city held out. Finally the Greeks devised a ruse: they pretended to sail home but left a huge hollow wooden horse which was filled with the pick of the Greek soldiery. The wise Cassandra pleaded that it not be brought into the city, but her advice was dismissed. The Trojans, believing the invasion had failed and the war was over, rejoiced, but in the dead of night, the Greeks hidden in the horse emerged, recalled their colleagues hidden nearby and sacked the city.

Helen was retrieved by her husband and brought back to Greece (Fig 5); Priam was killed (Fig 6), Cassandra, wrenched from the image of the goddess to which she clung for sanctuary, was raped (Fig 7) and then became the captive of the Greek commander, Agamemnon, while Andromache, faithful wife of Hector, was given as concubine to the son of Achilles. Hector and Andromache's baby, the hope of Troy, was thrown to his death from the walls (also Fig 6). In one last cruel gesture, the Greeks slew Polyxena, a virgin daughter of Priam and Hecuba, sacrificing an innocent Trojan maiden at the end of the war in cruel symmetry with the sacrifice of the Greek maiden, Iphigeneia, at the beginning.

Agamemnon's homecoming was joyless. His resentful wife, Clytemnestra, with the aid of her lover, Aegisthus, murdered him and Cassandra upon their arrival. Years passed before Agamemnon's son, Orestes, and daughter, Electra, could avenge his death by killing their mother and her paramour. To the only surviving member of the Trojan royal family, fate was kinder. Aeneas, cousin of Hector, escaped from the fallen city with his father and little son Ascanius. They set off for Italy, to settle such Trojans as had escaped the sack and would ultimately be the founders of Rome. Along the way they stopped at Carthage, where the Queen, Dido, fell in love with Aeneas. But fate overruled passion and Aeneas, following his destiny, was forced to abandon the besotted Dido, who found refuge only in death.

Fig 1: Paris sits at the right playing the lyre and minding his sheep while Juno (identified by her sceptre) hands him the golden apple that he is supposed to give to the fairest of the three goddesses. Minerva stands in the middle (identified by her snake-fringed aegis and spear); Venus comes climactically last, her charms carefully concealed - to be spectacularly revealed at the right moment. Athenian red-figure hydria (water jar), about 470 BC GR 1873.9.20.353 (Vases E 178)
Fig 2: Paris (far left) and Helen (centre) gaze at each other amorously, while Venus (far right) complacently observes her handiwork and Cupid, his job done, sits at her feet. Campanian red-figure situla (bucket), about 350-340 BC GR 1928.7-19.3
Fig 3: Iphigeneia approaches the altar behind which stands the priest with his knife ready to sacrifice her. She is shadowed by the deer that at the last moment will be substituted for her. Apulian red-figure krater (mixing bowl), about 360-350 BC GR 1865.1-3.21 (Vases F 159)

Fig 4: In the centre the two greatest heroes of the Trojan war confront each other. Achilles (left) charges forward irrepressibly while Hector, already wounded in two places, falls back. Athenian red-figure krater (mixing bowl), about 500-480 BC GR 1848.8-4.1 Vases E 468
Fig 5: Menelaus, the husband of Helen, pursues his wife (far right) after the fall of Troy. He intended to kill her in punishment for all the trouble she had caused but at the sight of her beauty, his sword drops from his hand. At the far left a handmaiden flies in terror. Athenian red-figure amphora (storage jar), about 470-450 BC GR 1837.6-9.71 (Vases E 263

Fig 6: An image captures the horror of the sack of Troy: the double murder of king Priam and his grandson, the son of Hector. Priam has been pushed down on the altar (that should have provided him with sanctuary) and is about to be battered to death by the baby held in the raised right hand of the warrior to the left, Neoptolemus, the son of Achilles. Athenian black-figure amphora (storage jar), about 540 BC GR 1842.2-3-14.3 (Vases B 205)

Fig 7: The ill-fated, innocent Trojan princess Polyxena has her throat cut over an altar, her log-like body firmly held by three Greek warriors, while Neoptolemus, the son of Achilles, plunges the sword in. Athenian black-figure amphora (storage jar), about 570-550 BC GR 1897.7-27.2
