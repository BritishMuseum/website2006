Greek god Apollo was the son of Zeus and Leto, born on Delos, which became his sacred island. Unlike his twin sister Artemis, the goddess of hunting, he was more closely associated with the art of music. His musical instrument, the lyre, was an invention by the Greek messenger god Hermes, who, as a child, escaped from his cradle to lead away and hide the flocks of Apollo. Apollo discovered the theft and the ensuing quarrel between the gods was ended only by the intervention of Zeus. As part of their reconciliation Apollo allowed Hermes to look after his flocks and in turn received the lyre.

As god of music, Apollo himself was said to have invented the kithara, a more complex ancient stringed instrument, and was the leader of the Muses, the patron figures of all the arts. His music is all-powerful, it gives stability and brings order into chaos.

Apollo was also the divinity of the sun, closely associated with the god Helios, whose fiery chariot travelled across the sky each day chasing away the stars that had been occupying it during the night. But it is particularly his image as a leader of the Muses and a patron of music that has dominated Western art since the Renaissance.
