Further reading

 


The story of Phaedra is most effectively rendered by the Greek playwright Euripides in his play Hippolytus. The Greek goddess of love, Aphrodite, is spurned by Hippolytos, son of the Greek hero Theseus and chaste devotee of the goddess of hunting, Artemis. Enraged, Aphrodite impels Hippolytos' stepmother Phaedra to fall desperately in love with her stepson. Phaedra confesses her feelings to her nurse who relates them to a horrified Hippolytos; and Phaedra, in shame, commits suicide. An incriminating note which she leaves for Theseus completes the tragedy: it leads to Hippolytos' death on account of Theseus' curse of his son, through an encounter with a monstrous bull that causes Hippolytos' chariot to crash. The psychological tensions between members of the family, particularly those of forbidden love and of chastity versus desire have guaranteed the story a long life in Western imagination.
