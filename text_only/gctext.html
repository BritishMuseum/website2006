The Great Court

 

Introduction

On 7 December 2000, the Queen Elizabeth II Great Court, at the heart of The British Museum, opened to the public.The two-acre square, enclosed by a spectacular glass roof, has transformed the Museum's inner courtyard, with the world-famous Reading Room at its centre, into the largest covered public square in Europe.
Designed by Foster and Partners, the £100 million project has been supported by grants of £30 million from the Millennium Commission and £15.75 million from the Heritage Lottery Fund.

The courtyard was one of the lost spaces of London, hidden from public view since 1857. The relocation and opening of the British Library at its St Pancras site, has enabled valuable space within the Museum to be utilised to the benefit of the Museum's visitors. The Great Court increases public space in the Museum by forty per cent, allowing visitors to move freely around the Main floor for the first time in 150 years. Once in the Great Court, visitors can choose from a number points of entry into the galleries. There is direct access west into the Egyptian Sculpture Gallery, east into the King's Library and north into the new Wellcome Gallery of Ethnography. Inside the courtyard itself, two monumental staircases encircle the drum of the Reading Room and lead to the Joseph Hotung Great Court Gallery, and the Court Restaurant. From the restaurant level a bridge link takes visitors into the upper galleries of the Museum.

 

Glass Roof and South Portico

As part of the Great Court project, the 1870s extension to the Museum's Weston Great Hall has been removed and a new southern portico has been reinstated using over 1,000 tonnes of stone.

As well as housing lifts providing access to the upper floor, the new South portico has three large portals through which the majority of visitors gain their first view of the Great Court.

Integral to the project on the South side of the site is the re-landscaping of the Museum forecourt to provide a fitting threshold to the refurbished Weston Great Hall and the Great Court. The Museum's Montague Place entrance has been similarly enhanced by the reinstatement of the railings removed at the time of the Second World War. Faithful copies of the original cast iron gates and railings were specially commisioned and now create an impressive feature on the north side of the Museum This sequence of linked spaces forms an important part of a new cultural route from the British Library at St Pancras to Covent Garden.

The Great Court's magnificent glass and steel roof was designed by computer. Work on the roof began in September 1999, and the 478-tonne steel structure, which supports 315 tonnes of glass has been built like a giant jigsaw puzzle. The installation of the glass panels was completed in July . The 11kms of roof structure has been built to an astonishing accuracy of 3mms.

 

The Round Reading Room

At the heart of the Great Court is the magnificent Reading Room. When completed in 1857, it was immediately hailed as one of the great sights of London. Despite being amongst the most famous rooms in the world, it has only been generally accessible to those with a Reader's ticket. its opening to the public on December 7, begins a new chapter in its distinguished history and is for the first time, available to all Museum visitors. Its magnificent interior has been carefully restored, including the repair of the papier mâché interior of the dome and reinstatement of the 1857 azure-blue, cream and gold decorative scheme devised by Sydney Smirke.It houses the Paul Hamlyn Library, a new public reference resource library of some 25,000 books, catalogues and other printed material focusing on the world cultures represented in The British Museum.

Complementing the library and part of the Walter and Leonore Annenberg centre within the Reading Room, is COMPASS, a multi-media public access system, which offers a multitude of possibilities for exploring the Museum's collections and enables users to gain a fuller understanding of objects and their context. Both the Paul Hamlyn Library and the Annenberg Centre's COMPASS facilities are conceived as new public resources for those who wish to explore the Museum's collections in new ways. Together they bridge the gap between the general enquiry services offered by the Information desks and the specialist research facilities of the Student's Rooms. High quality image printing and photocopying services will be available for visitors.

 

Clore Education Centre

Housed in the newly created lower level of the Great Court, the Clore Education Centre comprises the BP Lecture Theatre; the Hugh and Catherine Stevenson Theatre; the Raymond and Beverley Sackler Seminar Room; the Studio, used for art and craft activities; the Claus Moser seminar roomand the Ford Centre for Young Visitors. The Clore Education Centre will enable the Museum to expand its educational role in many new directions. Its two auditoria are home to a daily programme of lectures, film and videos, as well as conferences, concerts and other performances related to cultural festivals or special exhibitions. Five additional multi-purpose rooms are used for other programmes ranging from informal drop-in' sessions to courses for the general public and teacher training.

The Young Visitor Centre provides dedicated facilities and a range of tailor-made educational programmes for the hundreds of thousands of young people who visit annually in school and college groups. At weekends and during school holidays these areas will be used for family and community events.

 

New Galleries

The Great Court will also encompass new permenant gallery space. In 1970, The British Museum's Department of Ethnography was moved to the Museum of Mankind off Piccadilly because of the lack of space at Bloomsbury. Since then the collections have been displayed in a series of temporary exhibitions. As a major part of the Museum's development plans, the Ethnography collections are being brought back to Bloomsbury to be displayed in new permanent galleries in the Great Court (Sainsbury African Galleries). The return of the Ethnography collections is of vital importance to the Museum's aim of portraying a complete picture of human achievement and will enable the works of recent non-western societies to be seen in their proper context among other world cultures, past and present. An important element of the Sainsbury Galleries is a dedicated area for changing displays, focusing particularly on contemporary works. The Sainsbury African Galleries link to planned new galleries for American, Asian, European and Pacific cultures on the north side of the museum.

The Joseph Hotung Great Court Gallery is the Museum's major new space for temporary exhibitions, inaugurated by Human Image (7 December 2000- 11 February 2001). This is an ambitious and comprehensive examination of representations of the human form drawn from across the entire range of the Museum's collections. Life-size works form the focal point of the exhibition, ranging from Africa to the Far East, and Ancient Greece to the Americas.

 

Opening times

The Great Court will provide The British Museum with a well equipped building for the six million visitors it receives each year and will offer these visitors an excellent standard of visitor service.

The Great Court's extended opening hours (see below) will enable the Museum to develop new audiences, including those living and working locally. During the evening, visitors will be able to attend a lecture, film or concert in the Clore Education Centre, view a special exhibition in the Joseph Hotung Great Court Gallery, expand their understanding of collections in the Reading Room, enjoy a meal in the Court Restaurant or simply relax with friends in the café areas. The Great Court, Europes largest covered public square will inevitably become a new cultural venue for London and a destination attraction in itself.
