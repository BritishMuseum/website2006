The South Portico

 

Designed by Foster and Partners, The Great Court is the British Museum's exciting and imaginative development of Sir Robert Smirke's imposing inner courtyard, completed around 1850 but never used by the visiting public. After a few years the Reading Room was built at its centre, the surrounding areas becoming clogged with bookstacks, outbuildings and storage. For nearly 150 years, the courtyard was forgotten and neglected, one of the great hidden spaces of London. As part of the Great Court,The British Museum's magnificent South Portico is an entirely new construction in fresh stone. 65 feet high, it differs from the original (demolished in 1877 to make way for an extension of the Front Hall) in that it has three portals to allow entrance to the Great Court for large numbers of visitors, and houses lifts providing access to the upper floor.

The South Portico has become controversial because the contractor chose to deceive the Project Team by supplying as Portland a French oolitic limestone possessing very similar characteristics.With full regard to professional advice the building proceeded. The lighter appearance of the Portico compared to the adjacent weathered stonework is to be expected irrespective of the type of fresh stone employed. Pressure washing soon to be undertaken will remove impacted dust from the pores of the stone, a process which will mellow its hue.

The Great Court opened to the public on 7 December 2000. We would like to hear your views please e-mail southportico@thebritishmuseum.ac.uk
