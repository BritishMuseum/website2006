Methods and Materials

Cuneiform tablets

Cuneiform tablets were first made in the third millennium BC in Mesopotamia. They were made by incising script on clay tablets which were then sun baked. Cuneiform tablets provide an unparalleled resource for scholars of the ancient Mesopotamian civilisation. The British Museum holds a collection in excess of 120,000 of these tablets, which originated from twenty two major sites and is possibly the largest in the world.

Cuneiform tablets are fragile, having only been sun baked and are often contaminated with salt. Scholars need to handle tablets to decipher the text. The collection is very frequently consulted and consequently undergoes heavy handling. A conservation treatment was developed in the 19th Century to impart structural strength and allow the tablets to be handled without resultant damage. The treatment was introduced into the British Museum in the 1950s and consisted of heating the tablets slowly to 740°C, cooling, and then desalinating by immersion in running water. No attempt was made to characterise the clays, or to establish whether the same firing cycle would be appropriatefor groups of tablets from different sites. In the 40 years since the treatment was introduced in the British Museum some difficulties have been experienced with tablets from several locations; these present a variety of problems.
