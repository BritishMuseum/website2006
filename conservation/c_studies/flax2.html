Black-dyed Maori textiles

Creating the black dye

The dyeing process uses two stages. First, the white fibre is soaked in a bath of boiling water which also contains the bark or branches of certain trees or shrubs. Two of the most commonly used plants are known by the Maori names manuka (Leptospermum scoparium) and hinau (Elaeocarpus dentatus). Tannins from the plants dissolve in the water and are then absorbed into the fibre. In the second stage of the dyeing the fibre is soaked in a special black, iron-containing mud. During the immersion a black dye is formed in the fibre. The dye is formed by a chemical reaction between the iron in the mud and the tannin. The mechanism for formation of this dye was first proven by research in the British Museum's Department of Conservation.
