Dust Deposition


The British Museum is currently undergoing radical change with the implementation of the Great Court scheme within the Museum's central courtyard. This major project, supported by grants from the Millennium Commission and Heritage Lottery Fund, includes the restoration of the historic Reading Room and the creation of a Centre for Education, exhibition galleries and vastly improved visitor services. The entire courtyard will be spanned by a glass and steel roof, designed by the schemes architects, Foster and Partners. The first stage of the project entailed the demolition of the redundant book stacks surrounding the Reading Room that have been vacated by the British Library move to the new Library building at St Pancras.

From the earliest planning stages of the project, there was concern that the building works would generate particulate matter which might infiltrate adjacent galleries and deposit on objects. This in turn could lead to an increased need for conservation treatments. Dust is hygroscopic, acting as a nucleation point for moisture and pollutant gases in the atmosphere, and can cause accelerated deterioration. Every measure was taken to minimise the generation of dust on the Great Court site. The area was thoroughly sealed, water sprays were in constant use and working methods were selected for their propensity to produce low levels of dust.
