Methods and Materials

Cuneiform tablets

A major project was initiated in 1997 to study the conservation process and enable improvements to be made. The projects aims are:

  • to analyse the clays from which tablets were made and determine if there are differences between the clays used at different sites
  • to determine the chemical transitions occurring during the firing process and to optimise the process to minimise the stress generated by these transitions. This work is being carried out as a joint project with Birkbeck College, University of London.

The graphs illustrate a thermal analysis of a sample from a Babylonian cuneiform tablet. The blue line shows the weight loss as the material is heated. This is due to reactions in the clay producing gases. The red line is the amount of heat energy absorbed by the material during the heating. Peaks show when various reactions occur. The clays react and lose chemically bound water at temperatures below 600°C; and these reactions cause the desired increase in strength.

 The major reaction at 690°C is due to the decomposition of calcite. This reaction liberates carbon dioxide gas, which can generate high pressures and stress in the tablets. An ideal firing would allow these clay reactions to occur whilst avoiding the potentially damaging calcite decomposition reaction.
