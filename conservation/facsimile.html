Making facsimiles

Facsimiles are made for the British Museum for study purposes, for display, or for giving to owners as part of an acquisition agreement. The British Museum Company Ltd require exact copies to be used as `masters' for the commercial production of replicas, which will then be sold to the public. A limited number of facsimiles of objects are also manufactured on a commercial basis for other institutions, academics and for the public.

The technicians in the Facsimile Service specialise in the manufacture of high quality replicas, principally made of metal objects, stone, ceramic, ivory and bone, which are part of the collections of the British Museum.

The work involves taking a mould directly from the surface of the antiquity using a material which picks up the fine surface detail. The technicians work closely with the conservators to ensure the safety of the objects during this process. Moulds can be simple or complex, consisting of anything from one to 20 or more pieces.

Once the mould has been removed from the original object and reassembled, a cast is then made using plaster of Paris, synthetic resin, or by depositing a thin layer of copper metal, a process known as electroforming. The cast/electroform is then coloured, either by plating the metal surface with gold, silver or brass or by using pigments, to imitate the colour of the original. The production of these replicas is a time consuming and precise process, with complex objects taking several months to complete.
