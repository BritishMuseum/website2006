Reconstructions

Dry lacquer statue of Buddha

Most lacquered objects are made from wood to which the lacquer has been applied as a decorative coating. Dry lacquer objects differ in that they are built up from layers of pastes made by mixing lacquer with various powdered materials, such as saw dust. Though strong, dry lacquer can be brittle, especially when formed into thick layers.

This Burmese statue of Buddha in the early Kanboung style of the late 18th century had been broken into 73 pieces (above) varying in size from the almost complete upper torso to pieces only 15 centimetres square; the average thickness was 1.5cm. The individual fragments were strong and in general made a good join with neighbouring pieces. After cleaning, the pieces were reassembled using a suitable adhesive.

A lightweight but strong glass-fibre support was constructed to fit inside the base of the object (below). Missing decorative mouldings were replaced by taking a wax mould of undamaged areas and casting replacement parts in polyester resin paste. This material was also used to fill cracks and losses, which were painted to tone with the original surface (left).


The statue can be seen in the Joseph E Hotung Gallery of Oriental Antiquities
