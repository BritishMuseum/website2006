History

The British Museum was founded in 1753, but little evidence relating to the conservation of the collections has survived from the early days. By the mid-19th century, however, specialists in restoration were employed by the Trustees, of whom the best known were John Doubleday (first restorer of the Portland Vase) and Robert Ready, who succeeded Doubleday in c.1858. At least two of Ready's sons also served the Trustees, having their own workshop until the late 1930s.

Information on conservation in the 19th century is hard to come by as no records were kept of what was done to individual objects. However, occasional references in minutes of the meetings of the board of Trustees or in letters by curators give us glimpses of some processes, such as the use of fuller's earth as a poultice for cleaning sculpture and the application of acids for cleaning bronzes.

Other sources of information include a few drawings, which form part of the registered collections, and the objects themselves, post re-restoration, following initial treatment in the 19th or early 20th centuries. Modern techniques of chemical analysis are now used to identify the materials used to restore antiquities in the past. This process has enabled an archival catalogue to be compiled which lists the materials and methods employed before the days of record keeping. From this work, the main substances used were animal glue, shellac and plaster of Paris, supplemented by a variety of natural gums and resins.
