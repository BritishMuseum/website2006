History

The end of the World War I in 1918 brought about a major change in conservation at the British Museum as, when crates of objects were opened following war-time storage in the underground railway tunnels or in deep basements in the Museum itself, many antiquities were found to have deteriorated . The main problems were salt crystals forming on the surface of ceramics and porous stone (efflorescence), mould growth on organic materials, foxing on paper, and active corrosion on archaeological bronzes and iron objects. As a result, the Department of Scientific and Industrial Research employed Dr Alexander Scott FRS in 1919 as a consultant to The British Museum to investigate scientifically the causes of the deterioration and to suggest appropriate remedies. A small laboratory was set up, and Scott was joined by Dr Harold Plenderleith MC in 1924. This laboratory gradually expanded until it became a Museum department in its own right.
