History

In the 1920s and 1930s, Scott and Plenderleith laid the foundations of the scientific conservation of antiquities in the UK, building on the work of Friedrich Rathgen in Berlin and Gustav Rosenberg in Copenhagen. This was the hey-day of British archaeological expeditions in Egypt and the Middle East, and Plenderleith and his colleagues became involved with the conservation and scientific investigation of objects from such famous excavations as those of Howard Carter in the tomb of Tutankhamen and Leonard Wooley at Ur.

The 1930s also saw the start of international cooperation in the field of the conservation of antiquities and monuments, and the first specialised journal, Technical Studies in the Field of the Fine Arts, began publication in 1932 at the Fogg Art Museum of Harvard University.
