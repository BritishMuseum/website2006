Important Dates

The 18th Century
1753

11 January - Death of Sir Hans Sloane (1660-1753), physician, naturalist and collector, whose collection formed the basis of the British Museum.

7 June - The British Museum Act receives the royal assent from King George II. A board of Trustees is appointed charged with the care of three collections (Sloane, Cotton, Harley). Funding for the acquisition of the Sloane collection (£20,000 to his heirs), the purchase of the Harleian collection of manuscripts (£10,000) and the acquisition of a repository is provided by public lottery. The Cottonian collection, largely of manuscripts, had been given to the nation in 1700.

11 December - First meeting of the Board of Trustees in the Cockpit, Whitehall.

1754

Parliamentary enquiry into the scandalous conduct of the British Museum lottery.

Negotiations for the purchase of a 17th century mansion, Montagu House, in Bloomsbury to house the new Museum (concluded 1755).

1756

Appointment of the first staff (a Principal Librarian, six curators, a porter, a messenger, two watchmen and four maids).

Arrival of the first Egyptian mummy and coffin, bequeathed by Colonel William Lethieullier.

1757

Gift by King George II of the 'Old Royal Library' of the sovereigns of England and with it the privilege of acquiring books by copyright receipt.

Museum gardens opened to the public (by ticket).

1759 15 January - British Museum opens to the public, who are admitted in small groups, by ticket obtained in advance, for a conducted tour.

1772
Purchase of Sir William Hamilton's (1730-1803) classical antiquities - the first antiquities of note acquired by the Museum.
1775
Gift from circumnavigator Captain James Cook (1728-79) of objects from the South Seas.
1778 South Sea room opens.
1799 Bequest of a collection of books, prints and drawings, coins and medals, gems and minerals by Clayton Mordaunt Cracherode (1730-99).
The 19th Century
1802 Arrival of the Rosetta Stone and other antiquities ceded by the French under the terms of the Treaty of Alexandria (1801).
1805

Purchase, from his family, of the collection of classical sculpture made by Charles Townley (1737-1805); Townley's coins acquired 1814.
1807

Establishment of the Department of Antiquities with Taylor Combe (1774-1826) as Keeper.

Introduction of the military guard.
1808 Opening of the Townley Gallery (Classical and Egyptian Antiquities) by Queen Charlotte (gallery begun 1804; architect George Saunders (c. 1762-1839)).
1810 Admission tickets abolished. Visitors now admitted in unlimited numbers and no longer escorted. Tickets abolished.
1814 Purchase of the 'Phigaleian marbles' (the sculptures from the Temple of Apollo at Bassae, Greece, received 1815).
1816

Parthenon Sculptures ('Elgin Marbles') purchased by Parliament from Lord Elgin and vested 'in perpetuity in the Trustees of the British Museum'.

Temporary building for the 'Elgin Marbles' designed by Robert Smirke (1780-1867) (Attached Architect of the Office of Works) (opened 1817, demolished 1831)

1818 Arrival of the head of Ramesses II ('The Younger Memnon') from Thebes excavated by Giovanni Belzoni (1778-1823), donated by Henry Salt and Jean-Louis Burckhardt.
1823

Gift to the nation from George IV of George III's library ('The King's Library').

Funds voted by Parliament for a repository for the library Robert Smirke's plans for a new neo-classical style Museum building presented to the Trustees and approved.

14 July - work begins on The King's Library, the east wing of Smirke's quadrangular building.

1827

Work begins on the northern section of Smirke's western gallery (now the Egyptian Sculpture Gallery).

Completion of the King's Library [1]; books installed 1828. New Reading Rooms (now the Middle and South Rooms) included to the south of the main rooms.
1830 3 February - Charles Dickens obtains his first ticket of admission to the Reading Room.
1832 Permanent Elgin Room [17] opened to the public.
1833 Work begins on North Wing.
1834 Egyptian sculpture installed in northern division of West Wing (the southern section being occupied by the Townley Gallery).
1836 Establishment of the Department of Prints & Drawings.
1837

First opening on a public holiday - Easter Monday.

First employment of police.

1838 Opening of two new Reading Rooms in the North Wing.
1841

Excavations commence for the South Front.

Completion of the Arched Room at the western extremity of the North Wing.

1842 Arrival of the first collection of Lycian (Xanthian) Marbles excavated in Greece by Charles Fellows 1842-44; second collection received 1844.

Demolition of Montagu House begins, completed 1845 (apart from the wings and Great Russell Street frontage.

Townley gallery partly demolished (remainder taken down in 1846).
1844-49 Construction of East and West Officers' Residences.
1845

Austen Henry Layard begins excavations in Assyria; employed by the Trustees of the British Museum from 1846.

Robert Smirke retires because of ill health; his formal resignation is received 1846. Replaced by his younger brother Sydney Smirke (1798-1877).

 Portland Vase smashed and subsequently restored by the Museum's restorer John Doubleday.

1847

Opening of the new Entrance Hall (decoration by Collman & Davis).

Arrival of the first Assyrian sculptures.

1850 Plans for the design of the Forecourt submitted and approved (completed 1852).

Karl Marx acquires his first ticket to research in the Reading Room (last renewal 1877).

Children admitted but 'babes in arms' still excluded.
1851 2,527,216 visitors welcomed in the year of the Great Exhibition. King's Library and other library areas opened to the public.

Appointment of Augustus Wollaston Franks (1826-97) as curator of the British collections, leading over the subsequent forty years to a vast expansion of non-Classical antiquities and ethnography.
1852

Erection on Great Russell Street of miniature railings decorated by cast iron lions designed by Alfred Stevens (1818-75).

Completion of pedimental sculptures above the front steps: 'The Rise of Civilization' by Sir Richard Westmacott (1775-1856).

Initial proposals for a round reading room in the central quadrangle put forward by the Keeper of Printed Books, Antonio Panizzi (1797-1879) and Sydney Smirke.

1853 Employment of the Museum's first photographer - Roger Fenton.
1854 Work begins on the round Reading Room (opened 1857).
1857-9 Acquisition of sculptures from the Mausoleum at Halikarnassos (Bodrum, Turkey), one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient world, excavated by Charles Newton 1856-9.
1859 Erection of lions' head fountains on the colonnade (designed by Sydney Smirke junior).
1861 Department of Antiquities divided on the retirement of the Keeper Edward Hawkins (1780-1867) into (1) Greek & Roman Antiquities (Keeper - Charles Newton); (2) Coins & Medals (Keeper - W S W Vaux); (3) Oriental Antiquities (Keeper - Samuel Birch).
1863

Charles Turtle Wood begins excavations on the site of the Temple of Artemis at Ephesos, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World (to 1874).

Police replace military guard.

1865 Bequest of the ethnographical and prehistoric collection formed by Henry Christy (1810-65).
1866 Department of British and Medieval Antiquities and Ethnography (Keeper - Augustus Wollaston Franks) detached from Oriental Antiquities.
1869 Island Statue (Hoa Hakananai'a) donated by Queen Victoria.
1873 Work begins on a building for the British Museum (Natural History) at South Kensington, architect Alfred Waterhouse.
1879

Funds bequeathed by William White (d.1823) with a life interest to his widow become available.

First experiments on the use of electricity in the Reading Room.

'Babes in arms' admitted. Museum open all day Saturdays.

1880-83 Removal of natural history collections to South Kensington (now the Natural History Museum). (Formally opened 1881).
1882 Work begins on the White Wing, fronting Montagu Street (architect Sir John Taylor (1833-1912)); occupied from July 1885.
1884 Mausoleum Room [77/21] completed with funds from the White Bequest.
1886 Oriental Antiquities' renamed Department of Egyptian and Assyrian Antiquities.
1890 Galleries fully lit by electricity.
1894 Purchase of properties from the Bedford Estate giving the Museum ownership of the freeholds of all surrounding properties; negotiations concluded 1895; Treasury loan paid off 1947.
1896

Miniature railings outside the main railing on Great Russell Street demolished to make way for pavement improvements, including the planting of plane trees.

Museum now open Sunday afternoons.

1898 Bequest by Baron Ferdinand Rothschild (1839-98) of Renaissance objets d'art from the 'New Smoking Room' at Waddesdon Manor, Buckinghamshire.
The 20th Century
1900 Acquisition of the Predynastic mummy from Egypt (known as 'Ginger').
1902 V I Lenin obtains his first reader's ticket.
1906 Work begins on King Edward VII's Galleries with the aid of a legacy from Vincent Stuckey Lean (d.1899); architect John James Burnet (1859-1938); foundation stone laid 1907 by King Edward VII.
1914 Opening of King Edward VII's Galleries by King George V and Queen Mary (occupied 1920s).
1918 Closure of the Museum and wartime evacuation of some items from the collections to the Postal Tube Railway at Holborn, the National Library of Wales (Aberystwyth) and a country house near Malvern.
1922 Excavations led by Sir Leonard Woolley and sponsored jointly by the British Museum and the University of Pennsylvania begin at Ur, southern Mesopotamia. (end 1934).
1927 Discovery at Ur of a unique Sumerian cemetery (the 'Royal Cemetery') dating from c. 2600 BC.
1931

Permanent establishment of the Research Laboratory (now the oldest such establishment in continuous existence). (Temporary conservation laboratory set up 1920).

Appointment of the first female curator.
1936 Work begins on the Duveen Gallery to house the Sculptures of the Parthenon; funds offered by Lord Duveen 1928; agreement 1931; completed 1938; installation of sculptures begins 1939; opened following war damage 1962 (architect John Russell Pope(1874-1937)).
1939

Donation by Mrs E M Pretty of the treasure from the Anglo-Saxon ship burial discovered at Sutton Hoo, Suffolk.

23 August - Evacuation of the collections ordered (to Boughton and Drayton Houses, Northamptonshire; National Library of Wales, Aberystwyth; Aldwych Tube Tunnel); evacuation begins 24 August.

1941 10/11 May - Incendiary raid destroys south-west library bookstacks and a number of upper exhibition galleries, students rooms and offices.
1942 Occupation of underground storage at Westwood Quarry near Bradford on Avon begins.
1943 Discussions begin on removal of library. Incorporated in 1945 Greater London Development Plan.
1946 Establishment of separate Departments of Ethnography and Oriental Antiquities (now Department of Asia).
1955 Department of Egyptian Antiquities divided into Egyptian Antiquities (now Ancient Egypt and Sudan) and Western Asiatic Antiquities (now Ancient Near East).
1957 Purchase (with a donation from Gilbert Edgar) of the horological collection formed by Courtney Ilbert.
1962 Appointment by the Ministry of Works of Sir Leslie Martin and Colin St John Wilson to design a new building to the south of the Museum to house the library and other facilities.
1963 New British Museum Act; separation of British Museum and British Museum (Natural History); Board of Trustees reduced to 25; loans made easier.
1964 Appointment of first professional in house designer.
1968 British Museum Society (now British Museum Friends) founded.
1969

Opening of remodelled ground floor Classical sculpture galleries (begun 1962).

Establishment of separate departments of Medieval and Later Antiquities (later Medieval and Modern Europe) and Prehistoric and Romano-British Antiquities (later Prehistory and Early Europe). Both now (1 April 2003) reunited as Prehistory and Europe.

1970

Opening of remodelled Assyrian galleries.

Transfer of Department of Ethnography to Museum of Mankind, Burlington Gardens (now closed).

1972

Act of Parliament establishing The British Library (which includes the Museum's Library Departments); formal separation 1 July 1973.
1974 Government announce alternative site for British Library building at St Pancras; accepted 1975; go-ahead confirmed 1978.
1975 Work begins on New Wing (architect Colin St John Wilson); occupied from 1978; formally opened 1980.
1981 Remodelled Egyptian Sculpture Gallery opened (designer Robin Wade).
1985 Opening of the Wolfson Galleries of Classical Sculpture & Inscriptions by TRH The Prince and Princess of Wales.
1990 Opening of the Japanese Galleries and remodelled Oriental and Prints & Drawings Galleries.
1991 Opening of the first of nine Near Eastern and Egyptian galleries funded by Raymond and Beverly Sackler.
1992 Opening of the Joseph E Hotung Gallery of Oriental Antiquities and the Asahi Shimbun Gallery of Amaravati Sculpture.
1994

Launch of British Museum 250th anniversary development programme.

Appointment of Norman Foster & Partners as architects for the Great Court.

Opening of the Mexican Gallery (architect Teodoro Gonzales de Leon), the first Ethnography gallery to return to Bloomsbury.

1997 Transfer of British Library books to new building at St Pancras begins.

Opening of the HSBC Money Gallery.

Opening of the Weston Gallery of Roman Britain (25 October).

Closure of the round Reading Room.
1998 Last British Library books leave Bloomsbury.

2 March - Work begins on the Great Court.

25 June - Opening of British Library building by HM The Queen (architect Sir Colin St John Wilson).
1999

Opening of the Roxie Walker Galleries of Egyptian Funerary Archaeology.

Opening of the Chase Manhattan Gallery of North America.

The 21st Century
2000 6 December - Opening of the Queen Elizabeth II Great Court by HM The Queen.

Opening of the Korea Foundation Gallery.
2003

British Museum 250th Anniversary.

Opening of the Wellcome Gallery.

Opening of the refurbished King's Library with the exhibition 'Discovery and Learning in the Age of George III'.
