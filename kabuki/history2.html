The emphasis now shifted from beauty to skill, from dance to drama, and the onnagata – men who specialised in playing women – developed a new kind of dramatic female.

The Japanese ‘renaissance’ (the Genroku era, 1688-1704) saw a great flowering of creativity as well as the growing influence on Kabuki of its main rival – the Bunraku puppet theatre. Actors adapted puppet plays for the stage and created stylized movements to mimic the puppets themselves.

Later, audiences transferred their tastes not only to ‘realism’ but to Edo and Osaka where a stronger pride and nature unfamiliar to Kyoto was defining contemporary Japan. Here, between 1780 and 1830, Kabuki flourished around cults of rivalry – most famously between the actors Shikan and Rikan.
