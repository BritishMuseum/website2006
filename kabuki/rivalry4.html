And this it was. Audiences and patrons loved Shikan. He played the fame game, on and offstage, linking Kabuki cults in two cities. He and his manager answered Rikan's complaints apologetically, professing their innocence and pleading that it was the public, not them, who were making a mockery of Kabuki. But behind the scenes, Shikan seems to have cunningly fanned the cult of rivalry, feeding the fans' creativity and securing his own notoriety. Fanzines, chants, parody fly-bills - they turned Kabuki into the greatest Japanese sport of the early nineteenth century. But this made it no less theatrical, no less significant in the expression of Japan’s complex hopes as a nation.
