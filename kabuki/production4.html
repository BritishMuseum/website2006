Make-up, or kesho, is one of the vital ingredients of kabuki. Whether for the refined look of the female impersonator (onnagata) or the grand role of Kabuki hero, making a face (kao o tsukuru) is considered vital psychological preparation for a performance.

The base colour for most styles of make up is the white oshiroi made from rice powder. Its finesse on the onnagata symbolised delicate skin, complemented by a small, refined red mouth, while on Kabuki heroes it provided contrast for their striking colours (kumadori ) in the poor light of the theatres.

These bold lines break the face down into its symbolic structure, emphasising various expressions. Gradually they were toned out with shadow along the muscles as Kabuki flirted with greater ‘realism’, but individual colours continued to carry a distinct symbolism. For example, deep red: anger, righteous indignation or cruelty. Pink: charm, cunning or foppery. Blue: a subtler form of villain, or a ghost. Brown: gods or corrupt nobles.
