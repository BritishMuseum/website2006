Many of the costumes in Kabuki reflected the contemporary styles of the day and in fact the theatre began to set the trend of dress for fashionable society. Costumes indicated the social standing of a character, as well as more hidden meanings, through the choice of colour, contour and textile – but designers could not use noble fabrics as this was considered an offence to the ‘serious’ glamour of the ruling class. Indeed, as the audience’s demand for spectacle grew, theatres were even forced to submit their designs to the government. The ban, for example, on printed satins, could be effectively mimicked by clever embroidery, but the tension occasionally led to actors being arrested on stage.

Some plays are based upon a series of costume changes, narrating a character’s development, fall or hidden identity. The great Osaka actor, Rikan I famously accused his rival, the younger Shikan I, of putting flamboyance before art by changing costume so many times. But the crowds snapped up Shikan memorabilia that celebrated the abundance of his style.
