Around 10,000 plays have been written since the inception of Kabuki, and although many have now disappeared, over 200 classics are still performed. With a dialogue full of puns, double meanings, epigrams and allusion, their content can be classified into two main categories: jidaimono and sewamono. They echo, to some degree, the division of Shakespeare’s plays into Histories (including tragedy) and Comedy. These do not necessarily mean funny and serious, rather high society and common society. Moreover, Histories were often a way of disguising contemporary events about important Japanese figures who it was illegal to directly represent on stage. David Farr’s play, The UN Inspector, at the National Theatre until 5 October, provides a striking contemporary parallel.

Generally Kabuki’s subject matter would be based around a conflict in which the characters of a play become entangled. This would always move to a logical conclusion that lay within the recognised code of ethics, which governed Edo period society. If a hero transgressed, he would pay with his life. Honour always had to be served.
