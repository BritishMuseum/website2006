Drama and performance at the British Museum

In as much as performance can remove people from the immediate pressure of any given ‘reality’, it has inspired revolutions, generated common hopes in troubled societies and allowed human beings to look in a kind of mirror, sometimes of shattered glass, to consider their place in the world. Performance is central to every culture and its heritage belongs to us all.

The British Museum bears remarkable witness to many theatrical traditions from across the globe. Besides Kabuki’s importance to the Japanese collections, the African Galleries tell the story of the Gelede (‘Mother Spirit’) masquerade; the Parthenon friezes recreate the annual state procession, one of the most colourful and important performances in ancient Greece; and for visitors with a keen eye for detail a number of everyday objects brought to life in Shakespeare’s plays can be spotted in the European collections (Rooms 42-46).
