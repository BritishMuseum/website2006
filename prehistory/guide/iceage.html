There is no evidence for clothing, jewellery or art before modern humans (Homo sapiens sapiens). More sophisticated lifestyles appear with the emergence of fully modern people in Africa about 100,000 years ago. As they spread around the world, so we find evidence of their artistic behaviour - in Europe, this is particularly marked after about 35,000 years ago by elaborate jewellery, carved materials like ivory, and paintings and engravings on cave walls. This art shows how modern humans can express their thoughts, feelings and abstract ideas in media other than the written word. Surviving images also express the persistent inter-relationship between humans and the natural world - man had a dependency upon, great knowledge of and respect for animals, hence they were a major source of inspiration for early artists(3).

First farming and the allure of the exotic

Humans have always been fascinated by unfamiliar materials and as farming communities became more settled and culture more complex, there was an increasing demand for exotic materials. Furthermore, many objects were not made for practical purposes. Wonderfully crafted objects probably reflected the spiritual or political power of their creators and owners, and may have been ascribed specific ritual roles. In the Neolithic period, beautiful, but fragile, jadeite stone axes from the Alpine region of Europe were prized over wide areas and acquired a symbolic value. Later, in the Bronze Age, gold from places like Ireland was used to make prestige pieces such as the Mold Cape found in Wales. With farming, the accumulation of wealth, firstly in the form of livestock and then metals, became an important facet of human activity, and presented further opportunities for people to exercise ever more power and control, over others
