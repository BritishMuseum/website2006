With assistance from the Townley Group, work has recently begun on an exciting project to study an important collection of Nasca pottery from the Pacific coast of southern Peru. Nasca culture (200 BC - AD 600) is best known for the famous so-called Nasca Lines - gigantic ground drawings in the desert landscape that are visible from the air. The new study aims to link these drawings to the rich corpus of colourful polychrome motifs painted on Nasca vessels.

Among the significant South American holdings that have never been exhibited in the British Museum is an extraordinary assemblage of more than three hundred Nasca vessels from the Pacific coast of southern Peru. They bear bold, brilliantly colourful polychrome designs which represent a veritable painted codex on pottery and have the potential to unlock a world hidden from our gaze for nearly two thousand years. The designs feature a range of powerful deities and mythological beings, whose face masks and elaborate ritual attire find their counterparts in stunning objects wrought in gold and skilfully woven cotton textiles as fine as anything produced elsewhere in the world at the time.

The striking contrasts of the desert landscape around the Nasca Valley impress all who venture there. The great snow-capped peaks of the Andean cordillera stand as a spectacular backdrop and provide life-giving water to the dry coastal valleys. Water-management was of vital importance to Nasca farmers, who mastered the formidable challenges posed by this demanding environment. Ancient graves have yielded a wealth of objects which reflect not only a successful economy, but also bear witness to a rich spiritual and intellectual life. Nearby, huge figurative ground drawings, or geoglyphs, are found etched on the desert floor, accompanied by elaborate patterns of ceremonial straight lines extending for many kilometres - the famous Nasca Lines. These incorporate clearly recognisable images of plants, animals, birds and fishes, which are all important elements in Nasca cosmology.

Archaeologists are now beginning to unravel some of the mysteries surrounding these drawings and the people that produced them. Nasca was a regional state, contemporaneous with the renowned Moche culture on the north coast of Peru. It formed the early climax of south-coast cultural traditions, setting the stage for the later Huari and Inca empires in the adjacent Andean highlands. The economy depended on a combination of agriculture, fishing and trade. The capital, Cahuachi, was a noted pilgrimage site and ceremonial centre, comprising extensive complexes of adobe pyramids. Here, seasonal agricultural rituals were enacted, and burial finds with trophy heads testify to the role played by a ‘trophy-head' cult in the annual round of fertility rites. Invocations to the ancestors sought their favourable intervention to assure the protection of crops and a successful harvest.

Many of the clues which form the basis for reconstructing the full range and complexity of the Nasca pantheon are to be found in the figures painted on the Nasca vessels. The Museum's Nasca collection is one of the largest held anywhere outside Peru and the first step towards the serious study of this material entails making precise roll-out drawings of whole figures which are wrapped around the entire circumference of many of the vessels. This task has long been hindered by the lack of a graphics officer in the Department of Ethnography. Fortunately, the British Museum Society's Townley Group has stepped in with a generous offer of support which will enable us to engage a graphic artist from outside the Museum.

The creation of the roll-out drawings is a complex procedure. First, detailed sets of photographs must be made of the imagery covering the whole surface of vessels which often assume quite unusual shapes. Careful tracings are then made from a composite ‘photo-mosaic' and the design is meticulously coloured to match the original. This enables the whole figure to be observed and studied while avoiding any distortion - an essential prerequisite for more detailed comparative analysis. Although this methodology has not been rigorously applied before to Nasca iconography, it is already apparent that the colour coding is likely to contain vital information that will contribute to our understanding of the hidden meanings embedded in the dense imagery.

The goal of the Nasca project is to compile a comprehensive inventory of Nasca iconography by category: deities, priests, animals, birds, plants, geometric motifs, etc. Each category will be progressively refined as the project develops. To begin with, we will focus on the most complex scenes and figures with multiple elements. Motifs drawn from nature together with more abstract symbols will help amplify the iconographic context. Since no complete study of the full range of variation of motifs and figures has ever before been undertaken, this study will reveal the entire canon of the iconography and tell us much about the grammar governing the structure of the different designs. The subtle variations and patterning of key elements point to underlying connections between various scenes and personages which are not at all obvious at first glance. Far from being random figures with arbitrary associations, there are clear hints of an underlying coherence to the way in which Nasca priests and artisans perceived and depicted their world. Ultimately, we expect that the full corpus of mythological figures and deities will offer many novel insights into the Nasca pantheon.

Future articles in the BM Magazine and updates on this website will report on progress, as we reconstruct the colourful cast of characters painted on the Nasca pots.
