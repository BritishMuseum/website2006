Over the past few years, Clara Bezanilla, under the supervision of Colin McEwan and with contributions from the distinguished Maya scholar Ian Graham and the foremost Maya epigrapher in the UK, Simon Martin, has been collating and documenting all the objects, cast, photographs and archival material in the Department of Ethnography's Maudslay Collection. The ambitious aim of this work is to enable the Museum to carry out a Maya Writing Project. The components that have been envisaged to make up such a project are: workshops, an online COMPASS Tour, publications, a conference, an exhibition and better management of and access to the collections. It remains to be seen how much of this will be possible but plans are underway to hold a Maya workshop and conference at the Museum, hopefully next year.

Who was Maudslay and why was he so significant?

Alfred P. Maudslay (1850-1931) has often been referred to as the father of scientific field archaeology in Mesoamerica. Inspired by the travel accounts of Stephens and Catherwood, he carried out eight expeditions to the Maya area (primarily to the sites of Copán, Quiriguá, Yaxchilán, Chichén Itza and Palenque) between 1881 and 1894. He took superb photographs of the monuments and their hieroglyphic inscriptions, many of them on 12x10" glass plates. He recorded three-dimensional monuments using hundreds of plaster moulds which were later re-assembled to produce the cast. The hieroglyphic text and images found on flat surfaces were reproduced with paper squeezes. The plaster and paper moulds made during his expeditions, at great cost, were sent to England. Maudslay donated his collection to the Victoria and Albert Museum on condition that casts would be made by his assistant Giuntini at the Museum's expense.

The magnificent work that resulted from these expeditions, including drawings by Anne Hunter, photographs, site plans and descriptions, were published between 1889 and 1902 in the 'Archeology' volumes of the Biologia Centrali-Americana. These publications formed the first significant corpus of Maya inscriptions and remain an invaluable tool to modern epigraphers and archaeologists.

The Maudslay Collection, now in the Department of Ethnography, consists of over 400 plaster casts, paper and plaster moulds, glass negatives and journals written during his expeditions. It also includes nine stone sculptures from Copán (Honduras) and eight lintels from Yaxchilán (now Mexico). The casts were made between 1886 and 1891 and were later transferred to the British Museum. In 1923 T.A Joyce, Deputy-Keeper of the then Department of Ceramics and Ethnography, exhibited the casts and some of the original sculptures.
