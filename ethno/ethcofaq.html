What has become of the Museum of Mankind?
It no longer exists.The Ethnography Department has completely vacated the building, which is now part of the Royal Academy of Arts. All Departmental activities are now centered at the British Museum and our outstations at Orsman Road and Blythe Road.

What has become of the Department of Ethnography?
We have reformed as the Department of Africa, Oceania and the Americas. Our department still concerns itself largely, but not exclusively, with indigenous and living societies and seeks to reflect the nature, preoccupations and history of various peoples worldwide through fieldwork, publication and the exhibition of objects crucial for the understanding of the societies in question.

As well as looking at present day agrarian or urban societies, with one eye firmly fixed on future developments, the Department of AOA, through its collections, reflects the historical development of western perception and understanding of societies encountered since the 18th century. In the case of the Americas and parts of Africa the discipline of archaeology is also employed to study those ancient cultures which do not fall within the compass of the other antiquities departments at The British Museum.

What material is on permanent display?
There are three permanent galleries at the British Museum that exhibit ethnographic material : the Sainsbury African Galleries, the Chase Manhattan North American Gallery, and the Ancient Mexico Gallery. The African galleries are open daily but the Mexican and North American galleries are subject to closure.

Is it possible to see anything which is not on display?
We are currently undergoing a controlled programme of unpacking of material in the reserve collection. Therefore, not everthing is accessible. For advice contact the Collections Enquiries Service at the Centre for Anthropology ph 020 7323 8041.

When will the crystal skull be going on display?
The crystal skull is now on display in the 'Living & Dying' exhibition in the British Museum's Wellcome Trust Gallery. The exhibition is to the north side of the Great Court, ground floor.

The skull was on loan at the Henry Moore Institute in Leeds (12 December 2001 - 28 February 2002). Prior to that, it was on display in the Human Image exhibition, December 2000 - February 2001, and is in included in the catalogue of that exhibition. Documentation relating to the skull is available by contacting Jim Hamill, Collections Enquiries Service at the Centre for Anthropology
jhamill@thebritishmuseum.ac.uk,

If I am unable to see objects in your collections where else can I try?
There are several museums in the UK with collections similar to ours. In London, the Horniman Museum in Forest Hill has an excellent collection including a new African Gallery and the 'Centenary Gallery', which opened in June 2002, displaying collections from around the world. Outside London, the Pitt Rivers Museum in Oxford has a great deal of material on permanent display and the Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology in Cambridge is another good collection. Other good collections can be found at

Leeds City Museum (currently closed)
Manchester Museum
Liverpool Museum
Ipswich Museum
Brighton Museum and Art Gallery
The Royal Albert Memorial Museum & Art Gallery, Exeter
Kelvingrove Art Gallery and Museum, Glasgow
National Museums of Scotland, Edinburgh
Marischal Museum, Aberdeen

Our Study Room is happy to advise you about these and other museums with ethnographic collections.
