Department of Africa, Oceania and the Americas > research > fieldwork > Tunisian Textiles and Personal Adornment

Ongoing fieldwork-based research by Julie Hudson and Christopher Spring into Tunisian textile traditions and associated personal adornment. Previous trips have resulted in the collection, display and publication of significant textiles from both urban and rural traditions. Silk weaving flourishes in towns such as Mahdia on the east coast where ceremonial garments are produced, by professional male artisans using treadle looms, for the local community. Further south, in more isolated rural regions, wool is the primary raw material. It is prepared and used by women to weave shawls, cloaks and head-veils for personal use or for sale in local markets. Many of these items are further elaborated with lively and colourful embroidery.

Future research trips will seek to explore the textile traditions to the mountainous west of Tunisia and the string of small towns and villages along the northern coast. During the 15th century, the influx of refugees (many of whom were skilled artisans) from Andalusia to north and west Tunisia, greatly stimulated textile production. Technical innovations and the introduction of Hispano-Moresque design elements made a significant and lasting contribution to the expansion and development of the industry in this region.

A video based on research in Mahdia and on Jerba island, together with a selection of textiles and contemporary art collected in the field, may be seen in the Sainsbury African Galleries.
