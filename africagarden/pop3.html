Sweet Thorn – Acacia karroo

From the Western Cape through to Zambia and Angola, Acacia is integral to the history of southern Africa having been used for everything from raft-making and sewing needles to eye treatments and fencing for the houses of royal Zulu women. To both shepherding and settled-farming communities, it is a welcome indicator of the sweet veld (grasslands) prized for good grazing and fertile soils. Acacia takes its name – Sweet Thorn – from the gum exuded from wounds in its bark which has been exported as ‘Cape Gum’ for making sweets. Hundreds of African objects in the Museum, from pots to Mummies, are glued or bound with Acacia gum – for example, Chant Avedissian’s stencilled wall-hanging on the stairs leading down to the African Galleries.
