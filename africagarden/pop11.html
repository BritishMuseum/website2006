Woad, or Indigo – Isatis tinctoria

While it is unlikely there will ever be enough of it to colour all the pairs of jeans manufactured in a single year, indigo remains the only known, natural source of blue dye in the world. From the Tuareg nomads of the Sahara ('Blue Men of the South') to the grassland kingdoms of Cameroon, the intricate mysteries of indigo have passed between generations of specialist dyers for centuries. It once signified wealth, abundance and fertility and, although it may not subsequently have reflected such prosperity, West Africa was, until just a century ago, a seas of blue and white striped clothing. There are important examples of indigo-dyed clothing in case 8 of the Sainsbury African Galleries (Room 25). Besides clothing, other species of indigo in Africa are held to have medicinal uses such as alleviating menstrual cramps or influencing the sex of children. Another species' twigs are used as toothbrushes and its sap as mouthwash.
