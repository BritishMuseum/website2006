Painted sycamore fig wooden coffin of Denytenamun, Incense-bearer of
the temple of Amun, early 22nd dynasty, from Thebes.

The Egyptians believed that for a soul to have an afterlife, its body
must survive in a recognisable form. At first bodies wereburied in the sand
which preserved them naturally, but as more elaborate methods of burial
were used there was a need to prevent decay. There are few records of
how mummification was done; what is known is based mainly on accounts
by Classical authors, and studies of the mummies themselves. The oldest
known mummy dates from about 3500 BC.

The coffin was the most important item of burial equipment. Besides protecting the corpse, it provided magical assistance for the soul of the deceased and promoted the continuation of life. From the New Kingdom, the most popular form was the mummy-shaped type. This could serve as a reserve body should the mummy perish, or as an image of the dead person, with god-like qualities. It was also regarded as a cocoon or ‘egg’, in which the deceased lay ready to be reborn into the afterlife.
