This bronze sculpture of the benign and graceful goddess Parvati
depicts her as a beautiful young woman. This ideal depiction of
womanhood makes her an appropriate image for the consort of the
important male deity, Shiva. Bronze sculptures of this type are known
in India as utsavamurti, ‘festival images’, as their function is to be taken
out of the temple at festival-time and paraded through the streets. This
was an opportunity for all members of the public to have that beneficial
‘sacred view’ (darshan) of the deity.

In Indian thought the main female goddess is usually connected to ideas concerned
with fertility and abundance. Such goddesses, however, have many forms, some of them powerful. Parvati, in contrast, is calming and is often shown attending to her husband Shiva. In both paintings and sculpture, the two are frequently depicted in their Himalayan home of Mount Kailash with their two offspring – elephant-headed Ganesha and the warlike youth Karttikeya.

This sculpture was made using the lost-wax process, a method of bronzecasting at which Indian craftsmen are supremely competent.
