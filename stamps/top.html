Alexander the Great (356-323 BC)
was the son of Philip II of Macedon
and Olympias. Philip united his
native Macedonia before conquering
the rest of mainland Greece. Alexander
was educated by eminent Greek teachers
including Aristotle. After Philip’s
assassination in 336 BC Alexander began
expanding his territory, extending the boundary of the
Greek world to Egypt in the south and India in the east.
He died in Babylon.

Alexander is said to have carefully controlled the numerous representations of his portrait set up throughout his empire, allowing only a few sculptors and painters to produce his image. His posthumous portraits such as this follow similar general characteristics, although they vary in style. They show a more youthful, though perhaps more god-like character. He is clean-shaven, has longer hair, a more dynamic tilt of the head and an upward gaze. For a ruler to be shown without a beard was an innovation. Alexander was deified in his own lifetime, and after his death he was worshipped as a god in images such
as this.
