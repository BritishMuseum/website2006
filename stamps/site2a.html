Alexander the Great (356-323 BC)
was the son of Philip II of Macedon
and Olympias. Philip united his
native Macedonia before conquering
the rest of mainland Greece. Alexander
was educated by eminent Greek teachers
including Aristotle. After Philip’s
assassination in 336 BC Alexander began
expanding his territory, extending the boundary
 of the Greek world to Egypt in the south and
India in the east. He died in Babylon.

Alexander is said to have carefully controlled the numerous representations of his portrait set up throughout his empire, allowing
only a few sculptors and painters to produce his image. His
posthumous portraits such as this follow similar general characteristics, although they vary in style. They show a more youthful, though perhaps more god-like character. He is clean-shaven, has longer hair, a more dynamic tilt of the head and an upward gaze. For a ruler to be shown without a beard was an innovation. Alexander was deified in his own lifetime, and after his death he was worshipped as a god in images
such as this.

One of the most outstanding archaeologica
l finds in the British Museum was found
beneath a low grassy mound at Sutton Hoo
in Suffolk. Excavated in 1939 at the request
of the landowner, Mrs Edith
Pretty, the mound covered a deeply buried oak
ship, thirty metres long. At its centre was an
undisturbed burial chamber in which lay an
extraordinary collection of objects including this
unique helmet with its dramatic facemask. It was the
grave of an early Anglo-Saxon king who had been
ceremonially buried with his most treasured possessions:
a magnificent gold buckle, gold sword and belt fittings, purse-lid and clasps all inlaid with intricately cut garnets, a lyre, drinking horns and a sceptre, silver from Byzantium and Celtic hanging-bowls.

No body was found in the grave but later excavations established that the grave contained a body which survived only as chemical traces. The burial is dated by coins in the purse to around AD 625, suggesting that it could be the grave of Redwald, king of the East Angles (died before AD 627/8), and overlord of the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms from AD 616.
After a Coroner’s Inquest to decide ownership, Mrs Pretty generously gave the entire assemblage to the nation.

  2003 is the 250th anniversary of the death of the great collector, Sir Hans Sloane (1660-1753), whose collections formed the nucleus of the British Museum, and also the anniversary of the Act of Parliament by which the Museum was founded. To mark the occasion a set of six stamps, the first ever to feature the British Museum, has been produced by the Royal Mail.

To demonstrate the cultural diversity of the Museum’s collections a face has been selected from each of the five continents. In addition to these, the helmet from the Anglo-Saxon ship burial at Sutton Hoo, Suffolk, one of the Museum’s greatest treasures
from Britain is featured .

The Museum’s collections span the whole world from the first emergence of human beings to the present day. By following this trail you can travel from Ancient Egypt to pre-Conquest Mexico, from India to Easter Island and from the Greek world to Anglo- Saxon England. The faces are divine and human, made of contrasting materials: stone, wood, metal and turquoise by ancient craftsmen. They are monumental and life sized, abstract and realistic. Although their origins are diverse, their compelling stares link them across time and place.
