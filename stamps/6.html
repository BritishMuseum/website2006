Easter Island (Rapa Nui) is famous for its stone statues of human figures,
known as moai, probably carved to commemorate important ancestors.
The statues were made from around AD 1000 until the second half of the
seventeenth century. A birdman cult later became more important to the
islanders. This statue is unusual, as it is made of basalt rather than the more
usual volcanic tuff from the Rano Raraku quarry. It was probably first displayed
outside, on a stone platform, before being moved into a stone house at the
ritual centre of the birdman cult at Orongo.

In October 1868 HMS Topaze, on a surveying voyage, visited Easter Island. A party from the ship found the statue in one of the stone slab houses at Orongo, partly buried in the earth. The four-ton statue was subsequently moved to the ship with the assistance of the islanders. The Admirality presented the statue to Queen Victoria, who subsequently gave it to the British Museum

The crew recorded the name of the statue as Hoa Hakananai’a. At one time this was translated as ‘Breaking Waves’, but it is now thought to mean ‘Stolen’ or ‘Hidden Friend’.
