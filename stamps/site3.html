The Egyptians believed that for
a soul to have an afterlife, its body
must survive in a recognisable form. At first bodies wereburied in the sand which preserved them naturally, but as more elaborate methods of burial were used there was a need to prevent decay. There are few records of how mummification was done; what is known is based mainly on accounts by Classical authors, and studies of the mummies themselves. The oldest known mummy dates from about 3500 BC.

The coffin was the most important item of burial equipment. Besides protecting the corpse, it provided magical assistance for the soul of the deceased and promoted the continuation of life. From the New Kingdom, the most popular form was the mummy-shaped type. This could serve as a reserve body should the mummy perish, or as an image of the dead person, with god-like qualities. It was also regarded as a cocoon or ‘egg’, in which the deceased lay ready to be reborn into the afterlife.
 
 


 







One of the most outstanding
archaeological finds in the British
Museum was found beneath a low
grassy mound at Sutton Hoo in Suffolk.
Excavated in 1939 at the request of the
landowner, Mrs Edith Pretty, the mound covered a deeply buried oak ship, thirty metres long. At its centre was an undisturbed burial chamber in which lay an extraordinary collection of objects including this unique helmet with its dramatic facemask. It was the grave of an early Anglo-
Saxon king who had been ceremonially buried with his
most treasured possessions: a magnificent gold buckle,
gold sword and belt fittings, purse-lid and clasps all inlaid with intricately cut garnets, a lyre, drinking horns and a sceptre, silver from Byzantium and Celtic hanging-bowls.

No body was found in the grave but later excavations established that the grave contained a body which survived only as chemical traces. The burial is dated by coins in the purse to around AD 625, suggesting that it could be the grave of Redwald, king of the East Angles (died before AD 627/8), and overlord of the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms from AD 616.

After a Coroner’s Inquest to decide ownership, Mrs Pretty generously gave the entire assemblage to the nation.


 

 





Alexander the Great (356-323 BC)
was the son of Philip II of Macedon
and Olympias. Philip united his native
Macedonia before conquering the rest
of mainland Greece. Alexander was
educated by eminent Greek teachers
including Aristotle. After Philip’s
assassination in 336 BC Alexander
began expanding his territory, extending
the boundary of the Greek world to Egypt in
the south and India in the east. He died in Babylon.

Alexander is said to have carefully controlled the numerous
representations of his portrait set up throughout his empire,
allowing only a few sculptors and painters to produce his
image. His posthumous portraits such as this follow similar general characteristics, although they vary in style. They show a more youthful, though perhaps more god-like character. He is clean-shaven, has longer hair, a more dynamic tilt of the head and an upward gaze. For a ruler to be shown without a beard was an innovation. Alexander was deified in his own lifetime, and after his death he was worshipped as a god in images such as this.


 









Across both cheeks of this mask, a
stylized butterfly is picked out in
mosaic of a darker tone. The butterfly
is an emblem of Xiuhtecuhtli, a Fire
god from central Mexico, whose name
also means Turquoise Lord.

Turquoise mosaics were probably made by
Mixtec craftsmen for the Aztecs prior to the Spanish Conquest.Turquoise was highly-valued for its range of bluish-green hues that came to symbolise fertility and the sources of life. It was obtained by Aztec traders from mines in what is now the southwest United States, far beyond the limits of the Aztec empire.

We do not know exactly when these mosaics were made. Some are known to have been included in the treasure given by Motecuhzoma to the Spanish conqueror Hernan Cortez. The Museum’s group of nine was acquired in the nineteenth century in the territory of the old Habsburg Empire (Austria and Bohemia), which means they could be rare survivals of the treasures sent from Mexico at the beginning of the sixteenth century.
 
 


 





This bronze sculpture of the
benign and graceful goddess
Parvati depicts her as a beautiful
young woman. This ideal depiction
of womanhood makes her an
appropriate image for the consort of the
important male deity, Shiva. Bronze
sculptures of this type are known in India as
utsavamurti, ‘festival images’, as their function is to be taken out of the temple at festival-time and paraded through the streets. This was an opportunity for all members of the public to have that beneficial ‘sacred view’ (darshan) of the deity.

In Indian thought the main female goddess is usually connected to ideas concerned with fertility and abundance. Such goddesses, however, have many forms, some of them powerful. Parvati, in contrast, is calming and is often shown attending to her husband Shiva. In both paintings and
sculpture, the two are frequently depicted in their Himalayan home of Mount Kailash with their two offspring – elephant-headed Ganesha and the warlike youth Karttikeya.

This sculpture was made using the lost-wax process, a method of bronzecasting at which Indian craftsmen are supremely competent.


 











Easter Island (Rapa Nui) is famous
for its stone statues of human figures,
known as moai, probably carved to
commemorate important ancestors.
The statues were made from around
AD 1000 until the second half of the
seventeenth century. A birdman cult later
became more important to the islanders. This
statue is unusual, as it is made of basalt rather than the more usual volcanic tuff from the Rano Raraku quarry. It was probably first displayed outside, on a stone platform, before being moved into a stone house at the ritual centre of the birdman cult at Orongo.

In October 1868 HMS Topaze, on a surveying voyage, visited
Easter Island. A party from the ship found the statue in one of the stone slab houses at Orongo, partly buried in the earth. The four-ton statue was subsequently moved to the ship with the assistance of the islanders. The Admirality presented the statue to Queen Victoria, who subsequently gave it to the British Museum

The crew recorded the name of the statue as Hoa Hakananai’a. At one time this was translated as ‘Breaking Waves’, but it is now thought to mean ‘Stolen’ or ‘Hidden Friend’.
 
   
 
  2003 is the 250th anniversary of the death of the great collector, Sir Hans Sloane (1660-1753), whose collections formed the nucleus of the British Museum, and also the anniversary of the Act of Parliament by which the Museum was founded. To mark the occasion a set of six stamps, the first ever to feature the British Museum, has been produced by the Royal Mail.

To demonstrate the cultural diversity of the Museum’s collections a face has been selected from each of the five continents. In addition to these, the helmet from the Anglo-Saxon ship burial at Sutton Hoo, Suffolk, one of the Museum’s greatest treasures from Britain is featured .

The Museum’s collections span the whole world from the first emergence of human beings to the present day. By following this trail you can travel from Ancient Egypt to pre-Conquest Mexico, from India to Easter Island and from the Greek world to Anglo- Saxon England. The faces are divine and human, made of contrasting materials: stone, wood, metal and turquoise by ancient craftsmen. They are monumental and life sized, abstract and realistic. Although their origins are diverse, their compelling stares link them across time and place.
