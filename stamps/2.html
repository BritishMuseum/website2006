One of the most outstanding archaeological finds in the British Museum was
found beneath a low grassy mound at Sutton Hoo in Suffolk. Excavated in
1939 at the request of the landowner, Mrs Edith Pretty, the mound covered
a deeply buried oak ship, thirty metres long. At its centre was an undisturbed
burial chamber in which lay an extraordinary collection of objects including this
unique helmet with its dramatic facemask. It was the grave of an early Anglo-
Saxon king who had been ceremonially buried with his most treasured possessions: a magnificent gold buckle, gold sword and belt fittings, purse-lid and clasps all inlaid with intricately cut garnets, a lyre, drinking horns and a sceptre, silver from Byzantium and Celtic hanging-bowls.

No body was found in the grave but later excavations established that the grave contained a body which survived only as chemical traces. The burial is dated by coins in the purse to around AD 625, suggesting that it could be the grave of Redwald, king of the East Angles (died before AD 627/8), and overlord of the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms from AD 616.

After a Coroner’s Inquest to decide ownership, Mrs Pretty generously gave the entire assemblage to the nation.
