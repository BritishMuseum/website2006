Vikings buried this mass of over 8,500 silver coins and objects weighing over 40 kgs. Most of the coins in the hoard came from Britain, but some came from France and a small number from different parts of the Islamic world. Many of the objects are just parts of broken pieces of larger silver objects like brooches. The hoard might have been the war chest to pay a Viking army. It is the largest Viking treasure ever found outside of Russia.

Preston, Lancashire AD 905-910
Room 41


In 1966 the largest hoard of gold coins ever found in Britain was discovered on a building site. As well as 1,237 coins, the hoard contained gold rings and jewellery. The latest coin in the hoard dates to 1464 which means these gold objects were buried in 1464 or some time afterwards. This was the period of English history called the Wars of the Roses, a bitter civil war to control the country. Was the gold buried for safe keeping in those unsettled times? Why did the person who buried the gold never collect it again?

Nottinghamshire About AD 1464
Room 68

This treasure really was found buried in a chest. The discovery was reported before it was removed from the ground allowing archaeologists to carefully excavate the decayed remains of the chest. It was found to contain over 15,000 gold and silver coins, nearly 100 spoons, gold bracelets and other objects including silver pepper pots. The chest was probably buried for safe keeping during the trouble times when Roman rule was breaking down in Britain, but the owners never came back to recover it. This hoard was buried only a few miles from where the Mildenhall hoard was found.

Suffolk AD 410-430
Room 49


These famous chess pieces were found in mysterious circumstances some time before 1831. They were probably made in Norway and are carved from walrus ivory or whales' teeth. Who owned the chess pieces and why they were hidden are even greater mysteries. These small kings, queens, knights and bishops have fascinated people ever since their discovery. Modern chess sets have been modelled on the figures and even Harry Potter played with replica Lewis chessmen in his first film!

Uig, Isle of Lewis AD 1150-1200
Room 42

This hoard is part of a large silver dinner service. It contains bowls, plates and spoons that some of the richest people in the Roman Empire would have used at dinner or just for show. It includes the Great Dish, a large platter made from 8.2 kgs of silver and measuring 60 cms across. The platter is decorated with scenes from Roman pagan mythology, but some of the spoons are marked with Christian symbols. The hoard was found in a ploughed field in 1942. The farmer kept it on his mantel shelf before it came to the British Museum in 1946.

Mildenhall Hoard Suffolk AD 350-400
Room 49

The cape is made from a thin sheet of decorated gold originally beaten and hammered from a single gold ingot. This elaborate garment was made only a few centuries after people in Britain started to make objects from metal. The cape was probably only worn on special occasions, but it certainly was worn because you can see where it was torn and repaired on one side. The cape is one of the finest examples of prehistoric gold working from Europe. No other object like it has ever been found.

Flintshire 1900-1600 BC
Room 36

This gold cup was found in a burial mound on Bodmin Moor in 1837. Gold was a rare and valuable metal in prehistoric times. To be buried with a gold cup probably showed how important the dead person was. Like the Mold Cape, the cup was made from a single lump of gold. Great skill was needed to make the corrugated shape. Only two other gold cups like this one had ever been found in Europe until 2002 when a new larger cup was found at Ringlemere, Kent.

Cornwall 1700-1500 BC
Room 36


Torcs were neck rings worn by important people in the years before the Roman Conquest. They were usually made from gold or silver. The largest group of gold torcs ever found in Britain came from Snettisham in Norfolk. The first torcs were found here in 1948 and 1950. People thought that there were no more to be found, but in 1990 more torcs were discovered carefully buried in the ground. Was this an ancient treasury or bank where valuable objects were kept for safety, or were these torcs buried as gifts to the Gods?

Norfolk, England About 100 BC
Room 50


This Anglo Saxon burial is probably the most famous archaeological discovery ever made in Britain. In 1939, a huge mound was excavated revealing the decayed remains of a wooden ship. In the ship there was a burial chamber the size of a small room. It contained a sword, a shield, a helmet, jewellery and a purse. Other objects found included drinking horns, silver bowls made in the east Mediterranean and a decorated bowl from Wales or Ireland. No other burial has ever been found in Britain that is as rich as this one. Mystery surrounds who was buried here, or even if the mound contained a burial at all, as no body was found.

Woodford, Suffolk AD 620-30
Room 41


Thin slivers of wet wood are not what many people would think of as treasure. However, these tablets are letters and documents written almost 2000 years ago by soldiers and civilians living at the Roman fortress Vindolanda, near Hadrian's Wall. It is rare to find examples of writing on wood or paper from Britain's ancient past, but these tablets from Vindolanda have survived and they provide a unique window on life in Roman Britain. Because of this, curators at the British Museum think that they are the number one top ten British Treasure in the Museum.

Chesterholm, Northumbria AD 90-140
