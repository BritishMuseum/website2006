What exactly is Treasure?
Treasure is any item found made of more than 10% gold or silver which is over 300 years old, and in the case of coins, two or more such pieces. It also includes 10 or more bronze coins more than 300 years old found in the same locality. Objects found in association with Treasure also count as Treasure, for instance a pottery container with a hoard of Roman coins.

Examples of Treasure: a gold medieval finger ring. A hoard of 120 Roman silver and bronze coins with fragments of a pottery container. Single coins found on their own are not treasure and groups of coins lost one by one over a period of time (for example, those found on settlement or fair sites) will not normally be treasure. The best advice is: if in doubt, report your find.

A review of the Treasure Act has just been completed, this can also be found on-line at http://www.culture.gov.uk/global/publications/archive_2002/treasure_code.htm

What should I do if I find Treasure or think I might have found Treasure?
All Treasure finds must be reported to your local coroner within 14 days. Your local police station or museum will be able to tell you who to contact if you are unsure. Details are also given in the Treasure Act handbook which has a full list of all relevant coroner's in England and Wales. This can be obtained free of charge from Department of Culture, Media & Sport (Tel.: 0207 211 6200).
Even if your discovery is not Treasure in the legal sense, it may be a considerable historical importance because of what it can tell us about the past. In England and Wales the Portable Antiquities Scheme has staff across both countries who record discoveries of any ancient artefacts. In Scotland, different laws apply to discoveries of ancient objects. If you find any ancient artefact please take it to your local museum, your local council archaeologist or contact the Treasure Trove Advisory Panel Secretariat at the National Museums of Scotland,Chambers Street, Edinburgh. For more information about the laws surrounding Treasure and discoveries of ancient objects see http://www.treasuretrove.org.uk/

What happens after I have reported my find?
After you have reported your find to your coroner he or she will instruct you as to which museum or archaeological body you should take the find. If the museum decides that the find is treasure they will inform the British Museum or the National Museum of Wales. If they or a local museum wish the acquire the find, the coroner will hold an inquest to decide whether it is treasure. If declared treasure, the Treasure Valuation Committee will value the find and you would be awarded the full market value for the find. If at any stage the find is not considered Treasure it will be disclaimed and returned to you. If you require information about a specific treasure case being processed by the British Museum, please contact Lisa Voden-Decker, the Treasure Registrar, on 020 7323 8611 or mail to: lvodendecker@thebritishmuseum.ac.uk.

What advice do you have for metal detector users?
Always obtain permission from the landowner before using a metal detector on his or her land. Detecting on scheduled ancient monuments is strictly forbidden unless permission has been obtained from English Heritage or in Wales, Cadw. Also be aware that common land owned by your local councils may have bye-laws relating to metal detecting. Metal detectorists are also advised to join a recognised metal detecting club and a national representative body. For more details please contact either the National Council for Metal Detecting (http://www.ncmd.co.uk) or the Federation of Independent Detectorists (http://www.newbury.net/fid/).

What about underwater finds?
Under the Merchant Shipping Act (1995) all underwater finds of any date must be reported to the Receiver of Wreck whose job is to decide ownership. In all cases of wreck reported, you may receive a salvage reward. For more information contact the Receiver of Wreck at: Maritime and Coastguard Agency, Spring Place, 105 Commercial Road, Southampton SO15 1EG. Tel.: 01703 329474. Fax: 01703 329477.

Where can I record my finds and discover more about them?
All archaeological finds, regardless of whether they are Treasure or not, are important for our nation's heritage. Finders should therefore be aware that all non-treasure finds (e.g. medieval bronze buckles, single coin finds, Prehistoric flints) can be recorded voluntarily under the Portable Antiquities Scheme.

A leaflet about the scheme with a full list of contacts can obtained from the Resource Outreach Officer, c/o Dept. of Coins & Medals, British Museum, London WC1B 3DG. Tel.: 0207 323 8611. Alternatively visit the Portable Antiquities website at: http://www.finds.org.uk.
