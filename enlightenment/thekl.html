The King’s Library


Click to view larger image
The King’s Library was the original name applied both to the royal collection of over 60,000 books and to the room in the British Museum that housed them. The library had been formed by King George III (1760-1820) and was given to the nation in 1823 by his son King George IV. But there was insufficient room for it in Montagu House, the building which had housed the collection of the British Museum after its foundation in 1753. So a new building was begun on the same site, by the architect Sir Robert Smirke (1781-1867). The magnificent room for the King’s Library was constructed between 1823 and 1827, and was the first part of the new British Museum building as it is seen today. It was on a grand scale: 300 feet long, 41 feet high and 30 feet wide, with a central section 58 feet wide. Its great size called for the pioneering use of cast iron beams to support the ceiling.

In 1998 the books were transferred to their new home in the King’s Library in the new British Library building at St Pancras, London, designed by architect Sir Colin St John Wilson. Between 2000 and 2003 the original room at the British Museum was carefully restored to the previous glory of the 1820s. Hundreds of square metres of plaster were cleaned and repaired; the ceiling glows once again with its original stone paint and golden yellow roundels; a sun burst of gilding announces the very centre. 200 kilometres of wiring (twice round the M25) enabled a subtle lighting scheme to be installed. The balcony, previously thought to be of burnished brass, glitters once more with new gilding. Weeks of painstaking repair and cleaning revealed the warmth of the oak and mahogany floor, reinforcing the sense of space and order. Panels of sienna yellow scagliola, columns of Aberdeen granite, capitals of Derbyshire alabaster, pilasters and architraves of white marble once again offer their engaging polychromy: two centuries of use and London grime have been washed away.
