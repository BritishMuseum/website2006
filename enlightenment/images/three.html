More Information & Events

An gallery guide leaflet (Available at the entrance to the gallery)

A handlist of the objects on display (available for reference from the Information/Handling Collection desk in the gallery.

A catalogue of the books from the House of Commons Library which are incorporated into the exhibition (available on the computer terminals in the Information Centre in the Round Reading Room of the British Museum).

A beautifully illustrated book entitled Enlightenment. Discovering the World in the Eighteenth Century, edited by Kim Sloan (British Museum Press, 2003) (for sale in the Museum Bookshop, from the British Museum Press website or from booksellers).

Two free trails and a study pack are available from the Information Desks in the Great Court and Reading Room or by post from the Department of Education and Information.

Eighteenth century to eBay is a family trail round the Enlightenment Gallery. It is about why people collect things, how they can arrange them and what these things can tell us about the world. Suitable for families with children 7+.

Carry on collecting is a cross-Museum trail, starting in the Enlightenment Gallery, which introduces you to famous collectors from the past and from other cultures, and compares different ways of looking at objects: in the18th century they contributed to universal knowledge, whilst today we are more interested in objects in their cultural context.

Accessing Enlightenment is a study guide for students at A level and above, studying museology, consumer studies or 18th century cultural history. It links the experience of being a museum visitor now and in the 18th century: what sort of person visited museums, what was their educational background, how easy was it for them to get in and what did they learn while they were here? The study guide costs £8.99 and is a new type of learning resource produced by the Department for Education and Information; comments from users are warmly welcomed.

A range of books about the Enlightenment, the architecture of the British Museum, the history of the British Museum and the cultures that produced the objects on display in the exhibition are available in the Information Centre in the Round Reading Room of the British Museum.

Science: The Story of a Revolution
Thursday 24 November 2005 , 18.30
BP Lecture Theatre
£5, concessions £3

In the 17th century, revolutions in science challenged a world of superstition and inspired the foundation of the British Museum. John Gribbin and Lisa Jardine discuss the events and characters whose inventions and experiments changed history. Professor Lisa Jardine combines a career as a scholar and historian with a high media profile and has written widely, particularly on the history of the scientific revolution. John Gribbon is the author of a new book about the Royal Society, The Fellowship: the story of a revolution.

Museum Studies - student day
Thursday 26 January 2006 11.00-15.00

A day of talks by British Museum staff giving students a behind-the-scenes insight into the running and organization of an internationally celebrated museum. Education, Marketing, Curatorial and Visitor Services staff discuss museum theory and practice. Students wishing to broaden their knowledge of museums and the culture and heritage sector are welcome. Admission free, booking required (through Learning and Information Dept.)

Women and the Enlightenment: an occasional series of evening lectures
BP Lecture Theatre (£5, concessions £3; Tickets available through BM Box Office)

'Pandora's Breeches: women, science & power in the Enlightenment'
by Patricia Fara, Cambridge
Thursday 16 February 2006 18.30

The acclaimed author of Pandora's Breeches will challenge conventional history by showing how women have been unjustly concealed behind the reputations of Enlightenment scientific heroes such as Isaac Newton. Many of the world's greatest men of science relied on the help of female friends and relatives.

What did the Enlightenment do for women?' by Karen O'Brien, Warwick
Thursday 23 February 2006 18.30

The Enlightenment was an era of immense social transformation. Enlightenment thinkers from John Locke to Voltaire voiced their criticism of the traditional order, and developed radical new ideas about human rights, religious toleration, progress and the education of children. At the same time, they placed more emphasis upon the biological differences between men and women. This lecture will explore the paradoxes and possibilities of Enlightenment for women both in the 18th century and in our present day where its legacy can still be felt.
